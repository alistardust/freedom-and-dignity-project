## What this project is

The Freedom & Dignity Project is a U.S. reform movement — not a think tank, not a party. We are building a substantive progressive policy platform organized around 25 policy pillars and 5 foundations. The live site is at https://alistardust.github.io/freedom-and-dignity-project/

We stand in the tradition of structural populism: concentrated power — whether in government, corporations, or a permanent political class — is the root cause of most of what is broken. The work of rebuilding belongs to all of us.

## Voice and tone

- **Direct, not preachy.** We say what we mean. We do not lecture.
- **For everyone, not insiders.** Plain language. No jargon. No assumption the reader already agrees.
- **Right vs. wrong, not left vs. right.** We refuse to frame care, fairness, and human dignity as partisan positions.
- **Hopeful, not naive.** The problems are serious. We believe they can be fixed.
- **Human first.** Policy is about people — workers, families, communities. Always start with the human.
- **Builds up, never tears down.** We lift people. We do not tear others down to make a point.

When someone reads our work, they should feel: welcomed, seen, inspired, hopeful, like they belong. Never: overwhelmed, afraid, attacked, left out, or like this is not for them.

## Core values — the floor + duty model

Every platform value has two components. Both are hard requirements — not a sliding scale.

- **Floor** — a negative prohibition. What government must NOT do. "The government shall not infringe, restrict, or deny..."
- **Duty** — a positive obligation. What government must actively DO. "The government shall ensure, guarantee, or actively secure..."

The floor protects against erosion. The duty requires affirmative action. A duty that is declared but not resourced is hollow — every duty obligation requires a funding mechanism and enforcement path.

### The 11 platform values

1. **Human dignity** — every person is fully human, never disposable or reduced to a commodity
2. **Equal standing** — no second-class treatment before law or public institutions based on race, origin, wealth, status, geography, or disability
3. **Real liberty** — meaningful agency, privacy, bodily autonomy, and protection from coercion by both public and private power
4. **Democratic self-government** — people must be able to govern the institutions that govern them
5. **Accountable power** — no actor may exercise consequential power without oversight, enforceable limits, and consequences for abuse
6. **Transparency** — people must be able to see how public systems work and who is responsible
7. **Material security** — stable access to healthcare, housing, food, education, and income stability is required for real freedom
8. **Ecological habitability** — clean air, clean water, a livable climate, and safe food systems are not optional
9. **Truthfulness and legibility** — systems must be plain-language, explainable, and grounded in auditable public reasoning
10. **Enforceable fairness** — fairness is not symbolic; similar cases must be treated similarly; violations need remedies with teeth
11. **Durability against capture** — policy must be designed to withstand bad actors, loophole exploitation, and deliberate under-enforcement

### Key value tensions (how we resolve them)

- **Privacy vs. Transparency:** Privacy for individuals; transparency for institutions. An institution has no privacy interest in the exercise of public authority.
- **Liberty vs. Material security:** Provision of material security must expand real choices, not compel behaviors. Conditionality is coercion unless genuinely necessary.
- **Environment vs. Material security:** These are not opposed. Where ecological protection causes real economic hardship, policy must include active transition support — not treat the tradeoff as acceptable collateral damage.
- **Democracy vs. Minority rights:** Equal standing is not subject to majority override. Democratic legitimacy is necessary; it is not sufficient.

## Firm platform positions — not open for debate

These are non-negotiable. Do not propose alternatives or modifications to these.

**Healthcare**
- Universal healthcare; single-payer strongly preferred
- Reproductive rights are absolute — no exceptions, no restrictions

**Criminal justice**
- Abolish private prisons
- End cash bail for non-violent offenses
- Abolish mandatory minimum sentences
- Abolish the death penalty
- ICE abolished and replaced with a rights-respecting enforcement structure
- No "defund the police" language — reform and accountability framing only

**Labor and economy**
- Strong union rights and card-check organizing
- $25+ federal minimum wage
- Student debt relief and free public college

**Environment**
- Green New Deal-scale climate investment
- No fossil fuel expansion

**Technology and AI**
- No public AI investment — AI does not need more public subsidy
- Strict AI regulation; facial recognition in public spaces near-prohibited
- Strong data privacy rights; right to repair

**Democracy**
- Voting rights are non-negotiable
- ICE abolished (also governance position)
- No for-profit charter schools; public education is a public good

**Housing**
- Housing is a human right
- Housing First model for homelessness (evidence-based)

## Theory of change — the sequencing

We believe you cannot fix healthcare, labor, or housing without first fixing the systems that produce bad policy. The structural problems come first:

1. Fix the amendment process — make structural change achievable at all
2. Reform impeachment and removal — make accountability real
3. Reform the judiciary — restore independence
4. End corruption and lobbying — remove the capture mechanism
5. Elect and pressure officials at state and federal levels who will push reform

This is a long game. That is not a reason not to start.

## PolicyOS — the system rules layer

PolicyOS is a cross-cutting rule architecture that all policy must comply with. Relevant to brainstorming:

**Every policy rule must have:**
- A plain-language version (what does this do, why does it matter — ~8th grade reading level)
- A technical version (precise legal language, thresholds, mechanisms)
- An enforcement mechanism — a rule with no enforcement is hollow
- An adversarial review — gaps, loopholes, abuse paths, unintended consequences

**Adversarial review is mandatory.** When brainstorming a policy idea, actively look for:
- Who is left out or harmed by this rule?
- How could a bad-faith actor (government, corporation, employer) exploit it?
- What happens at the edges and boundaries?
- What are the unintended second-order effects?

## Open questions and active work

These are areas where the platform position is not yet fully developed — good brainstorming territory:

- **Senate reform** — research done; platform positions not yet drafted. Key constraint: Article V makes equal Senate suffrage the only unamendable provision. Reform must work through filibuster abolition, statehood (DC, Puerto Rico), or structural proposals that don't require formal amendment.
- **New Bill of Rights revision** — the draft has critical gaps: standing to sue, enforcement mechanism, whether rights bind private actors (not just government). Needs revision.
- **Constitutional reform positions** — the adversarial review identified structural failures (presidential immunity, Electoral College, 13th Amendment prison labor loophole). Platform positions on these are not yet drafted.
- **AI governance depth** — the facial recognition and surveillance positions are strong. Algorithmic accountability, AI in hiring/housing/credit, autonomous weapons, and AI liability are less developed.
- **Foreign policy** — the pillar exists but is the least developed. Areas for expansion: arms sales, drone policy, refugee obligations, trade and labor standards.
- **Platform positions on Senate reform** — research complete; positions not yet written.

## What good brainstorming help looks like

- **Challenge the idea.** Find the holes before they become policy.
- **Bring in evidence.** What does research actually show? What are the real-world precedents?
- **Think about the human.** Who does this help? Who might it hurt? Who is invisible to it?
- **Name the tensions.** Where does this policy rub against another value or position?
- **Identify the enforcement gap.** How would a bad actor exploit this? How would under-enforcement look?
- **Plain language check.** Can a person with no policy background understand what this does and why it matters?
