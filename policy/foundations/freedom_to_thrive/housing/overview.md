# Housing

**Foundation:** [Freedom to Thrive](../../foundations/freedom_to_thrive/values.md)

## Purpose

Guarantee stable, safe, habitable, and affordable housing for all people—addressing the full chain of failures from speculative corporate ownership and predatory landlords, through inadequate public housing, to zoning that artificially constrains supply. This pillar establishes rights for tenants, limits on speculative ownership, supply reform, anti-displacement protections, and Housing First approaches to homelessness.

## Core Principle

**Stable housing is a prerequisite for health, employment, education, and civic participation.** Housing systems must be designed to reduce extraction, displacement, and dependency—not normalize them. Shelter alone is not sufficient; quality, affordability, and security of tenure are the standard.

## The Problem It Solves

- Corporate and institutional investors are systematically purchasing single-family homes at scale, directly competing with owner-occupants and driving up prices in entry-level housing markets. Private equity ownership of rental housing has been linked to higher rents, reduced maintenance, and aggressive eviction practices.
- Exclusionary zoning—primarily single-family-only zoning covering roughly 75% of residential land in major American cities—artificially constrains housing supply, drives scarcity, and concentrates the costs of growth on lower-income communities.
- The U.S. public housing system has been chronically underfunded for decades, with the Capital Fund gap exceeding $70 billion. Residents live in deteriorating, often hazardous conditions while waiting lists stretch years.
- Homelessness affects over 600,000 Americans on any given night, yet criminalization of survival behavior—sleeping in public, encampments—has proven ineffective. Housing First approaches show dramatically better outcomes at lower cost.

## Design Logic

- Anti-speculation and corporate ownership limits address the demand-side distortions that institutional investors create in owner-occupancy markets, restoring access for ordinary buyers.
- Zoning and supply reform expands production of affordable, family-capable housing in high-demand areas while requiring anti-displacement safeguards so new growth doesn't displace existing residents.
- Tenant protections and eviction reform ensure that housing security is real in practice, not just nominal on paper—including right to counsel, habitability enforcement, and anti-retaliation rules.
- Housing First homelessness policy replaces punitive criminalization with evidence-based approaches that provide permanent housing and wrap-around services as the foundation for stability.

## Key Reform Areas

- Corporate and institutional ownership limits for single-family and owner-occupancy housing
- Anti-speculation vacancy taxes and anti-land-banking measures
- Zoning reform and supply expansion with anti-displacement safeguards
- Tenant rights: habitability, repairs, eviction due process, and anti-retaliation
- Public and social housing investment and maintenance standards
- Housing First homelessness policy and supportive services
- Rent stabilization and anti-displacement protections
- Community land trusts, cooperatives, and nonprofit housing models
- Anti-gentrification protections and preservation of existing affordable stock
- Right to repair for housing systems and landlord repair obligations

## Research & Context

Research from the Federal Reserve and National Bureau of Economic Research documents that institutional investor purchases of single-family homes—which rose dramatically after 2010 and again during the pandemic—are concentrated in entry-level price tiers, directly competing with first-time buyers. A 2022 NBER paper found that institutional landlords in Atlanta raised rents faster and evicted tenants at higher rates than small landlords, and that neighborhoods with high institutional ownership showed worse maintenance quality and lower tenant satisfaction.

Exclusionary zoning research from economists at Harvard, Berkeley, and the Urban Institute consistently finds that restrictions on multifamily housing significantly raise costs in high-demand metros. Minneapolis's elimination of single-family zoning in 2019 and subsequent rent stabilization showed meaningful effects on rent inflation compared to comparable peer cities. California's ADU reform package produced over 100,000 new permitted units in four years. YIMBY-adjacent reforms consistently outperform supply-restriction approaches in reducing cost burdens.

The Housing First model—providing permanent housing without preconditions such as sobriety or employment—shows dramatically superior outcomes across dozens of randomized controlled trials and longitudinal studies. The At Home/Chez Soi study (Canada) and multiple U.S. studies show that Housing First produces 80%+ housing retention rates compared to 40-50% for treatment-first models, at comparable or lower cost. The criminalization alternative—encampment sweeps, sleeping bans, anti-camping laws—shows no evidence of reducing homelessness and substantial evidence of increasing trauma, health harms, and costs.

Community land trusts and shared-equity models have demonstrated sustained affordability over decades. The Burlington Community Land Trust (now Champlain Housing Trust), studied extensively by the Lincoln Institute of Land Policy, shows that CLT homes remain affordable through resales at rates that market housing cannot sustain—preserving public investment in affordability across generations rather than converting it to windfall equity gains.
