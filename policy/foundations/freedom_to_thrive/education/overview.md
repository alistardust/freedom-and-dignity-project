# Education

**Foundation:** [Freedom to Thrive](../../foundations/freedom_to_thrive/values.md)

## Purpose

Establish universal access to high-quality education as a guaranteed right, ensuring every person—regardless of wealth, geography, race, or disability—can access the learning they need for economic mobility, civic participation, and full human development. This pillar addresses structural inequity in school funding, predatory student lending, secular public schooling, and the full pipeline from K-12 through higher education and lifelong learning.

## Core Principle

**Educational opportunity must not be determined by the wealth of one's family or zip code.** Every student deserves high-quality instruction, safe learning environments, and the ability to access higher education and workforce training without being burdened by predatory debt or arbitrary exclusion.

## The Problem It Solves

- Public school quality is directly tied to local property wealth, producing dramatically unequal educational outcomes by geography, race, and income. Schools in lower-income areas systematically lack resources, qualified teachers, and physical infrastructure.
- Higher education costs have risen far faster than wages, trapping millions in decades of student debt from institutions that frequently fail to deliver the promised economic returns—especially for-profit colleges.
- Vocational and technical pathways are systematically undervalued and underfunded, forcing students toward costly four-year degrees even where trade credentials better serve their goals.
- Civic education, media literacy, and science education are under political attack, threatening students' ability to reason, evaluate evidence, and participate in democratic life.

## Design Logic

- Funding equity requires breaking the link between local property wealth and school quality through state and federal equalization mechanisms, with additional resources for high-need schools.
- Debt elimination and free community college address the higher education access crisis directly—eliminating the predatory loan trap and ensuring vocational and community college pathways are accessible to all.
- Secular public schooling and evidence-based curriculum protect both religious freedom (by not imposing religion) and scientific literacy (by requiring empirical instruction).
- Governance accountability ensures that communities have input without enabling discrimination, resource inequity, or political indoctrination.

## Key Reform Areas

- Universal K-12 funding equity — break the property-wealth-to-school-quality pipeline
- Student debt relief and loan reform — broad federal forgiveness and restructured lending
- Free community college, vocational, and technical education for all eligible students
- Teacher compensation, workload, and professional development standards
- Civic education, media literacy, and science-based curriculum requirements
- Religious neutrality and evidence-based standards in public schools
- Anti-segregation zoning and school assignment reform
- Charter school and private school accountability when receiving public funds
- Higher education affordability and public investment in research integrity
- Lifelong learning and reskilling for workers displaced by automation

## Research & Context

The United States spends more per pupil on average than most OECD countries, yet produces dramatically unequal outcomes correlated strongly with race and income. A 2019 EdBuild report found that non-white school districts receive $23 billion less in funding annually than predominantly white districts, despite serving similar numbers of students. Property-tax-based school funding is the structural driver: districts with higher property values raise more revenue at lower rates, perpetuating advantages regardless of state aid formulas.

Student debt has surpassed $1.7 trillion, and federal reserve research shows that debt burdens delay homeownership, family formation, and wealth accumulation disproportionately for Black and Latino borrowers. The for-profit college sector accounts for 11% of enrollment but 44% of federal student loan defaults, according to the Senate HELP Committee's 2012 investigation. Predatory institutions systematically overpromise economic returns, target vulnerable populations, and leave graduates with worthless credentials and crushing debt.

American students consistently trail OECD peers in science literacy and civic knowledge. Studies by the National Assessment of Educational Progress (NAEP) show that fewer than a quarter of 8th graders score proficient in civics. The erosion of science education standards—particularly in states that have introduced non-scientific frameworks into biology and earth science curricula—has documented negative effects on workforce preparation in science, technology, engineering, and medicine.

Research on integration shows that socioeconomically diverse schools produce better outcomes for low-income students without harming higher-income peers. The Century Foundation and The Educational Opportunity Project at Stanford (SEDA dataset) document that school district boundaries and attendance zones remain among the most powerful drivers of educational inequality in the country, with segregation by income and race often as stark as it was in the 1970s.
