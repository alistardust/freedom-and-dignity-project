# A New Bill of Rights — Adversarial Review
*Freedom and Dignity Project — May 2026*

> **Adversarial review purpose:** Per PAOS-TEST-0008, this review actively searches for gaps, loopholes, edge cases, unintended consequences, abuse paths, structural weaknesses, and historical failure modes. It is a *required* part of the policy development process before adoption or publication. Findings are framed as issues to address in revision, not reasons to abandon the project.

> **Citation note:** Case names, statutory references, and historical facts are real. Speculative analysis is labeled as such. This review does not fabricate authority.

> **Document reviewed:** `policy/proposals/rights/new-bill-of-rights.md` — *Draft for public review, Freedom and Dignity Project, April 2026*

---

## Executive Summary

The New Bill of Rights draft is a coherent, well-structured document that correctly identifies the structural gap at the heart of the U.S. Constitution: the absence of affirmative rights. Its floor-plus-duty model is architecturally sound and addresses the most common failure mode of rights documents — aspirational language that compels nothing. However, several amendments have critical deficiencies that must be addressed before adoption: (1) Amendment I's "equal weight" sub-right may inadvertently prohibit the Electoral College while remaining silent on the precise mechanisms that make modern vote suppression effective; (2) Amendment V's "right to be forgotten" creates a direct and unresolved collision with First Amendment values, public records law, and the rights of accountability journalism; (3) Amendment VI's "free, prior, and informed consent" requirement has no arbitration mechanism for divided tribal consent and could be manipulated to produce strategic deadlock; (4) Amendments VII and X establish affirmative rights without specifying who bears enforcement authority, standing, or remedies against private actors; and (5) the document's horizontal application — whether these rights bind private actors — is left entirely implicit and unanswered throughout, which is the single most consequential gap in the document.

---

## Cross-Cutting Issues (Applicable to All Amendments)

These structural issues run across the entire document and must be resolved before any individual amendment is finalized.

### Horizontal Application: Does This Bind Private Actors?

The most important question the document does not answer is whether these rights apply only against government (vertical application) or also against private actors including corporations, employers, landlords, insurers, and platforms (horizontal application).

The U.S. Constitution's Bill of Rights is exclusively *vertically* applied — it constrains government, not private parties. All protections against private discrimination and exploitation rest on statutory law (*Civil Rights Act of 1964*, *Fair Housing Act of 1968*, *NLRA*, etc.), which can be repealed. This gap has been catastrophic: *The Civil Rights Cases* (1883) held the Civil Rights Act of 1875 unconstitutional because Congress cannot use the 14th Amendment to reach *private* conduct.

The New Bill of Rights appears to intend broader coverage. Amendment III's floor explicitly applies to "employer, government, or institution." Amendment V applies to "government or private entity." Amendment VII's floor extends to "government, corporation, or entity." But Amendments I, IV, VIII, IX, and X are silent on horizontal scope. This inconsistency will be exploited: if the document intends private sector application, it must say so uniformly and address the significant doctrinal complications. If it does not intend private sector application, its protections in Amendment X (right to rest), Amendment VIII (employment discrimination), and Amendment III (workplace organizing) are worth far less than they appear.

**Issue to address in revision:** Define horizontal application explicitly and uniformly across all amendments. For amendments intended to bind private actors (which should be most of them), specify whether direct constitutional liability or statutory implementation is the expected mechanism.

---

### Standing: Who May Sue?

The document establishes rights but does not specify who may sue to enforce them. Under current federal constitutional law, standing requires injury-in-fact, causation, and redressability (*Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife* (1992)). The Court has used standing doctrine to dismiss environmental, informational, consumer, and civil rights cases where the injury is systemic or diffuse rather than particularized.

Amendment VIII's floor statement — "a justice system that produces systematically unequal outcomes by race or wealth fails this floor" — is a textbook systemic injury that standing doctrine has historically made extremely difficult to litigate. Without explicit standing provisions, a hostile court can acknowledge the right while preventing any individual plaintiff from establishing injury sufficient to sue.

**Issue to address in revision:** Include an explicit standing provision — or at minimum, authorize Congress to establish broad standing to enforce these rights — to prevent the document's protections from being defeated on procedural grounds.

---

### Self-Execution: Are These Rights Directly Enforceable Without Legislation?

Constitutional provisions are either "self-executing" (enforceable in court without implementing legislation) or non-self-executing (requiring legislative action to have legal effect). The document does not address this question. Rights framed as "the government *must* build and maintain systems" (Amendment X duty) are likely non-self-executing — they require implementing legislation. Rights framed as "no law may" (Amendment I floor) are more likely self-executing.

This distinction matters enormously. A non-self-executing right that requires Congress to pass legislation is a right that can be permanently deferred by a Congress that opposes it. The history of the Equal Rights Amendment — passed by Congress, ratified by the required number of states, still not recognized as law — illustrates the fragility of rights that require congressional implementation.

**Issue to address in revision:** Specify which provisions are self-executing and which require implementing legislation. For those requiring legislation, include a judicially enforceable duty to pass implementing legislation within a specified time period, with a fallback of direct judicial enforcement if Congress fails to act.

---

### Remedies: What Happens When Rights Are Violated?

The document establishes floors but does not specify what judicial or administrative remedies are available when government crosses them. Under current constitutional doctrine, suits against government for constitutional violations are constrained by qualified immunity (for individual officers, *Harlow v. Fitzgerald* (1982)), sovereign immunity (for the government itself, waivable only by statute), and the absence of an implied damages remedy for most constitutional violations (*Bivens v. Six Unknown Named Agents* (1971) and its subsequent gutting).

Without an explicit remedies provision, the document's floors can be crossed without consequence — exactly the pattern the U.S. Constitution has exhibited.

**Issue to address in revision:** Include a remedies section establishing that violations of any floor or duty entitle the aggrieved party to injunctive relief, compensatory damages, and, where appropriate, structural relief. Explicitly abrogate qualified and sovereign immunity for violations of these rights.

---

### Interaction with Existing Constitutional Text

The document is drafted as if it will be added to or operate alongside the existing U.S. Constitution. It does not address conflicts with existing constitutional provisions. Key unresolved conflicts:

- Amendment I's "equal weight" sub-right may conflict with the 12th Amendment (Electoral College).
- Amendment IV's "freedom from forced medical treatment" may conflict with existing public health emergency powers doctrine.
- Amendment V's "right to be forgotten" may conflict with the First Amendment.
- Amendment VIII's "full participation" and Amendment IX's "minority cultural protection" will generate conflicts in the same space currently occupied by the tension between anti-discrimination law and religious liberty claims (*Masterpiece Cakeshop v. Colorado Civil Rights Commission* (2018); *303 Creative LLC v. Elenis* (2023)).

**Issue to address in revision:** Include an explicit provision stating how these amendments interact with and, where relevant, supersede conflicting provisions of the existing Constitution and Bill of Rights.

## Amendment I — Right to Vote

### Gaps

**Felon Disenfranchisement:** The amendment guarantees suffrage to "every eligible person" but does not define eligibility or address felon disenfranchisement. Approximately 4.6 million Americans are disenfranchised due to felony convictions (Sentencing Project, 2022), with disproportionate impact on Black Americans. The floor as written — "no law may restrict the right of any eligible person" — is silent on whether permanent disenfranchisement satisfies the eligibility carve-out. A state could define "eligible" to exclude all former felons and satisfy the letter of this amendment. This must be addressed.

**Ranked Choice Voting:** "Equal weight" is undefined. Does it require ranked choice? Does it prohibit plurality-wins elections where a candidate wins with 35% support? Does it prohibit proportional representation methods that would actually equalize vote weight better than single-member districts? The sub-right is present but undefined.

**Mail-In Ballot Deadlines:** The duty provision requires "vote-by-mail" but does not address return deadlines, postmark rules, or the contested question of whether states may refuse to count ballots received after Election Day. Courts have divided sharply on these questions. The document's protection is real but its operational content needs specificity.

**Electoral College:** The "equal weight" sub-right, if self-executing, is arguably incompatible with the Electoral College as currently structured: a vote cast in Wyoming for president has more electoral weight than a vote cast in California. The document does not address this conflict. If the intent is to prohibit the Electoral College, that should be stated. If it is not, the sub-right needs a carve-out that will confuse its application in every other context.

### Loopholes

The floor prohibits "voter suppression in any form" and names specific examples (ID requirements, polling place closures, purging, racial gerrymandering). Naming examples implies a list, which creates the inference that unnamed methods might be permissible. A hostile legislature would argue: we're using a method not on the list, therefore not prohibited. The phrase "in any form" should control over the listed examples, but the naming convention creates ambiguity.

"Practical access" as a sub-right is defined by reference to practicality, a deeply contested standard. Courts applying rational basis review can find almost any voting restriction "practically" justifiable. The floor needs a stronger standard: not "practically achievable" but "actually achieved," with a presumption of violation for any restriction that cannot be shown to increase ballot security without reducing access.

### Unintended Consequences

The duty to provide "automatic voter registration" and "sufficient polling locations" creates a federal mandate on states without specifying funding mechanisms. If implementation costs are unfunded mandates, states with limited revenue capacity will face compliance obligations they cannot meet, creating a new litigation front rather than a genuine expansion of access.

### Abuse Paths

"Ballot integrity" as a sub-right is a term that has been weaponized to justify restrictions designed to suppress votes rather than protect them. The "Stop the Steal" campaign of 2020 mobilized "ballot integrity" as justification for disenfranchisement. The sub-right should be defined to make explicit that it protects *all counted ballots*, not a mechanism to challenge or remove ballots.

### Adversarial Review Finding

Amendment I correctly identifies the core problem — suffrage that exists on paper but is suppressed in practice — but its "equal weight" language raises a conflict with the Electoral College that must be resolved. Its eligibility language must address felon disenfranchisement. Its floor's naming of specific forms of suppression creates an implied list that a hostile court will use to authorize non-listed methods. These are revision-worthy but not fatal issues.

## Amendment II — Right to Self-Governance

### Gaps

**Billionaire Media Ownership:** The floor prohibits any "private individual, corporation, or concentrated private or foreign interest" from capturing "democratic institutions through which people govern themselves." Media ecosystems — including social media platforms, search engines, and cable news networks — are not clearly "democratic institutions" in the legal sense, but they are arguably the information infrastructure through which democratic self-governance occurs. If Elon Musk's acquisition of Twitter/X and its use to amplify preferred political content, suppress disfavored content, and shape election discourse does not constitute "capture of democratic institutions," what does? The document does not say.

**Algorithmic Manipulation:** The floor is silent on algorithmic curation of political information. A platform that shows users only content reinforcing their existing beliefs, depresses turnout among targeted demographics through engagement optimization, or steers advertising targeting based on inferred political behavior could argue it is not "capturing" democratic institutions. The capture framing addresses acquisition; it does not address distortion through design.

**Foreign Interference:** "Foreign entity" is named in the floor but undefined. Does this include foreign-owned companies operating in the U.S.? Foreign state actors operating through domestic cutouts? Foreign nationals purchasing media companies? The regulatory scope is unclear.

### Loopholes

"Dark money, gerrymandering designed to entrench power, and institutional structures that systematically silence communities violate this floor" — the word "designed" creates an intent requirement that mirrors the constitutional failure in *Washington v. Davis*. Gerrymandering that is structurally entrench-power-serving but not provably "designed" to do so will evade this floor. Compare this to the voting rights context: *Rucho v. Common Cause* (2019) found that courts could not regulate partisan gerrymandering partly because assessing "design" requires political judgment courts shouldn't make. A floor that requires proof of design replicates the problems of intent-based constitutional standards.

### Unintended Consequences

The duty requires "public financing of elections." This is the right policy direction. But public financing systems have historically been undercut by their co-existence with unlimited private spending (*Buckley v. Valeo* (1976); *Arizona Free Enterprise Club v. Bennett* (2011), striking down Arizona's public financing matching provision). Without addressing the First Amendment doctrine treating campaign expenditures as speech, public financing can be built and bypassed simultaneously. The document needs to confront this head-on.

### Abuse Paths

"Equal representation — communities have a right to meaningful voice in proportion to their numbers" creates a potential conflict with geographic representation models, tribal representation, and any form of deliberate over-representation of historically suppressed communities. An anti-affirmative-action litigant could use this sub-right to challenge Indigenous set-asides, majority-minority districts, or other representation mechanisms intended to include historically excluded groups. This must be explicitly addressed.

### Adversarial Review Finding

Amendment II is the most conceptually ambitious of the ten amendments and the most vulnerable to definitional manipulation. The core problem: "capture," "distort," and "effectively control" are all undefined standards that will be litigated for decades. The amendment correctly identifies the problem of private power over democratic institutions but does not yet provide the definitional clarity needed to enforce it against sophisticated bad-faith actors.

## Amendment III — Right to Organize

### Gaps

**Gig Workers and Misclassification:** The right protects "any person" from retaliation for "labor organizing." But independent contractors — including gig workers deliberately misclassified as contractors to avoid labor law coverage — are not legally "employees" under current law and are therefore excluded from collective bargaining rights under the National Labor Relations Act (29 U.S.C. § 151 et seq.). The amendment does not address misclassification. A corporation that converts its entire workforce to independent contractor status would argue that those workers have the right to *meet* (they can't be stopped from assembling) but not the right to *bargain collectively* (there's no employer relationship). The amendment must specify that misclassification does not eliminate labor rights.

**Algorithmic Management and Anti-Union Surveillance:** The floor prohibits "union-busting through surveillance, termination, or intimidation" but does not address modern algorithmically-mediated labor control: automated scheduling designed to prevent workers from having stable enough hours to organize; algorithmic monitoring that generates pretextual termination grounds; mandatory "captive audience" meetings (now addressing algorithmic platforms rather than human supervisors). These are real, documented tactics; the floor's current language does not clearly reach them.

**International Workers:** The amendment protects "any person" — does this include undocumented workers? Agricultural workers? Domestic workers? These three categories were explicitly excluded from the original NLRA (1935) — in the case of farmworkers and domestic workers, deliberately, to preserve the racialized labor structure of the South as a condition of New Deal passage. The amendment should explicitly name these historically excluded groups.

### Loopholes

"No employer, government, or institution may *prohibit, punish, or retaliate* against any person" — this is negative prohibition only. It does not require employers to *recognize* a union, bargain in good faith, or reach an agreement. Under current NLRA doctrine, an employer can comply with this floor by not formally punishing workers while simply refusing to negotiate, delaying bargaining interminably, and making union certification practically worthless. The duty section addresses "enforceable" union rights, but the floor must affirmatively require good-faith bargaining, not just prohibition of formal retaliation.

### Unintended Consequences

The right to organize for "political advocacy without retaliation" could be read to prohibit at-will termination for political activity — a significant expansion of current employment law. Currently, most states allow termination for political activity that creates workplace disruption. The amendment needs to specify whether political activity protections apply to all political activity, only protected electoral activity, or activity that is specifically organizational in character.

### Adversarial Review Finding

Amendment III is strong in its core provisions but leaves two major gaps: gig worker misclassification and the distinction between prohibition of retaliation versus affirmative duty to bargain. A determined bad-faith employer could satisfy the letter of this amendment while making the right to organize practically worthless.

## Amendment IV — Right to Bodily Autonomy

### Gaps

**Forced Sterilization:** The amendment covers "reproductive choices" and "reproductive rights" but does not explicitly name forced sterilization, which was legally sanctioned in *Buck v. Bell* (1927) — a case never formally overruled, though widely understood to be no longer good law — and practiced on incarcerated people as recently as 2010 (California Department of Corrections, sterilization of incarcerated women; state investigation published 2014). The document should name forced sterilization as a named floor violation, as it named the boarding school era in Amendment IX.

**People with Disabilities:** The freedom from "forced medical treatment" sub-right may conflict with guardianship law, which in most states permits court-appointed guardians to make medical decisions — including irreversible ones — for adults with intellectual disabilities without their consent, on a "best interests" standard that has been widely abused. The amendment's scope for people under guardianship is unclear and must be addressed.

**Incarcerated Persons:** The bodily autonomy guarantees in this amendment are facially applicable to "every person." But incarcerated people are currently subject to forced medical treatment, denial of reproductive healthcare, forced psychiatric medication, and invasive searches in ways that would violate this floor. The amendment should either explicitly address the incarceration context or make clear that these rights do not evaporate upon conviction.

### Loopholes — "Freedom from Forced Medical Treatment" vs. Public Health

The sub-right "no person may be compelled to undergo medical procedures" is broadly stated. Compulsory vaccination programs, mandatory tuberculosis treatment for infectious cases, and quarantine authority have been upheld as constitutional exercises of the state's police power (*Jacobson v. Massachusetts* (1905)). If this sub-right is read as an absolute prohibition on compelled medical treatment, it would invalidate public health interventions with strong evidence bases. If it is read as subject to a public health exception, the exception could be weaponized to justify invasive mandatory procedures well beyond epidemic control. The scope of this sub-right must be defined with reference to evidence-based public health necessity, proportionality, and the least-restrictive-means principle.

**End-of-Life and MAID (Medical Aid in Dying):** The "end-of-life decisions" sub-right is stated without reference to MAID specifically. Medical Aid in Dying is banned in the majority of U.S. states. If this sub-right is self-executing, it would presumably preempt those bans — which may well be the intent. But it should be stated. The floor language ("laws that ban abortion, prohibit gender-affirming care, or criminalize personal medical decisions violate this floor") should be read to include MAID bans, but adding it explicitly would remove any ambiguity.

### Unintended Consequences

The duty to provide "practical, accessible, and affordable access to the full range of reproductive healthcare... regardless of geography, income, or insurance status" creates an affirmative government obligation that would require, at minimum, federal subsidization of abortion services in states that currently ban them. This is the correct policy direction but the implementation mechanism — how, who funds, what timeline — must be specified or the duty becomes a target for fiscal deferrals that render it meaningless.

### Adversarial Review Finding

Amendment IV correctly names the core bodily autonomy rights and would, if self-executing, overturn the post-*Dobbs* abortion bans, state bans on gender-affirming care, and (implicitly) MAID bans. The forced medical treatment provision requires careful scoping to prevent weaponization against public health authority. Forced sterilization and incarcerated persons' bodily rights should be named explicitly.

## Amendment V — Right to Privacy

### Gaps

**The "Right to Be Forgotten" vs. the First Amendment:** The most significant structural problem in Amendment V. The right to "request deletion of personal data held by private entities" directly conflicts with existing First Amendment doctrine. In *Florida Star v. B.J.F.* (1989), the Supreme Court held that truthful information about matters of public significance generally cannot be suppressed even if the subject prefers privacy. The California "right to be forgotten" under CCPA and the EU's GDPR have already generated litigation over whether data deletion requirements constitute impermissible compelled speech or suppression of truthful information.

If a domestic violence survivor requests deletion of their location data from an aggregator, the right to be forgotten clearly serves its purpose. If a convicted politician requests deletion of records of their conviction from news archives, the same right becomes an accountability-destruction mechanism. The right to be forgotten must be scoped to distinguish between: (a) commercial data broker aggregation of personal data, (b) public records and journalism archives, (c) data held by institutions with legitimate retention interests (medical records, employment records with litigation holds), and (d) data about public figures in their public roles. The document does not make any of these distinctions.

**Public Records Law Conflict:** Government records — including court records, property records, arrest records, and business filings — are generally public under FOIA and state public records laws. A right to request deletion of personal data "held by private entities" does not directly conflict with these, but the floor's broader language — "no government or private entity may... collect, retain, sell, or use data about a person's life... without informed, meaningful, and revocable consent" — could be read to conflict with the government's legitimate maintenance of public records, which by definition retain data without individual consent.

**Accountability for Public Figures:** A privacy right that extends to public figures in their official roles is incompatible with democratic accountability. Politicians, executives, and public officials in the exercise of public functions cannot have a constitutional right to privacy that shields their official conduct from scrutiny. This is a well-established principle in defamation law (*New York Times v. Sullivan* (1964)) and should be explicitly reflected in the privacy amendment.

### Loopholes

The floor requires "informed, meaningful, and revocable consent" for data collection. The history of consent mechanisms in the digital economy — 42-page privacy policies presented on a take-it-or-leave-it basis, "consent" embedded in terms of service, cookie banners designed to obscure opting out — demonstrates that formal consent requirements without structural design mandates are easily circumvented. The duty to "establish and enforce strong data protection standards" addresses this, but the floor's consent standard must be defined: consent is meaningful only if refusal carries no penalty and the alternative (not using the service) is genuinely available.

### Unintended Consequences

The floor's prohibition on the "commercial sale of personal data without consent" would, if directly applied to current U.S. data economy actors, require restructuring of the business models of essentially every major technology company. This is arguably appropriate, but the magnitude of the disruption should be acknowledged. The document should either specify an implementation timeline or acknowledge that this right requires substantial transitional infrastructure.

### Adversarial Review Finding

Amendment V's right to be forgotten is the single most legally vulnerable provision in the New Bill of Rights. As currently written, it will collide directly with the First Amendment, and a hostile Supreme Court will resolve that collision by gutting the privacy right — exactly as happened with privacy rights more broadly in the post-*Katz* era. The distinction between commercial data aggregation and public-interest journalism/accountability must be drawn in the text itself.

## Amendment VI — Right to Indigenous Sovereignty

### Gaps

**Who Gives Consent?** "Free, prior, and informed consent" (FPIC) is a recognized standard under the UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP, 2007, endorsed by the U.S. in 2010). But implementation of FPIC is contested in several ways: (1) when tribal governance is internally divided, which faction speaks for the nation?; (2) when a proposed project affects multiple tribal nations with different positions, does any single nation's refusal constitute a veto?; (3) what constitutes consent being "informed" — does it require environmental impact assessments, legal counsel, extended consultation timelines?; (4) can consent be withdrawn after initial agreement, and if so, when?

None of these questions are answered in the document. They are not rhetorical: the Dakota Access Pipeline produced exactly the scenario where one segment of the Standing Rock Sioux Tribe's government was presented as consenting while the broader tribal community protested, and federal agencies used that partial consent to proceed.

**Interaction with State Sovereignty:** "Tribal governments are sovereign and must be treated as co-equal by federal and state governments" — the state sovereignty issue is the most contested dimension of tribal sovereignty. States have historically claimed authority over non-Indians within reservations, authority over taxation, authority over gaming and land use, and authority over water rights. The document's recognition of co-equal sovereignty with states raises the question of which law governs conflicts between tribal and state authority. The existing framework (*Worcester v. Georgia* (1832); the *McClanahan* test) is complex and incomplete. This amendment must either address the conflict framework or explicitly supersede the existing framework.

**Alaska Natives and Native Hawaiian Peoples:** The amendment addresses "Indigenous nations" and cross-references the Declaration of Indigenous Rights but does not address Alaska Native peoples, whose legal status under the Alaska Native Claims Settlement Act (ANCSA, 1971) is materially different from lower-48 tribal nations — ANCSA replaced traditional tribal sovereignty with regional and village corporations. Amendment IX addresses Native Hawaiian peoples specifically but does not specify how they interact with Amendment VI's Indigenous sovereignty framework. This gap should be closed.

### Loopholes

The floor states that no government "may abrogate, diminish, or override the sovereign rights of Indigenous nations... without the free, prior, and informed consent." The qualifying phrase "without consent" creates a structurally available consent-laundering path: a well-resourced actor could identify a tribal member or a small faction with any arguable representative standing and use that consent to proceed. *Morton v. Mancari* (1974) and the federal trust responsibility doctrine provide some structural protection against this, but the consent standard itself must specify that consent means the considered, deliberative, and verifiable decision of the recognized governing body of the nation, not consent manufactured through division or coercion.

### Adversarial Review Finding

Amendment VI is the most architecturally incomplete of the ten amendments. FPIC is a real and valuable standard but its enforcement mechanism must be specified. The co-equal sovereignty claim for tribal governments vs. states is the most legally ambitious provision in the document and will generate maximum resistance from state governments. This amendment cannot be adopted without a companion implementation document specifying the FPIC process, the adjudication mechanism for disputed consent, and the framework for tribal-state-federal conflict resolution.

## Amendment VII — Right to a Healthy Environment

### Gaps

**"Stable Climate" — Who Determines Stability?** The sub-right provides a "right to a livable climate not deliberately destabilized by preventable emissions." "Deliberately destabilized," "preventable," and "livable" are all undefined. A government that enacted insufficient emissions reductions could argue it was not "deliberately" destabilizing the climate. An industry actor could argue its emissions were not individually "preventable" given technology available at any given time. Climate litigation in domestic U.S. courts has repeatedly failed on causation grounds — the difficulty of tracing systemic harm to specific actors is exactly the evidentiary problem that makes climate rights provisions difficult to enforce. *City of Oakland v. BP* (2018), *Mayor and City Council of Baltimore v. BP* (2021), and other municipal climate cases have been dismissed on various grounds.

**Future Generations — Justiciability Problem:** "Environmental decisions must account for the rights of those who will inherit this world" is the most politically compelling provision and the most legally vulnerable. Future persons do not currently have legal standing to sue (*Massachusetts v. EPA* (2007) involved state standing to sue for climate action, not future-person standing). This provision requires either: (a) a statutory representative mechanism (like the Welsh Future Generations Commissioner) to give future-person interests a voice in current proceedings; or (b) explicit standing for environmental organizations to represent future-generation interests.

**Private Actors:** "No government, corporation, or entity may take actions that knowingly degrade air quality, contaminate water sources... or accelerate climate change" creates a direct private right of action against corporations. This is appropriate policy, but the knowledge standard ("knowingly") must be defined — does it require actual knowledge, or constructive knowledge, or foreseeability? And what is the remedy? Injunction, damages, disgorgement? The remedies question determines whether this provision is useful in practice.

### Loopholes

**"Knowingly" as a Scienter Standard:** The floor's "knowingly degrade" and "knowingly... accelerate climate change" language echoes criminal law scienter standards. This creates an evasion path: corporations that deliberately suppress or manufacture uncertainty about environmental harms — a well-documented practice in the fossil fuel industry (see *Naomi Oreskes & Erik Conway, Merchants of Doubt* (2010); internal Exxon documents showing knowledge of climate risks since the 1970s) — can argue they did not "knowingly" cause harm if their internal documentation can be characterized as inconclusive. A "knew or should have known" standard (i.e., negligence) would be more protective.

**Environmental Racism — "No Community May Bear a Disproportionate Burden":** This is exactly right as policy but extremely difficult to enforce without a disparate impact standard. Under current Equal Protection doctrine (*Washington v. Davis* (1976)), proving discriminatory environmental siting requires intent, not just disparate impact. This floor, if read to require only a showing of disproportionate burden (not discriminatory intent), would be more protective than current constitutional doctrine — but that reading must be made explicit.

### Adversarial Review Finding

Amendment VII has the right scope and the right values. Its critical weaknesses are evidentiary: "knowingly," "deliberately," and "preventable" are scienter standards that will be litigated to exhaustion by well-resourced industry actors whose business model depends on defeating them. The amendment needs a negligence-minimum scienter floor and explicit private rights of action with defined remedies. The future generations provision requires a standing mechanism to be more than aspirational.

## Amendment VIII — Right to Equal Justice

### Gaps

**"Systematically Unequal Outcomes" — Enforcement Without Intent Standard:** The floor's declaration that "a justice system that produces systematically unequal outcomes by race or wealth fails this floor, regardless of formal neutrality" is one of the most important provisions in the document. It is also in direct tension with existing constitutional doctrine: *McCleskey v. Kemp* (1987) held that statistical evidence of systemic racial disparity in death sentencing does not establish a constitutional violation without proof of discriminatory intent in the specific case.

If this amendment supersedes *McCleskey*, it creates a disparate impact standard for the criminal justice system — meaning statistical evidence of racial disparity is itself sufficient to establish a constitutional violation. This is the correct policy direction. But the document must say so explicitly. A hostile court presented with this language and existing doctrine will default to the *McCleskey* intent standard unless the text forecloses it.

**Affirmative Remedies vs. Prohibition:** Does "equal justice" require only that government stop discriminating, or does it require affirmative structural remediation? The duty provision addresses this — government "must dismantle the structural barriers." But the floor provision only prohibits certain conduct. A government that has stopped *adding* structural barriers but refuses to dismantle existing ones could argue it is in compliance with the floor while the duty remains aspirational. The enforcement gap between floor and duty must be closed.

**"Family Integrity" — Immigration Context:** "No government may arbitrarily separate families" is the correct response to family separation policies at the border. But "arbitrarily" creates a carve-out that will be litigated: is family separation pursuant to an immigration enforcement policy "arbitrary"? *Zadvydas v. Davis* (2001) and *INS v. St. Cyr* (2001) established some constitutional limits on immigration detention, but the plenary power doctrine — which gives Congress and the executive near-unlimited authority over immigration — could be invoked to characterize family separation as non-arbitrary policy rather than constitutional violation. The amendment must address this explicitly if it is meant to constrain immigration enforcement.

### Loopholes

The "full participation" sub-right prohibits exclusion "from public life, employment, housing, education, and civic institutions without discrimination." The phrase "without discrimination" implies a formal equality standard: you can be excluded for non-discriminatory reasons. This mirrors the exact structure of anti-discrimination law that has been unable to reach structural exclusion and systemic inequity. If the document means something more — structural inclusion, affirmative access, outcome-oriented equity — it must say so.

**Accessibility:** The accessibility sub-right (right of people with disabilities "to full and equal access to all public and private spaces and services") is the only sub-right that explicitly extends to private as well as public spaces. This is appropriate — and it establishes a clear precedent that horizontal application is intended in at least some contexts, which strengthens the argument that other sub-rights should also be read horizontally.

### Adversarial Review Finding

Amendment VIII correctly frames equal justice as a structural rather than individualized right — and its "systematically unequal outcomes" language is the document's most significant legal innovation, explicitly rejecting the intent doctrine that has gutted 14th Amendment equal protection claims for 50 years. This provision will face maximum resistance from defenders of existing doctrine. The document should fortify it by explicitly stating that statistical evidence of systemic disparity is sufficient to establish a floor violation and by specifying that injunctive relief including structural remedies is available.

## Amendment IX — Right to Cultural Identity

### Gaps

**Religious Freedom vs. LGBTQ+ Rights:** The amendment protects "the right to practice one's faith... free from government interference." The right to "full participation" in Amendment VIII protects LGBTQ+ persons from discrimination. These two rights will conflict, as they already do under current law. *303 Creative LLC v. Elenis* (2023) held that a business owner has a First Amendment right to decline to create custom websites for same-sex weddings. *Masterpiece Cakeshop v. Colorado Civil Rights Commission* (2018) left the question open but signaled sympathy for religious exemptions.

The document must specify how these rights rank when they conflict, or provide a balancing framework. Leaving two constitutional rights in unresolved conflict is not a drafting choice — it is a delegation of the question to courts, which in the current environment means outcomes that may contradict the document's underlying values.

**"Minority Cultural Protection" vs. Amendment VIII Equal Treatment:** The state "may not use its resources to suppress or erase minority cultures." But what about majority culture practices that discriminate against minority community members within those cultures? A minority cultural community that discriminates by gender, sexuality, or disability status — and whose members are the subject of that discrimination — creates a conflict between Amendment IX's protection of the cultural community and Amendment VIII's guarantee of individual equal treatment. The document must specify that cultural protection cannot be used to shield intra-community discrimination.

**Who Decides What Is a Protected "Culture"?** The document protects "language, religion, cultural practices, or community identity" but does not define "culture" or specify who has authority to determine what counts as a protected cultural practice. This is non-trivial: QAnon claims to be a cultural identity; neo-Confederate heritage organizations claim cultural protection; extremist religious communities claim cultural practices that harm members. The document needs either a definition of protected culture or a principled exclusion for practices that violate other enumerated rights.

### Loopholes

"The state may not use its resources to suppress or erase minority cultures" — this prohibition applies only to direct state action. It does not reach private actors who suppress minority cultures. Given that private actors (tech platforms, media corporations, employers) are often the primary instruments of cultural suppression, this is a significant limitation.

### Adversarial Review Finding

Amendment IX is valuable but requires a conflict-resolution framework for the religious freedom vs. LGBTQ+ rights collision. The document cannot leave this to courts without addressing it. The "who decides what is a culture" problem is genuine and requires a principled answer that prevents the protection from being claimed by movements that exist to harm others.

## Amendment X — Right to Basic Necessities

### Gaps

**"Living Income" — Who Decides the Amount?** The sub-right establishes "the right to an income sufficient to meet basic needs." This is self-executing in form — "the right exists" — but the content is empty without a mechanism to determine the minimum. The duty provision says government must build "a living income floor (through wages, direct supports, or both)." But neither the floor nor the duty specifies: who determines the level, how often it is updated, and whether it is judicially reviewable for adequacy. A minimum wage of $1/hour technically satisfies a "living income floor" if the floor is never defined.

This is not a minor detail. The entire history of minimum wage law in the U.S. is a history of legislatures setting wages below subsistence and federal courts refusing to review the adequacy of legislative judgments on economic matters. Without a judicially reviewable standard for what constitutes a "living income," this provision will be satisfied by whatever Congress chooses to enact, regardless of whether it actually sustains life.

**"Right to Rest" — Against Whom?** The right to "time free from work, free from obligation, and free from the demand to be always available" is one of the document's most forward-looking provisions, responding to the always-online, gig-economy, 24/7-availability work culture. But it is unclear whether this right runs horizontally against private employers or only vertically against government. If vertical only, it prohibits governments from requiring public employees to be always on-call, which is appropriate but narrow. If horizontal, it potentially requires private employers to respect off-hours time — a significant labor law reform that would need an implementation mechanism.

**Education — "Quality" and "Free from Debt Servitude":** "The right to a quality public education at all levels, free from debt servitude" is the strongest affirmative education right in any proposed U.S. constitutional text. "Quality" is undefined but judicially reviewable — courts have assessed education quality in school funding cases under state constitutions. The "free from debt servitude" language would, if self-executing, prohibit federal student loan programs as currently structured. That may be the intent. If so, it should be stated explicitly.

### Loopholes

**Healthcare — "Without Financial Ruin":** The right to "medically necessary care without financial ruin" is a carefully hedged formulation — notably, it is not a right to *all* healthcare, only medically necessary care, and the limit is financial ruin, not financial hardship. A system that leaves people with $50,000 in medical debt satisfies this floor as long as it does not financially ruin them — an indefensible result. "Without financial ruin" needs to be replaced with "without cost as a barrier to access" or at minimum "without cost exceeding a defined percentage of income."

**Housing — "Stable, Safe, and Affordable":** These are three separate adjectives with different implications. "Stable" addresses tenure security and eviction protection. "Safe" addresses habitability. "Affordable" addresses cost relative to income. All three are correct. None is defined. Courts have been reluctant to impose specific definitions of housing adequacy on legislative branches. The document needs at minimum an operational definition of "affordable" (the HUD standard of 30% of income is widely used) and a specification of what "stable" means (just cause eviction requirements, right to renew, etc.).

**Food — "Adequate and Nutritious":** These two adjectives require careful definition in a country where food deserts — areas with no access to affordable nutritious food — are a documented systemic feature in low-income and Black communities. "Adequate" without "nutritious" could be satisfied by minimal caloric provision. "Nutritious" without reference to accessibility and affordability could be satisfied by a system where nutritious food exists but is unaffordable to the poor. The intersection of these terms with the economic conditions that produce food insecurity must be addressed.

### Unintended Consequences

The duty provision for Amendment X is the most comprehensive affirmative government obligation in the document: "universal healthcare, affordable housing programs, nutrition support, a living income floor (through wages, direct supports, or both), free public education through higher education, subsidized childcare, elder care infrastructure, and labor protections for rest." The aggregate cost of fully satisfying this duty would require a major restructuring of U.S. fiscal priorities. The document is silent on funding mechanisms, taxation authority, and the interaction between this duty and federalism (which level of government bears which obligation). This does not mean the rights are wrong — it means the companion legislation must be specified.

**Interaction with Market Mechanisms:** The duty's final clause — "the market alone cannot and does not satisfy this duty" — is correct as an empirical statement but raises the question of what role market mechanisms play in implementing these rights. Universal healthcare can be implemented through a single-payer system, a regulated private insurance market, or hybrid models. The document appropriately does not mandate implementation mechanism, but the anti-market framing may be read by hostile courts as prohibiting market-based implementation approaches that could otherwise satisfy the duty.

### Adversarial Review Finding

Amendment X is the most politically consequential amendment in the document and, as written, the most vulnerable to fiscal deferralism — the practice of acknowledging rights while claiming insufficient resources to implement them. Every key term in the rights list needs a defined floor value (what is "living income," what is "affordable," what is "quality education") that is judicially reviewable for adequacy. The right to rest must specify whether it runs against private employers and by what mechanism. Without these specifications, Amendment X risks becoming a constitutional aspiration rather than an enforceable right — exactly the failure mode the floor-plus-duty model is designed to prevent.

## Preamble — Review

### Gaps

The Preamble invokes FDR's 1944 Economic Bill of Rights and positions this document as its realization. This is historically and politically powerful framing. However, the Preamble's claim that these rights extend to "every person... within the reach of this government" — not "every citizen" — is one of the most significant choices in the document and is nowhere addressed in the text or enforcement provisions.

If "every person within the reach of this government" includes undocumented immigrants, visa holders, foreign nationals on U.S. soil, and persons in U.S. territories (Puerto Rico, Guam, U.S. Virgin Islands, American Samoa), the document is making a substantive choice that has enormous political and legal implications and that should be acknowledged and explained rather than embedded in a single preamble sentence.

## What the Document Does Well

This review has focused on weaknesses because that is its purpose. But an honest adversarial review must also identify what is architecturally sound:

1. **The floor-plus-duty model** is the correct architecture for rights documents that intend to compel affirmative action rather than merely prohibit government conduct. It is more sophisticated than any provision of the existing U.S. Constitution.

2. **The explicit rejection of formal neutrality** as sufficient in Amendment VIII is the document's most important legal innovation. "A justice system that produces systematically unequal outcomes by race or wealth fails this floor, regardless of formal neutrality" — this directly repudiates *McCleskey* and fifty years of intent doctrine. It is exactly right and should be preserved and fortified.

3. **Amendment VI's recognition of Indigenous sovereignty** as preexisting and treaty-grounded is legally and historically accurate in a way that no existing federal constitutional provision is.

4. **Amendment IV's bodily autonomy framework** is comprehensive and would, if self-executing, resolve the post-*Dobbs* landscape in favor of reproductive rights, gender-affirming care, and end-of-life autonomy.

5. **The "every person" scope** in the Preamble — extending rights beyond citizenship — reflects a principled and defensible reading of what this government owes to persons within its reach.

None of these strengths excuse the gaps identified above. But they establish that this document has a genuine constitutional architecture worth defending and refining. The adversarial review is in service of making that architecture durable.
