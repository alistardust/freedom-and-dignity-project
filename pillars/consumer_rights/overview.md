# Consumer Rights

**Foundation:** [Real Freedom](../../foundations/real_freedom/values.md)

## Purpose

Protect consumers from deceptive, coercive, and extractive business practices—including hidden fees, predatory subscriptions, software-locked functionality, and systematic denial of the right to repair what you own. This pillar establishes that purchasing a product conveys real ownership, that pricing must be transparent, and that manufacturers may not use software locks, monopoly service chains, or planned obsolescence to extract additional revenue from things people already own.

## Core Principle

**Real ownership means full access to what you bought.** Consumers must be able to understand what they are paying for, cancel what they don't want, repair what breaks, and use their property without corporate gatekeepers extracting rent from functionality they already purchased.

## The Problem It Solves

- Hidden fees, junk fees, and drip pricing cost American consumers an estimated $90 billion annually across hotel, airline, banking, and telecom industries alone—often disclosed only at the final stage of checkout after significant time investment.
- Manufacturers increasingly lock product features behind subscriptions, deny repair access to independent technicians, and void warranties for using third-party parts—converting ownership into perpetual rent dependency.
- Farmers lose critical harvest days when agricultural equipment software locks prevent independent repair, with John Deere's software restrictions creating documented cases where equipment sat idle for weeks awaiting authorized dealer service.
- Mandatory arbitration clauses strip consumers of the ability to seek class-action redress, effectively immunizing corporations from accountability for widespread small-dollar harms that aggregate to billions.

## Design Logic

- Ownership rights rules establish that purchase conveys functional access, and that post-sale software restrictions converting owned features into subscriptions are prohibited.
- Right to repair rules require that manufacturers provide parts, tools, documentation, and diagnostic access to independent repairers and consumers—across vehicles, electronics, farm equipment, appliances, and commercial equipment.
- Transparency and anti-junk-fee rules require full upfront pricing disclosure and prohibit cancellation friction, negative-option renewals, and subscription traps.
- Strong enforcement with private rights of action and meaningful penalties makes these protections real rather than aspirational.

## Key Reform Areas

- Junk fee and hidden fee prohibition — full upfront price disclosure requirements
- Subscription trap rules — easy cancellation through the same medium used to enroll
- Product ownership rights — features present in hardware may not be locked behind subscriptions post-purchase
- Right to repair — vehicles, electronics, farm equipment, appliances, commercial equipment, medical devices
- Agricultural right to repair — time-sensitive repair access for farming operations
- Warranty and durability standards — products must meet quality and lifecycle expectations
- Mandatory arbitration limits and class-action access restoration
- Consumer product quality and safety standards
- Anti-monopoly repair service chain enforcement
- Public procurement preferences for repairable, sustainable products

## Research & Context

The FTC's 2022 report on junk fees documented that hidden and drip-priced fees across hotels, airlines, ticket sales, banking, and telecom represent tens of billions in annual consumer costs, with the average American household paying an estimated $700+ in unavoidable junk fees per year. The FTC's own consumer research shows that mandatory disclosure of total price at first listing substantially reduces the exploitative effect of drip pricing—making the policy fix technically straightforward despite intense industry lobbying against it.

Right-to-repair research by iFixit and the US PIRG Education Fund documents that independent repair creates significant economic value: iFixit estimates that Americans spend $40 billion annually on repairs that could be done for far less with better access to parts and tools. A 2021 FTC report found that manufacturers' restrictions on independent repair harm consumers, raise costs, and create environmental waste without clear safety or quality benefits. The report found that most claimed safety justifications for repair restrictions were pretextual.

Agricultural right-to-repair is among the most documented economic harms: a 2019 investigation by Motherboard/Vice documented dozens of cases where farmers were unable to repair equipment due to software locks, with delays costing thousands of dollars per day during planting and harvest. John Deere's End User License Agreement for purchased tractors explicitly states that buyers only receive a license to operate the machine—not ownership of the software—a position that has generated significant legal challenge and state legislative response.

Mandatory arbitration research by the Economic Policy Institute shows that forced arbitration clauses in consumer contracts effectively strip class action rights from 80% of workers and consumers who are subject to them. A 2018 study by the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau found that class action settlements produced $2.2 billion in relief to 34 million consumers over five years—relief that arbitration bans eliminated for companies that included arbitration clauses.
