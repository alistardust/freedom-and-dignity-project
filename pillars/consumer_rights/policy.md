# Consumer Rights — Policy Rules

## Scope Code: CON / RPR

This document contains all Consumer Rights and Right to Repair policy rules organized by family code. These rules establish comprehensive standards for hidden fees, subscription practices, product ownership, right to repair across all sectors, warranty and durability standards, and enforcement mechanisms.

---

## CON-GEN — General Consumer Protections

### CON-GEN-001 — Prohibit deceptive and exploitative business practices
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Consumer protection law must prohibit deceptive, coercive, exploitative, or structurally unfair business practices even where no single transaction appears individually extreme.
**Notes:** Consumer protection must address systemic unfairness even when individual transactions appear minor. The aggregated effect of widespread deceptive practices constitutes real economic harm.

### CON-GEN-002 — Ban confusion-based and friction-based business models
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Business models that rely on confusion, hidden fees, procedural traps, manipulative interfaces, or intentional friction to extract value are prohibited.
**Notes:** Business models designed around manipulative interfaces or procedural traps extract value through confusion rather than legitimate competition. This rule targets the structural incentive to deceive.

### CON-GEN-003 — Consumers have clear enforceable rights across key areas
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Consumers must have clear rights to understandable pricing, terms, cancellation, repair, redress, and data control.
**Notes:** Clear, enumerable rights to pricing, terms, cancellation, repair, redress, and data control form the foundation of effective consumer protection. Vague protections are effectively unenforceable.

### CON-GEN-004 — Adhesion contracts may not waive fundamental consumer rights
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Contracts of adhesion and take-it-or-leave-it consumer terms may not waive fundamental consumer rights or immunize unlawful business conduct.
**Notes:** Take-it-or-leave-it consumer contracts are not the product of genuine bargaining and cannot eliminate protections consumers could not meaningfully negotiate. This closes the gap between formal and substantive consumer rights.

### CON-GEN-005 — Restrict or ban mandatory arbitration in consumer contracts
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Mandatory arbitration clauses in consumer contracts that prevent meaningful legal recourse should be banned or strictly limited.
**Notes:** Mandatory arbitration clauses prevent class actions and effectively immunize corporations from accountability for widespread small-dollar harms. Restricting them restores meaningful access to legal redress.

### CON-GEN-006 — Fine print cannot substitute for substantive fairness
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Fine-print disclosures may not be used as a substitute for substantive fairness or clear informed consent.
**Notes:** Disclosure requirements lose their consumer-protective function when buried or technically framed in ways no reasonable consumer can act on. Real informed consent requires genuine clarity, not legalistic cover.

## CON-FEE — Fee Transparency and Junk Fees

### CON-FEE-001 — Prohibit hidden fees, drip pricing, and junk fees
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Hidden fees, junk fees, drip pricing, and post-selection fee inflation are prohibited.
**Notes:** Drip pricing and junk fees impose billions in annual costs on consumers by withholding total price until checkout. Prohibition forces honest competition on actual price.

### CON-FEE-002 — Full upfront total price disclosure required
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Total price must be clearly disclosed up front in consumer transactions, including recurring fees, mandatory surcharges, cancellation costs, and renewal terms.
**Notes:** Consumers cannot make rational purchasing decisions without knowing total cost at first presentation. This rule eliminates the practice of revealing fees only after consumers have invested significant time in the transaction.

### CON-FEE-003 — Prohibit confusing billing and negative-option renewal traps
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Businesses may not rely on confusing billing cycles, negative-option renewals, or cancellation friction to retain customers against their intent.
**Notes:** Confusing billing cycles and cancellation friction exploit cognitive load and status quo bias to retain customers against their intent. This rule requires genuine consent rather than manufactured inertia.

### CON-FEE-004 — Easy subscription cancellation through same enrollment medium
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Automatic renewals and subscriptions must be easy to cancel through the same medium used to enroll.
**Notes:** Making cancellation harder than enrollment is a structural trap designed to profit from consumer frustration. This rule requires symmetry between signup and cancellation pathways.

## CON-SUB — Subscription and Cancellation

### CON-SUB-001 — Subscriptions may not replace feasible ownership
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Subscription models may not be structured to replace ownership where ownership remains feasible and the primary purpose is recurring extraction rather than genuine service delivery.
**Notes:** Where product ownership is feasible, subscription models designed primarily for extraction rather than service delivery constitute a transfer of value from consumers to manufacturers. This rule preserves the option of genuine ownership.

### CON-SUB-002 — Disclose ownership versus subscription status clearly
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Consumers must be able to understand when they are buying ownership, license access, temporary use, or subscription dependency.
**Notes:** Consumers must be able to distinguish what they own from what they are renting or licensing. Opacity about the nature of the transaction undermines informed decision-making.

### CON-SUB-003 — No degrading owned products to force subscription conversion
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Businesses may not materially degrade owned products or previously purchased functionality in order to force subscription conversion.
**Notes:** Deliberately degrading previously-owned functionality to force subscription adoption is a form of fraud by design. This rule prohibits using product degradation as a subscription sales tactic.

### CON-SUB-004 — Core device functions may not require subscription
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Essential or core device functionality may not be placed behind recurring subscription paywalls where doing so converts normal ownership into dependency.
**Notes:** Converting essential device functionality into a subscription dependency transforms ownership into perpetual rent. This rule preserves the basic expectation that a purchased device performs its core function.

### CON-SUB-005 — Legitimate ongoing services may use subscription models
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Subscription models are permitted for ongoing services that require continuous external resources, such as connectivity, cloud storage, or live service provision.
**Notes:** Subscription models are appropriate for services requiring continuous external resources such as connectivity, storage, or live content. This rule limits the prohibition to cases where ownership is the reasonable expectation.

### CON-SUB-006 — Disclose owned versus service-based features at point of sale
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** The distinction between owned functionality and service-based functionality must be clearly disclosed at point of sale.
**Notes:** Consumers making purchase decisions need to know at the point of sale which features are owned and which require ongoing payment. Post-purchase discovery of subscription requirements undermines informed consent.

## CON-OWN — Product Ownership Rights

### CON-OWN-001 — Purchase conveys full access to core functionality
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Purchase of a physical product conveys full access to its core functionality, and such functionality may not be restricted behind ongoing subscription fees.
**Notes:** The fundamental expectation of purchase is that you own what you buy. Restricting functionality present at purchase behind recurring fees converts a sale into a subscription without consumer consent.

### CON-OWN-002 — Ownership may not be converted to subscription dependency
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Ownership of a product may not be converted into a subscription dependency through software locks, paywalls, or post-sale feature restrictions.
**Notes:** Software locks, paywalls, and post-sale restrictions that convert owned functionality into rental dependency are a form of retroactive contract modification. This rule ensures ownership means ownership.

## CON-AUTO — Automotive Consumer Rights

### CON-AUTO-002 — Core vehicle functions may not be subscription-locked
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Core vehicle functions, including safety systems, performance capabilities, climate control, and hardware-enabled features, may not be locked behind subscription fees after purchase.
**Notes:** Vehicle safety systems, performance, and hardware-enabled features are part of what consumers pay for when purchasing a vehicle. Post-sale subscription requirements for hardware already present and paid for constitute a fundamental breach of ownership expectations.

### CON-AUTO-003 — Hardware-enabled features at time of sale remain accessible
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Features physically present and enabled by hardware at the time of sale must remain accessible without recurring payment.
**Notes:** Features enabled by hardware at time of sale must remain accessible without recurring payment. This prevents manufacturers from artificially degrading paid-for vehicle capabilities to create subscription revenue.

## CON-ELC — Electronics Consumer Rights

### CON-ELC-002 — Computing hardware performance may not be subscription-gated
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Core computing hardware performance, including processing power, storage access, and device capabilities, may not be artificially restricted behind subscription models.
**Notes:** Artificially restricting processing power, storage, or device capabilities behind subscription models converts owned hardware into a rental. This prevents manufacturers from throttling paid-for capabilities to create upsell pressure.

### CON-ELC-003 — Basic device functions may not require subscriptions
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Devices such as printers, computers, and consumer electronics may not require subscriptions to perform basic functions for which the hardware is capable.
**Notes:** Devices must perform the basic functions their hardware supports without requiring a subscription. Post-purchase subscription gates on basic functionality undermine the premise of device ownership.

## CON-CNS — Consumer Safety

### CON-CNS-001 — No required proprietary consumables where alternatives are feasible
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Products may not require proprietary consumables or subscription-based supply systems where compatible alternatives are feasible.
**Notes:** Requiring proprietary consumables where compatible alternatives exist forces consumers into recurring extraction arrangements tied to the initial purchase. This rule protects competitive consumable markets.

### CON-CNS-002 — No degrading functionality when third-party consumables are used
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Manufacturers may not disable, degrade, or restrict product functionality based on use of third-party consumables.
**Notes:** Manufacturers may not use software to detect and penalize third-party consumables by degrading product functionality. Such practices are designed to eliminate competition rather than protect product quality.

## CON-FTR — Feature Locking and Post-Sale Restrictions

### CON-FTR-001 — May not artificially disable available hardware features
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Manufacturers may not artificially disable or withhold functionality that is technically available in hardware solely to create paid upgrade tiers.
**Notes:** When hardware can perform a function, software locks that hide that capability solely to create paid tiers are artificial restrictions without legitimate technical justification. This rule prohibits monetizing capability that consumers already own.

### CON-FTR-002 — Software feature restrictions require legitimate justification
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Software-based feature restrictions must be justified by legitimate technical, safety, or service-related needs and may not be used purely for monetization.
**Notes:** Software-based restrictions must be grounded in technical, safety, or genuine service requirements—not pure monetization. This standard requires manufacturers to demonstrate legitimate purpose rather than simply assert it.

## CON-TRN — Transparency Requirements

### CON-TRN-004 — Standardized disclosure of included versus paid features
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Products must clearly disclose which features are included at purchase and which require ongoing payment, in a standardized and understandable format.
**Notes:** Consumers need clear, standardized information about which product features are included at purchase and which require ongoing payment. Standardization prevents comparison-shopping from being obscured by differing disclosure formats.

### CON-TRN-005 — Post-sale paywalling of previously included features prohibited
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Post-sale changes that move previously included features behind paywalls are prohibited.
**Notes:** Moving features that were previously included at purchase behind new paywalls is a retroactive change to the terms of sale. This rule protects consumers from having purchased capabilities taken away after the fact.

## CON-ENF — Enforcement

### CON-ENF-001 — Violations require restoration, restitution, and penalties
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Violations of ownership-based functionality rules must result in mandatory feature restoration, consumer restitution, and penalties.
**Notes:** Meaningful enforcement requires mandatory remediation, not just cessation. Requiring feature restoration, consumer restitution, and financial penalties creates real deterrence against violations.

### CON-ENF-002 — Private right of action for unlawful post-purchase restrictions
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Consumers must have a private right of action where product functionality is unlawfully restricted after purchase.
**Notes:** Consumers must be able to individually enforce their ownership rights rather than depending entirely on regulatory action. Private rights of action create distributed enforcement and stronger deterrence.

## CON-QLT — Quality Standards

### CON-QLT-001 — Products must meet minimum durability and quality standards
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Consumer products must meet minimum durability, safety, and quality standards appropriate to their category and expected use.
**Notes:** Baseline quality standards ensure that consumer products perform their intended function for a reasonable period. Without minimum standards, manufacturers have market incentives to externalize product failure costs onto consumers.

### CON-QLT-002 — Marketing claims must match actual product quality
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Businesses may not market products as durable, premium, sustainable, or high-quality where design and materials systematically contradict those claims.
**Notes:** Marketing products as premium or sustainable when design and materials contradict those claims is a form of deceptive trade practice. This rule requires honest quality representation.

### CON-QLT-003 — Systematic low-quality manufacturing practices subject to review
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Repeated category-wide low-quality manufacturing practices must be subject to investigation, standards updates, and corrective action.
**Notes:** Industry-wide patterns of inadequate quality require systemic regulatory response rather than case-by-case enforcement. Category-level investigation authority prevents manufacturers from treating product failures as acceptable business losses.

### CON-QLT-004 — Essential household goods need stronger baseline quality standards
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Essential household goods, appliances, and core consumer devices should be subject to stronger baseline quality and repairability standards because failure imposes broader social costs.
**Notes:** Essential goods like appliances impose social costs when they fail because households depend on them. Higher baseline standards for these categories reflect the broader public interest in reliable essential products.

## CON-WAR — Warranty Standards

### CON-WAR-001 — Warranties must be understandable, fair, and enforceable
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Warranty systems must be understandable, fair, and enforceable and may not rely on procedural traps or vague exclusions to avoid responsibility.
**Notes:** Warranties that rely on procedural complexity or vague exclusions to avoid liability do not provide the consumer protection their name implies. This rule requires warranties to function as genuine commitments.

### CON-WAR-002 — Manufacturers must disclose support timelines at point of sale
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Manufacturers must provide clear support timelines, update commitments, and serviceability expectations at point of sale.
**Notes:** Consumers making durable purchases need to know how long manufacturers will support the product with parts, updates, and service information. Disclosure at point of sale enables informed purchasing decisions.

### CON-WAR-003 — Early support withdrawal must trigger remedies
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Support withdrawal that materially undermines product function before the end of a reasonable lifecycle must trigger remedies.
**Notes:** When manufacturers withdraw support before the end of a reasonable product lifecycle, they materially undermine the value consumers received. Remedies ensure manufacturers internalize the costs of premature obsolescence.

## RPR-SYS — System Principles

### RPR-SYS-001 — Right to repair owned products without unreasonable restriction
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Individuals and independent service providers have the right to repair, maintain, modify, and restore legally owned products without unreasonable restriction.
**Notes:** The foundational principle of right-to-repair is that legal ownership includes the right to maintain and modify. This establishes the baseline from which all more specific repair rights flow.

### RPR-SYS-002 — Manufacturers must provide repair access at fair and reasonable terms
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Manufacturers must provide access to parts, tools, documentation, firmware, and diagnostic information necessary for repair at fair and reasonable terms.
**Notes:** Access to parts, tools, and documentation at fair terms is a prerequisite for meaningful right-to-repair. Without this, theoretical repair rights are practically unenforceable.

### RPR-SYS-003 — Products may not be designed to prevent repair
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Products may not be designed to prevent or unreasonably restrict repair, including through proprietary locks, software restrictions, or unavailable components.
**Notes:** Design-for-obstruction—whether through proprietary locks, unavailable components, or software restrictions—defeats repair rights before they can be exercised. This rule closes the design-around loophole.

## RPR-DES — Design for Repairability

### RPR-DES-001 — Products must be designed for reasonable disassembly
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Products must be designed for reasonable disassembly, component replacement, and maintenance using accessible tools and methods.
**Notes:** Repairability by design ensures that repair rights can be exercised in practice. Products that cannot be disassembled without specialized proprietary tools make repair rights theoretical rather than real.

### RPR-DES-002 — Wear components must be individually replaceable
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Critical components subject to wear must be replaceable without requiring full system replacement.
**Notes:** If critical wear components require full system replacement, the cost of repair approaches the cost of replacement, defeating repairability. Component-level replaceability is essential for practical repair access.

### RPR-DES-003 — Manufacturers must publish repairability scores
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Manufacturers must publish repairability scores or equivalent transparency metrics for products.
**Notes:** Standardized repairability transparency allows consumers to factor repairability into purchase decisions and creates market incentives for better design. France's repairability index provides an existing policy model.

## RPR-LIFE — Product Lifecycle

### RPR-LIFE-001 — Products must meet minimum durability standards by category
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Products must meet minimum durability and lifecycle standards appropriate to their category and intended use.
**Notes:** Category-appropriate lifecycle standards create a floor below which premature failure becomes a legal and regulatory problem. This shifts the economic incentive from planned obsolescence toward durable design.

### RPR-LIFE-002 — Manufacturers must support products for a minimum period
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Manufacturers must support products with parts, updates, and service information for a defined minimum period.
**Notes:** Products must remain serviceable for a defined minimum period after sale. Without support minimums, manufacturers can abandon products shortly after sale, stranding consumers without parts or service information.

### RPR-LIFE-003 — Artificial lifespan limitation through software is prohibited
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Artificial limitation of product lifespan through software, firmware, or design choices intended to force replacement is prohibited.
**Notes:** Using software or firmware to deliberately limit product lifespan to force replacement is planned obsolescence by design. This rule prohibits using digital controls to manufacture scarcity in otherwise functional products.

## RPR-AUTO — Automotive Right to Repair

### RPR-AUTO-001 — Vehicles must provide independent diagnostic and repair access
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Vehicle manufacturers must provide independent access to diagnostic systems, repair data, and parts without requiring dealer-only tools or authorization.
**Notes:** Independent access to diagnostic systems and repair data is a prerequisite for competitive repair markets. Dealer-only tools or authorization requirements create effective repair monopolies that raise costs and limit access.

### RPR-AUTO-002 — Repair rights apply to all vehicles including agricultural and industrial
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Right-to-repair protections apply to all vehicles, including agricultural, industrial, and commercial equipment such as tractors, combines, and heavy machinery.
**Notes:** Right-to-repair protections cover the full range of vehicles including agricultural, industrial, and commercial equipment. Gaps in coverage create exemptions that manufacturers actively exploit.

## RPR-ELC — Electronics Right to Repair

### RPR-ELC-001 — Consumer electronics must allow component-level repair
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Consumer electronics manufacturers must allow component-level repair and replacement, including storage, memory, batteries, and displays.
**Notes:** Component-level repair of storage, memory, batteries, and displays is more sustainable and cost-effective than full device replacement. Manufacturers that make component repair impossible create unnecessary waste and consumer cost.

## RPR-ENF — Right to Repair Enforcement

### RPR-ENF-001 — Regulators may compel access to repair materials
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Regulators must have authority to compel access to parts, tools, manuals, schematics, firmware, and diagnostic systems where repair rights apply.
**Notes:** Enforcement authority that can compel access to parts, tools, and documentation ensures that right-to-repair obligations are not simply ignored. Without compulsion authority, manufacturers can delay compliance indefinitely.

### RPR-ENF-002 — Violations carry penalties, private right of action, and injunction
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Violations of right-to-repair obligations must carry meaningful penalties, private rights of action, and injunctive relief.
**Notes:** Meaningful penalties, private rights of action, and injunctive relief create real deterrence and distributed enforcement. Weak penalties make non-compliance an acceptable cost of doing business.

### RPR-ENF-003 — Safety claims blocking repair must be specifically demonstrated
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Manufacturers may not use safety or cybersecurity claims pretextually to block lawful repair; such claims must be specifically demonstrated and narrowly tailored.
**Notes:** Safety and cybersecurity justifications for repair restrictions must be specifically demonstrated, not asserted. Pretextual safety claims have been the primary mechanism by which manufacturers block repair rights in practice.

### RPR-ENF-004 — Repair standards cover vehicles, electronics, farm, medical devices
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Product categories subject to right-to-repair standards should include vehicles, farm equipment, appliances, consumer electronics, medical devices where safe, computing devices, and home systems.
**Notes:** The covered product categories are defined broadly to prevent manufacturers from arguing their product type falls outside right-to-repair requirements. Medical device coverage is conditioned on safety considerations.

### RPR-ENF-005 — Public procurement favors repairable and sustainable products
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Public procurement should favor products with high repairability, long support windows, open standards, and low lifecycle waste.
**Notes:** Government purchasing power can drive market adoption of repairable design. Procurement preferences create economic incentives for manufacturers even before mandatory standards are fully established.

## RPR-AGR — Agricultural Right to Repair

### RPR-AGR-001 — Full diagnostic access required for agricultural equipment
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Manufacturers of agricultural and industrial equipment must provide full access to diagnostic tools, software, firmware interfaces, and error codes necessary for repair and maintenance.
**Notes:** Agricultural equipment repair requires access to the same diagnostic systems, firmware, and error codes that authorized dealers use. Without this access, independent repair is impossible regardless of mechanical skill.

### RPR-AGR-002 — Dealer-only repair access restriction is prohibited
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Access to repair diagnostics and service systems may not be restricted to authorized dealers where such restriction limits repair, increases cost, or causes operational delays.
**Notes:** Restricting repair diagnostics to authorized dealers creates monopoly service conditions that increase cost and cause delay. Independent repair access is essential for competitive service markets in agriculture.

### RPR-AGR-003 — Software locks may not prevent lawful equipment repair
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Software locks, firmware restrictions, or digital controls may not prevent lawful repair, part replacement, or modification of owned equipment.
**Notes:** Software and firmware locks on agricultural equipment convert what would otherwise be straightforward mechanical repair into an unauthorized act. This rule re-establishes that ownership includes the right to repair.

### RPR-AGR-004 — Unauthorized repair may not trigger equipment degradation
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Manufacturers may not disable or degrade equipment functionality due to unauthorized repair or use of third-party parts unless a specific and demonstrable safety risk exists.
**Notes:** Manufacturers may not disable equipment functionality as a response to unauthorized repair or use of third-party parts unless a specific safety risk is demonstrable. Punitive functionality removal is a form of sabotage of owned property.

### RPR-AGR-005 — Time-sensitive equipment must be repairable without unreasonable delay
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Equipment required for time-sensitive operations, including agricultural machinery, must be repairable without unreasonable delay caused by manufacturer restrictions or service monopolies.
**Notes:** Agricultural machinery used during planting and harvest must be repairable within the timeframe those operations require. Manufacturer-imposed delays during critical periods constitute direct economic harm.

### RPR-AGR-006 — Prevent repair bottlenecks during critical agricultural periods
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Policies must prevent repair bottlenecks that cause economic loss due to inability to operate equipment during critical periods such as planting or harvest.
**Notes:** Policies must specifically address the time-sensitive nature of agricultural repair needs. A repair monopoly that causes minor delays in normal circumstances becomes catastrophic during planting or harvest.

### RPR-AGR-007 — Parts, tools, and manuals available at fair and reasonable terms
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Manufacturers must provide access to replacement parts, tools, service manuals, and repair documentation for agricultural and industrial equipment at fair and reasonable terms.
**Notes:** Physical access to parts and documentation at reasonable prices is a prerequisite for right-to-repair to have practical meaning. Excessive pricing on parts or documentation is a repair restriction through pricing rather than prohibition.

### RPR-AGR-008 — Equipment ownership may not be reduced to a license
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Ownership of agricultural and industrial equipment may not be reduced to a limited license through software terms that restrict repair, modification, or normal use.
**Notes:** End-user license agreements that purport to grant only a license to operate—not ownership of—purchased equipment represent a legal fiction that courts and policy must reject. Ownership means ownership, not a conditional license.

### RPR-AGR-009 — Contractual repair restrictions on owned equipment are unenforceable
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Contractual terms that restrict lawful repair or modification of owned equipment are unenforceable.
**Notes:** Contractual terms that purport to restrict lawful repair or modification of owned equipment are contrary to public policy. Manufacturers may not use contract terms to contract away ownership rights recognized in law.

### RPR-AGR-010 — No exclusive repair market control through technical restrictions
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Manufacturers may not maintain exclusive control over repair markets for essential equipment through technical restrictions, contractual limitations, or service monopolies.
**Notes:** Technical restrictions, contractual limitations, and service monopolies that collectively give manufacturers exclusive control over repair markets violate competition principles. Essential equipment repair markets must remain open.

### RPR-AGR-011 — Agricultural repair violations trigger accelerated enforcement
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Violations of agricultural right-to-repair rules must trigger accelerated enforcement, including injunctive relief, penalties, and mandated access restoration due to the time-sensitive nature of equipment use.
**Notes:** The time-sensitive nature of agricultural operations means standard enforcement timelines are inadequate. Accelerated enforcement including injunctive relief is necessary to prevent catastrophic losses during critical planting and harvest periods.

## RPR-COM — Commercial Equipment Right to Repair

### RPR-COM-001 — Repair rights apply fully to commercial equipment
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Right-to-repair protections apply fully to commercial equipment used in food service, retail, hospitality, and similar industries.
**Notes:** Commercial equipment failures impose direct business costs and operational disruption. Full right-to-repair coverage for commercial equipment prevents the same repair monopoly harms that affect agricultural and consumer equipment.

### RPR-COM-002 — Manufacturers may not restrict diagnostic information access
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Manufacturers may not restrict access to diagnostic information, error codes, or service modes necessary to identify and repair equipment failures.
**Notes:** Access to diagnostic information is prerequisite for independent repair. Restricting error codes and service modes to authorized technicians creates a diagnostic monopoly that forecloses independent repair before it begins.

### RPR-COM-003 — Error systems may not obscure faults or mislead operators
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Error systems may not be designed to obscure faults, mislead operators, or require proprietary intervention where repair is otherwise feasible.
**Notes:** Error systems designed to be opaque or misleading extend repair monopolies by making independent diagnosis impossible. This rule requires that error reporting be designed to enable repair, not obstruct it.

### RPR-COM-004 — Authorized-only repair restrictions are prohibited where access is limited
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Manufacturers may not require that repairs be performed exclusively by authorized service providers where such restriction limits access, increases cost, or creates unreasonable downtime.
**Notes:** Exclusive authorized repair requirements create service monopolies that raise costs and limit access for commercial operators. Where such restrictions limit access or increase downtime, they are prohibited.

### RPR-COM-005 — Software-enforced service monopolies are prohibited
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Service monopolies enforced through software locks, contractual terms, or technical barriers are prohibited.
**Notes:** Service monopolies created through software locks, contracts, or technical barriers foreclose competitive repair markets by design. Prohibition restores the competitive repair markets that benefit both operators and independent technicians.

### RPR-COM-006 — Equipment design may not predictably cause extended repair-related downtime
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Equipment design may not intentionally or predictably cause extended downtime due to restricted repair access, particularly where such downtime impacts business viability.
**Notes:** When equipment design predictably causes extended downtime through restricted repair access, the design choice is effectively an economic attack on operators. This rule prohibits using design to manufacture dependency.

### RPR-COM-007 — Recurring failure states due to design lockout subject to review
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Systems that generate recurring service calls or failure states due to design complexity or lockout mechanisms must be subject to review and corrective action.
**Notes:** Equipment that repeatedly fails due to design complexity or lockout mechanisms—rather than wear—is designed for service dependency. Regulatory review authority addresses patterns of design-driven failure.

### RPR-COM-008 — Manufacturers may not obscure repair pathways or conceal faults
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Manufacturers may not design systems that intentionally obscure repair pathways, conceal faults, or prevent independent troubleshooting.
**Notes:** Systems designed to conceal faults or obstruct independent troubleshooting extend repair monopolies through information asymmetry. This rule requires that design support repair rather than obstruct it.

### RPR-COM-009 — Third-party repair tools and interfaces may not be blocked
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Third-party tools, diagnostic devices, and repair interfaces may not be restricted, blocked, or penalized where they enable lawful maintenance and repair.
**Notes:** Blocking third-party repair tools and diagnostic devices forecloses independent repair even when parts and documentation are technically available. This rule protects the ecosystem of tools that makes independent repair possible.

### RPR-COM-010 — Manufacturers may not interfere with third-party repair solutions
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Manufacturers may not disable or interfere with third-party repair solutions unless a specific, demonstrable safety or security risk exists.
**Notes:** Active interference with third-party repair solutions is a form of sabotage of independent repair markets. The safety or security exception requires specific demonstration, not mere assertion, to prevent pretextual blocking.

## RPR-ANTI — Anti-Restriction Principles

### RPR-ANTI-001 — No restriction of repair to authorized service networks
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Manufacturers may not restrict repair to authorized service networks where such restriction limits competition, increases cost, or reduces access.
**Notes:** Manufacturer restrictions limiting repair to authorized service networks create market foreclosure that harms independent technicians and consumers. This rule requires competitive repair access across all covered product categories.

### RPR-ANTI-002 — DRM and component pairing systems blocking repair are prohibited
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Use of digital rights management, software locks, or pairing systems to block repair or replacement of components is prohibited unless strictly necessary for safety or security and subject to oversight.
**Notes:** Digital rights management and component pairing systems used to block repair are prohibited unless strictly necessary for safety or security with regulatory oversight. This closes the technical loophole most commonly used to block repair.

### RPR-ANTI-003 — Third-party repair may not void warranty unless causally linked
**Status:** INCLUDED
**Statement:** Warranty terms may not be voided solely due to third-party or self-repair unless the repair directly caused the defect.
**Notes:** Warranties may not be voided simply because a consumer used independent repair. Only repair that directly caused the defect at issue can justify warranty denial, consistent with the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act principle.
