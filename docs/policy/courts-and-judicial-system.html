Purpose

Define how courts operate, how judges are selected and constrained, and how interpretation of law and rights is governed, ensuring the judiciary remains independent, accountable, ethically bound, and structurally designed to uphold rights rather than undermine them.

Core Principle

The judiciary must be independent, accountable, ethically bound, and structurally designed to uphold rights, not undermine them. Courts exist to protect constitutional principles and individual rights, not to impose ideological preferences or partisan outcomes.

The Problem It Solves

The federal judiciary, particularly the Supreme Court, has accumulated immense power without corresponding accountability mechanisms. Lifetime appointments create incentives to appoint the youngest possible ideologues who will shape law for 30-40 years. No binding ethics code governs Supreme Court justices, enabling conflicts of interest, undisclosed gifts, political activities, and spouse involvement in cases without consequence[1]. Interpretive methodologies like strict textualism are weaponized to ignore legislative intent and dismantle established rights. Recent Supreme Court decisions, overturning Roe v. Wade in Dobbs, granting broad presidential immunity, gutting the Voting Rights Act, eliminating affirmative action, expanding gun rights while restricting regulatory authority, demonstrate a court willing to overturn precedent and impose ideological outcomes with sweeping consequences[2]. Venue shopping allows plaintiffs to select sympathetic judges for nationwide injunctions. Recusal standards are voluntary and unenforceable, with justices deciding their own conflicts. The result is a judiciary that operates more as a partisan super-legislature than an independent arbiter of law.

Design Logic: How These Positions Work Together

This policy area addresses judicial dysfunction through four mechanisms: structural reform (term limits to end lifetime appointments), ethical enforcement (binding codes with real penalties), interpretive standards (requiring consideration of intent and context, not just text), and procedural improvements (venue reform, mandatory recusal standards, transparency).

Term limits for Supreme Court justices (18-year staggered terms frequently discussed) solve multiple problems simultaneously: eliminate strategic retirement timing, reduce confirmation stakes, ensure regular turnover that reflects democratic change, discourage appointing extremists who will serve for decades, and address age-related decline without relying on voluntary retirement. The same logic applies to lower federal judges, though implementation details differ.

Ethics reform establishes binding codes applicable to all federal judges including Supreme Court justices. Current "ethics standards" for the Supreme Court are voluntary, unenforceable, and routinely ignored. Binding codes would cover: financial conflicts and disclosure, gifts and outside income, political activities and public statements, recusal requirements, spouse and family involvement in cases before the court, transparency of financial arrangements, and enforcement mechanisms with real consequences (censure, removal from cases, potential impeachment for egregious violations).

Interpretive standards address the weaponization of textualism to ignore legislative intent. Strict textualism claims to interpret only the text as written, ignoring context, purpose, or consequences. In practice, this becomes a tool to undermine laws by reading them in the most narrow, literal way regardless of obvious legislative intent. The reform requires courts to consider intent, context, and purpose alongside text, not ignoring text, but not weaponizing selective literalism to subvert clear meaning. It also encourages Congress to include plain-language intent statements in legislation, creating an explicit interpretive record.

Important constitutional limitation: Congress's power to prescribe interpretive rules is strongest for statutory interpretation: courts have long followed Congressional guidance on how to read statutes (see the Dictionary Act, 1 U.S.C. § 1). However, Congress cannot mandate how courts interpret the Constitution itself: that falls within the exclusive domain of the judicial branch under Article III. JUD-INT-001 and JUD-INT-002 as written apply to statutory interpretation, which is within Congress's legitimate authority. JUD-INT-003 (requiring Congress to include plain-language intent statements in bills) is the most constitutionally sound approach and is independently valuable regardless of any mandate on courts. The platform does not claim Congress can overrule constitutional interpretation, it asserts only that courts interpreting statutes should not weaponize a single methodology to subvert the clear purpose of laws Congress enacted.

Venue reform prevents judge shopping. Current rules allow plaintiffs to file cases in any district with minimal connection, enabling selection of single sympathetic judges (often ideologically aligned or appointed by same party) who then issue nationwide injunctions affecting the entire country based on one judge's view. Reform would require substantive local connection for venue, limit or eliminate nationwide injunctions from single district judges, or require multi-judge panels for cases with national scope.

Recusal standards make conflict-of-interest rules mandatory and enforceable. Currently justices decide their own recusal with no review or consequence for refusing. Reform would establish clear, mandatory recusal triggers (financial interest, family involvement, prior participation in case, public statements indicating prejudgment) with independent review mechanisms and enforcement.

Rules are organized by family: ethics (ETH) for conduct and accountability codes, integrity (INT) for interpretive standards ensuring legal reasoning serves justice rather than ideology, oversight (OVR) for mechanisms ensuring accountability, records (REC) for transparency and recusal documentation, venue (VEN) for procedural reforms preventing judge shopping and requiring random case assignment, size (SIZ) for court structure reform including term limits and lower court expansion, nomination (NOM) for confirmation process reforms, and shadow docket (SHD) for emergency procedure and nationwide injunction constraints.

CORT-ETHL-0001

Supreme Court term limits

This position requires a constitutional amendment to establish term limits for Supreme Court justices. Term limits would prevent justices from serving for 30 or 40 years and ensure regular, predictable turnover on the Court.

Supreme Court justices shall serve 18-year staggered active terms, after which they shall assume senior status and may sit on lower federal courts; a new appointment shall occur every two years, providing each presidential term with two appointments; justices currently serving at the time of enactment shall be grandfathered under existing life tenure with the new system applying to all subsequent appointments; implementation requires either a constitutional amendment or, if structured as a passive senior-status assignment system, may be achievable by statute, though the constitutional amendment path is the only certain route and the one this platform endorses.

Establish term limits for Supreme Court justices through constitutional amendment, the intended vehicle, not a workaround. A constitutional amendment is the right approach for the same structural reason the Sixteenth Amendment was needed to authorize income taxation: it settles the question permanently, beyond legal challenge and beyond simple-majority statutory reversal. This is a feature of the platform, not a gap to be explained away. Multiple specific term lengths have been seriously proposed and have scholarly and legislative support: 12-year terms (advocated by some as sufficient for regular turnover without sacrificing continuity), 15-year terms (proposed as a middle ground), and 18-year staggered terms (the most widely circulated proposal, providing two appointments per presidential term). The 18-year figure is not uniquely established as correct, it reflects a judgment that two appointments per term creates predictable rotation without churn so rapid that continuity suffers. Scholars Steven Calabresi and James Lindgren's empirical study concluded that average Supreme Court tenure had grown dramatically over the 20th century and that staggered 18-year terms would produce a well-functioning, regularly renewed court.[5] The Supreme Court Tenure Establishment and Retirement Modernization Act (TERM Act), introduced in multiple Congresses with broad co-sponsorship, proposes 18-year terms with senior-status rotation to other federal courts thereafter. Fix the Court, the judicial reform advocacy organization, has documented the escalating confirmation stakes of lifetime appointments and actively advocates for term limits as a structural fix.[7] Each presidential term would include two Supreme Court appointments, creating predictable and regular turnover. This eliminates strategic retirement timing, reduces confirmation stakes, ensures court composition gradually reflects democratic change, and addresses age-related decline without relying on voluntary retirement. Appointments remain secure (18 years exceeds most legal careers) while preventing single presidents from dominating the court for generations. May extend to lower federal court judges with different term lengths appropriate to those positions. [ADVERSARIAL] Constitutional ambiguity: Article III’s “good behavior” tenure clause may not permit Congress to impose term limits by statute, the senior-status workaround argued by Calabresi and Lindgren has never been adjudicated, and a constitutional amendment is the only certain path. Escalation risk: if one party controls both the presidency and Congress when implementation begins, they could structure the staggered schedule to create disproportionate vacancies under a favorable president; the schedule must be established in law with fixed dates independent of the implementing Congress’s partisan advantage. Grandfathering inequality: current justices grandfathered under life tenure while new justices serve 18-year terms creates a two-tier court during the transition period; justices who would have been replaced under the new system have no incentive to step aside. Strategic behavior: justices approaching the end of their active term may shift decisional calculus knowing their replacement is imminent, or senior-status justices may seek to influence outcomes through selective availability for lower-court assignments. Threshold uncertainty: 18 years is the most widely proposed figure but has no uniquely principled basis over 15 or 20 years; the term length should be assessed against the actual goal of regular, predictable turnover.

CORT-ETHL-0002

Judicial ethics code

This position creates a binding ethics code for all federal judges, including Supreme Court justices. The code would cover financial conflicts, gifts, political activities, and recusal requirements, with real enforcement mechanisms to hold judges accountable for ethical violations.

All federal judges, including Supreme Court justices, shall be bound by a mandatory and enforceable Code of Judicial Conduct covering financial conflicts, gift acceptance, outside income, political activity, recusal obligations, and disclosure of spouse and family member interests in matters before the court; the code shall be enforced by an independent investigative body with authority to compel recusal, impose censure, and refer egregious violations to Congress for impeachment consideration; voluntary self-enforcement is prohibited.

Establish binding, enforceable code of ethics applicable to all federal judges including Supreme Court justices. Must cover: financial conflicts and mandatory disclosure, gifts and outside income from interested parties, political activities and public statements, recusal requirements for conflicts, spouse and family involvement in cases, transparency of financial arrangements, and enforcement mechanisms with real consequences. Currently Supreme Court operates under voluntary guidance without enforcement, resulting in undisclosed gifts, financial conflicts, family member political activity in related cases, and refusal to recuse without consequence[1]. Binding code establishes baseline ethical standards comparable to those governing all other public officials and federal judges, with enforcement through ethics investigations, public censure, removal from specific cases, and potential impeachment for egregious violations. [ADVERSARIAL] Enforcement body independence: any investigative office for Supreme Court conduct faces the structural problem of who appoints the investigators, appointment by the Judicial Conference, Congress, or the President each creates different capture risks. Impeachment as backstop: the only formal mechanism for removing a federal judge is impeachment, a political process; an ethics code without a meaningful removal mechanism beyond impeachment provides accountability in name but not always in practice. Definition gaps: the code must specify financial thresholds with precision, structuring gifts as a series of small transactions is already a documented pattern of evasion. Scope of family restrictions: the code must define which family relationships trigger review without being so broad as to chill family members’ independent professional activities unrelated to the justice’s docket. Chilling effect: justices who feel targeted by ethics complaints may become adversarial toward the oversight institution, complicating the structural relationship between Congress and the Court.

CORT-ETHL-0003

Establish an independent Judicial Conduct Commission with binding investigative and enforcement authority over all federal judges, including Supreme Court justices

This position establishes an independent commission with the power to investigate and enforce ethics rules for all federal judges, including Supreme Court justices. The commission would ensure judges cannot simply ignore ethics violations without consequences.

This platform supports the Supreme Court Ethics, Recusal, and Transparency Act (S. 359, 118th Congress) as a minimum floor and further supports creation of a fully independent Judicial Conduct Commission with: (a) investigative authority independent of the Judicial Conference, with subpoena power to compel financial records from third parties; (b) mandatory recusal authority -- the power to order a justice's recusal from specific cases when documented financial conflicts exist; (c) authority to refer egregious, documented violations to Congress for impeachment consideration with a written public report; and (d) criminal referral authority to the Department of Justice for willful non-disclosure of financial interests required by federal statute. Article III independence protects judicial decisions from political interference; it does not grant immunity from accountability for financial corruption.

(2023) (would require the Judicial Conference to create a complaints mechanism and binding code covering all federal judges -- this position supports S. 359 as a floor and goes further); 28 U.S.C. § 455 (requiring federal judges to disqualify themselves in proceedings where their impartiality might reasonably be questioned -- currently unenforceable against Supreme Court justices who self-certify recusal decisions); 5 U.S.C. app. 4 (Ethics in Government Act, requiring financial disclosure -- insufficient as applied to SCOTUS given documented non-compliance). Enforcement mechanisms: Subpoena power to compel financial records from third parties -- enables investigation independent of justice cooperation; mandatory recusal authority -- creates a remedy other than impeachment for individual conflicts; Congressional referral with written public report -- creates a formal accountability record that enables legislative follow-up; DOJ criminal referral authority -- provides criminal enforcement pathway for willful non-disclosure. PolicyOS compliance: KERN-0001 (an ethics enforcement body that depends on voluntary compliance by the institution it oversees provides no meaningful accountability -- independent investigative authority and enforcement are floor requirements); KERN-0004 (a Supreme Court whose justices accept undisclosed financial benefits from parties with interests before the Court cannot be described as accountable to democratic processes rather than to their benefactors). Unintended consequence -- an independent Judicial Conduct Commission with subpoena authority could be weaponized for political harassment of justices by an opposition Congress; the statute must require supermajority approval from the Commission itself before issuing subpoenas, and limit investigation scope to financial and recusal matters. Abuse path -- a politically motivated Commission could time investigations and referrals to maximize damage during confirmation fights or major case deliberations; the statute must specify that pending Commission investigations and referrals are not publicly disclosed until resolved, except where the relevant case has already been decided.

CORT-ETHL-0004

Mandatory financial disclosure for Supreme Court justices equivalent to STOCK Act standards, covering all hospitality, travel, gifts, real estate transactions, and outside income

This position requires Supreme Court justices to publicly disclose all gifts, travel, hospitality, real estate deals, and outside income using the same standards that apply to members of Congress. Transparency helps the public identify conflicts of interest.

Mandatory disclosure covering the following categories must apply to all Supreme Court justices by statute, filed annually within 30 days of year-end and posted publicly: (a) all travel funded by any non-governmental person or entity, including transportation, lodging, and meals, with aggregate value above $50; (b) all gifts from any single source with combined annual value exceeding $50; (c) all real estate purchases, sales, transfers, or refinancings, including transactions involving immediate family members' property in which the justice has any beneficial interest; (d) all speaking fees, book advances and royalties, teaching honoraria, and consulting income; (e) all paid memberships in organizations or educational programs. Material omission or non-disclosure constitutes a federal ethics violation subject to civil penalty and independent investigation. The STOCK Act's enforcement infrastructure provides a ready model; a SCOTUS disclosure statute must cross-reference STOCK Act enforcement mechanisms and apply the same penalties for willful non-compliance.

Legal basis: Stop Trading on Congressional Knowledge Act of 2012, Pub. L. No. 112-105, 126 Stat. 291 (the existing framework for financial disclosure by members of Congress and senior executive branch officials -- this rule extends comparable standards to SCOTUS justices who are currently exempt from equivalent requirements); Ethics in Government Act of 1978, 5 U.S.C. app. 4 (requiring annual financial disclosure reports from federal judges, but with historical disclosure gaps that did not cover third-party-funded hospitality, travel to speaking engagements, real estate transactions involving family members, or speaking fees from institutions with interests before the Court); 28 U.S.C. § 455 (recusal obligations for federal judges -- financial disclosure enables parties and courts to identify conflicts triggering this statute). Empirical evidence: ProPublica's multi-year investigative series documented that the gap between what the law requires and what justices accepted without disclosure runs to tens of millions of dollars across multiple justices over two decades; Kaplan, J., Elliott, J., & Mierjeski, A. (2023, April 6). Clarence Thomas and the billionaire. ProPublica; Fix the Court has catalogued undisclosed benefits, travel, gifts, and outside income across multiple current justices at https://fixthecourt.com/fix/financial-disclosure/; the STOCK Act reporting infrastructure -- requiring transaction disclosure within 30 days -- demonstrates that comparable real-time disclosure is operationally feasible for the executive and legislative branches and should be extended to the judicial branch. Enforcement mechanisms: Annual statutory filing requirement -- creates a paper trail for comparison against independent reporting of financial benefits received; civil penalty for material omission or non-disclosure -- provides financial deterrence; independent investigation authority -- removes self-enforcement; STOCK Act cross-reference -- incorporates an existing, tested enforcement framework. PolicyOS compliance: KERN-0001 (meaningful financial disclosure is a floor requirement for public trust in the judiciary -- without it, documented patterns of undisclosed financial relationships cannot be investigated or remedied); KERN-0004 (financial transparency for unelected lifetime appointees who exercise enormous power over constitutional questions is an accountability floor for any system claiming democratic legitimacy). Adversarial review: Gap -- the $50 aggregate threshold for gifts and travel may be too low to be practically enforceable across all speaking invitations and minor hospitality, creating an administrative burden that could become a compliance excuse; alternatively it may be too high to capture patterns of small, systematic benefits; the threshold should be set with reference to what level of benefit has demonstrated capacity to create documented conflicts of interest, not as an arbitrary bright line. Loophole -- justices could avoid disclosure by having benefits provided to third parties (family trusts, family members not covered by the definition of "immediate family") rather than directly; the statute must define "beneficial interest" broadly to cover any financial arrangement where the justice or their household derives economic benefit, regardless of whose name the arrangement is in. Unintended consequence -- mandatory disclosure of speaking fees and teaching honoraria could deter justices from legitimate educational engagement if any institution's prior legal positions could be construed as creating a disclosed conflict; the statute should specify that disclosure alone is not automatic grounds for recusal -- recusal is required only where the disclosed benefit creates an actual documented conflict with a party in a pending case. Abuse path -- adversarial parties could file recusal motions based on disclosed hospitality or speaking engagements from organizations with tangential connections to pending cases, weaponizing disclosure requirements as a litigation tactic to force recusals; the statute must specify standards for recusal motions -- requiring documented, material financial conflicts with a party to the case, not mere association with an organization that has expressed a general view on the relevant legal question.

INT Judicial Integrity and Interpretation 0/3 active
CORT-INTL-0001

Require intent-based interpretation

This position requires courts to consider what Congress intended when it passed a law, not just the dictionary definition of the words. It prevents judges from ignoring obvious legislative purpose to reach outcomes they prefer.

Federal courts shall employ a multi-tool interpretive methodology requiring consideration of statutory text, legislative history, structural context, purpose, and the consequences of competing interpretations; interpretation that produces results demonstrably contrary to the evident purpose of a statute, reached through selective literalism or willful disregard of legislative context, shall be reversible on appeal; Congress shall include formal statements of legislative purpose in all major legislation to provide authoritative interpretive guidance.

Courts must consider legislative intent, context, and purpose alongside statutory text when interpreting laws. Prohibits using strict textualism to ignore obvious legislative intent or reach absurd results. Does not ban consideration of text or require ignoring plain language, but prevents weaponization of selective literalism to subvert clear meaning. Particularly important for rights-related laws where narrow textualism has been used to dismantle protections. Courts should interpret statutes honestly to serve their purpose, not use interpretive methodology as tool to impose ideological outcomes. [ADVERSARIAL] Selective intent: legislative history is itself manipulable, sponsors insert favorable floor statements knowing they may later be used in litigation; requiring courts to consider intent does not prevent cherry-picking the intent statements that support a preferred outcome. Separation of powers: requiring courts to use a specific interpretive methodology by statute touches on judicial independence and may need framing as a default rule rather than a command. Competing intents: different members of Congress had different intents for the same provision; majority coalitions are built on silence and ambiguity; “legislative intent” for complex legislation is often a construction courts impose after the fact. Gaming the system: if intent statements become legally required and legally binding, lobbying effort shifts to shaping those statements rather than bill text. Retroactivity gap: the rule provides no guidance for interpreting the large body of existing statutes that predate any intent-statement requirement.

CORT-INTL-0002

Limit strict textualism

This position limits the use of strict textualism, a method where judges claim to read only the text of a law while ignoring its purpose or context. In practice, strict textualism is often used selectively to justify predetermined outcomes.

Federal courts shall not employ interpretive methodologies that mechanically exclude consideration of legislative history, statutory purpose, or the foreseeable consequences of competing interpretations; in cases where strict textual reading produces a result absurd on its face or directly contrary to the documented intent of the enacting Congress, courts must explain why the textual reading serves the statute’s purpose before adopting it; this rule does not prohibit textual analysis but prohibits its use as a pretext for judicially preferred outcomes contrary to clear legislative intent.

Strict textualism claims to interpret only statutory text while ignoring context, purpose, legislative history, or consequences. In practice this becomes tool for results-oriented judging: selectively reading text narrowly to reach preferred outcomes, using dictionary definitions from different eras to support conclusions, and claiming consideration of purpose is "activism." This rule limits use of strict textualism to undermine clear legislative intent. Requires courts to use multiple interpretive tools: text, intent, context, purpose, consequences, and common sense. When text and obvious intent conflict due to drafting imperfection, courts should not weaponize the gap to subvert the law's purpose. Goal is honest interpretation serving justice. [ADVERSARIAL] Definitional challenge: “strict textualism” vs. “honest text-based interpretation” cannot be distinguished by a clear rule, every judge who uses textualism believes they are applying it honestly; the rule’s effectiveness depends on appellate courts willing to identify and reverse bad-faith applications, which requires political will that may not exist. Enforcement mechanism: reversal on appeal already exists; adding an interpretive methodology requirement doesn’t create a new remedy. Overcorrection risk: pure purpose-based interpretation allows courts to substitute their own policy judgment for Congress’s under cover of “purpose.” Unpredictability: requiring multi-tool analysis without clear hierarchy among interpretive sources may increase rather than decrease decisional inconsistency across circuits. Ideological symmetry: courts across the ideological spectrum use purposivism to reach preferred outcomes just as textualists do; neither methodology is ideologically neutral in application.

CORT-INTL-0003

Require plain-language intent in laws

This position encourages Congress to include plain-language statements in every law explaining what problem it solves and what it is supposed to achieve. These statements make it harder for judges to distort the law's meaning through selective interpretation.

Congress shall include, in every major piece of legislation, a formal Findings and Purpose section stating: the problem the statute addresses, the outcome the legislation seeks to achieve, and the interpretive principles governing ambiguous provisions; these sections shall be treated by courts as authoritative legislative statements of intent for purposes of statutory construction; failure to include such a section shall not invalidate legislation but shall be noted in any interpretive proceeding as a factor reducing the weight of contested legislative history.

Encourage or require Congress to include plain-language intent statements in legislation explaining: what problem the law addresses, what outcome it seeks to achieve, how provisions should be interpreted to accomplish purpose, and what interpretations would subvert the law's intent. Creates explicit interpretive record making bad-faith distortion harder. Does not bind courts to follow Congress's preferred interpretation (separation of powers) but provides authoritative statement of legislative purpose rather than forcing courts to reconstruct intent from scattered sources. Makes honest interpretation easier and dishonest interpretation more visible. [ADVERSARIAL] Lobbying capture: mandatory purpose statements that carry legal weight become high-value lobbying targets, organized interests will spend heavily to shape these statements in ways that limit or expand the statute in their favor. Drafting costs: requiring formal findings sections adds time and complexity to the legislative process, potentially disadvantaging smaller or less organized constituencies. Judicial non-binding: separation of powers means Congress cannot compel courts to weigh intent statements; a court that disagrees with Congress’s stated purpose can simply note it and proceed with textual analysis. Retroactivity gap: existing major statutes lack formal purpose statements and this rule provides no guidance for their interpretation. Ambiguity: “major legislation” is itself undefined, the threshold for when this requirement triggers must be specified or it will be litigated.

OVR Judicial Oversight 0/1 active
CORT-OVRG-0001

Judicial oversight mechanisms

This position establishes independent oversight of judicial conduct, including conflicts of interest and ethical violations. Oversight focuses on unethical behavior, not legal decisions, preserving judicial independence while ensuring accountability.

An independent Office of Judicial Conduct shall receive, investigate, and adjudicate complaints concerning federal judicial ethics violations, financial conflicts, and recusal failures for all Article III judges; the office shall have authority to compel financial disclosures, order recusal in cases with documented conflicts, impose public censure, and refer serious violations to Congress with a written public report; the office’s jurisdiction extends to conduct, not legal decisions; Article III protects judicial rulings from review by this office, not conduct amounting to corruption or ethical violation.

Establish independent oversight for judicial conduct without undermining judicial independence on legal decisions. Key distinction: judges cannot be punished for legal decisions (even unpopular or wrong ones, reversible on appeal); but judges must be accountable for unethical conduct, conflicts of interest, bias, or abuse of office. Mechanisms include: independent inspector general or ethics office to investigate conduct complaints, transparent process for reviewing ethics concerns with public documentation, power to recommend sanctions (censure, fine, removal from specific cases), and clear distinction between reviewable conduct (ethics, conflicts, bias) versus protected judicial decisions (legal interpretations, rulings). Preserves judicial independence while ending ethical impunity. [ADVERSARIAL] Independence of the oversight body: the oversight office is itself subject to appointment politics, if the executive branch controls appointments, the office could be weaponized against politically disfavored judges; if Congress appoints, partisan balance requirements may paralyze it. Conduct vs. decisions: the line between reviewable “conduct” and protected “legal decisions” is genuinely difficult to draw, a pattern of rulings correlated with financial interests could be characterized as protected judicial decisions. Complaint flooding: well-resourced litigants and advocacy groups could flood the office with complaints against judges who rule against them, creating a harassment mechanism that chills judicial independence. Article III challenge: federal courts may hold that an external oversight body cannot compel a sitting Article III judge to take any action, including recusal, without constitutional authorization. Scope creep: an oversight body with broad investigative authority may gradually expand its definition of reviewable “conduct” to include legal positions it disagrees with.

REC Records and Recusal Standards 0/1 active
CORT-RECS-0001

Strong recusal standards

This position establishes mandatory and enforceable rules requiring judges to recuse themselves when they have a financial interest in a case, when close family members are involved, or when they have made public statements showing bias. Self-enforcement of recusal is prohibited.

Federal judges, including Supreme Court justices, must recuse from any matter in which they hold a direct financial interest, in which an immediate family member holds a financial or professional interest, in which they previously participated as counsel or lower-court judge, in which they have received substantial gifts or hospitality from a party or its representative, or where public statements demonstrate prejudgment of the specific legal question at issue; recusal decisions shall be reviewed by an independent body rather than self-determined; violations shall result in reversal of affected decisions on appeal, public censure, and potential disciplinary proceedings.

Establish mandatory, enforceable recusal standards for all federal judges including Supreme Court justices. Clear triggers requiring recusal: direct financial interest in outcome, immediate family member with financial or professional interest, prior participation in case (as attorney, consultant, lower court judge), public statements indicating prejudgment on specific legal issue, receipt of substantial gifts or payments from parties or their representatives, or reasonable appearance that impartiality is compromised. Independent review mechanism to assess contested recusal decisions with public documentation of basis for recusing or declining. Consequences for inappropriate refusal to recuse include reversal on appeal, public censure, and potential disciplinary action for repeated violations. Ends current system where justices decide their own conflicts with no review or consequence, resulting in judges hearing cases involving their financial interests, their family members' political activities, or issues where they've publicly prejudged the outcome. [ADVERSARIAL] Quorum problem: unlike lower federal courts, the Supreme Court has no substitute or replacement mechanism; mandatory recusal of two or more justices can produce 4–4 ties with no controlling precedent, or deprive the Court of a quorum, the recusal rules must address how to handle cases where multiple justices have conflicts. Evasion through structure: financial conflicts can be obscured by holding investments through spouses, trusts, or closely held entities; disclosure and recusal rules must cover beneficial interests, not just direct holdings. Threshold ambiguity: “substantial” gifts require a specific dollar threshold or the rule will be interpreted inconsistently. Self-reporting remains: even with independent review of contested recusal decisions, the initial identification of a potential conflict depends on the judge disclosing it; undisclosed conflicts cannot be reviewed. Retroactivity: judges with pre-existing financial relationships established before the rule may claim grandfather protections; the rule must specify how it applies to ongoing financial arrangements.

VEN Venue Reform 0/2 active
CORT-VENS-0001

Prevent judge and venue shopping

This position prevents plaintiffs from shopping for friendly judges by filing lawsuits in distant districts with minimal connection to the case. It requires plaintiffs to demonstrate a real local connection to the venue they choose.

Federal venue rules shall require plaintiffs challenging federal government action to file in the district where the plaintiff is domiciled, where the agency action was taken, or in the District of Columbia, unless the plaintiff demonstrates substantial localized harm specific to another district; a single district judge may not issue an injunction with effect beyond the geographic boundaries of that district without authorization from a three-judge panel; forum selection primarily for the purpose of obtaining a sympathetic individual judge constitutes an abuse of venue and grounds for mandatory transfer.

Reform venue rules to prevent forum shopping for sympathetic judges who issue nationwide injunctions. Current practice allows plaintiffs to file in any district with minimal connection, enabling strategic selection of single ideologically aligned judges who then block national policies through nationwide injunctions. Reform would: require plaintiffs to demonstrate substantial local connection to chosen venue (actual localized harm or substantial local operations, not just theoretical possibility of harm); limit geographic scope of district court injunctions to the affected district or circuit (not nationwide from single judge); or require multi-judge panels for cases seeking nationwide relief. Preserves access to courts for genuine local claims while preventing strategic judge shopping for nationwide policy blockades. Addresses immigration, environmental, healthcare, and election law cases being blocked nationwide by single district judges in carefully chosen venues. An independent Office of Judicial Accountability must receive and review annual financial disclosures from all Article III judges, investigate recusal violations, and publish findings; self-enforcement of recusal decisions is prohibited for the Supreme Court.

JUD-REC-001 and JUD-ETH-002 establish the standards; JUD-LGO-004 establishes the enforcement infrastructure those rules require. Investigative journalism by ProPublica documented undisclosed gifts, travel, and outside income in the hundreds of thousands of dollars for multiple current Supreme Court justices with no enforcement consequence. CORT-LGOS-0005

Amicus curiae funding and coordination disclosure

This position requires amicus briefs filed in federal courts to disclose all funding sources and any coordination with parties or repeat-filer networks. This transparency reveals when well-funded advocacy groups are manufacturing the appearance of independent expert consensus.

Amicus curiae briefs filed before any federal court must disclose all funding sources, any coordination with a party whose position the brief supports, and membership in any repeat-filer network coordinating across multiple cases before the same court.

The amicus curiae system has been systematically captured by well-funded advocacy networks that coordinate filings to simulate independent expert agreement where none exists. Empirical research on Supreme Court amicus practice has documented that a small number of repeat-filer organizations, many funded by overlapping donor networks, dominate the amicus docket, creating the appearance of broad independent endorsement for ideologically driven positions. Supreme Court Rule 37 requires disclosure of any party's counsel who authored an amicus brief, but does not require disclosure of funding from non-counsel sources or coordination with parties' legal teams. The transparency gap allows interested parties to amplify their legal arguments through nominally independent third-party filings without the court or public knowing the source. This rule extends disclosure requirements to all funding sources (not merely authoring counsel), any communication or coordination with the party being supported, and organizational membership in networks with coordinated docket strategies. [ADVERSARIAL] Proving coordination: informal coordination through phone calls and shared talking points is invisible without subpoena power; the rule can compel disclosure of formal arrangements but cannot reach undocumented coordination. First Amendment concerns: compelled disclosure of advocacy organization donors has been challenged under the First Amendment, with the Supreme Court recognizing some protection for associational privacy, the rule’s constitutionality may be contested. FRAP Rule 29 gap: existing federal rules require disclosure of authoring counsel but not funding sources; the enforcement mechanism for non-compliance (striking non-complying briefs) must be specified. Intermediary structures: funding flowing through multiple layers of 501(c)(3) and 501(c)(4) entities can make tracing original funders practically impossible even with mandatory disclosure. Foreign funder coverage: the rule must specify whether it covers foreign-sourced funding, which raises additional legal questions.

CORT-LGOS-0006

Federal judiciary demographic diversity requirements

This position requires Congress to establish a merit-based screening process for judicial nominees that actively considers demographic diversity as part of judicial legitimacy. Annual public reports on federal court demographics by circuit hold the system accountable.

Congress must establish a merit-based screening process for all Article III judicial nominations that actively evaluates demographic diversity as a component of judicial legitimacy; annual reports on the demographic composition of all federal courts disaggregated by circuit must be published.

Federal courts interpret the rights of all Americans, but their composition has historically failed to reflect the population they serve. As of 2024, women and racial minorities remain significantly underrepresented at all levels of the federal judiciary relative to their share of the legal profession and the general population. Judicial diversity is not merely symbolic: empirical research has found that judicial panel composition affects outcomes in discrimination, civil rights, and employment cases, and that diverse panels produce reasoning that accounts for a wider range of lived experience. A merit-based screening process that affirmatively considers diversity alongside credentials, experience, and judicial temperament does not reduce quality, it expands the pool of qualified candidates and addresses structural barriers (geographic networks, law school ties, clerkship pipelines) that have historically excluded talented jurists from consideration. Annual public reporting creates accountability for progress without mandating specific quotas. [ADVERSARIAL] Legal challenge: diversity requirements in government appointment processes may face equal protection challenges; a requirement that explicitly weighs demographic diversity in judicial selection may be challenged as unconstitutional under evolving equal protection doctrine. Structural diversity without ideological diversity: administrations could appoint demographically diverse candidates who are ideologically uniform, satisfying the letter of the rule while defeating the goal of a judiciary reflecting varied perspectives. Metric definition: which demographics count and how? Race, gender, socioeconomic background, geographic origin, the rule’s meaning depends entirely on operationalization. Reporting without enforcement: annual reporting without consequences for persistent gaps is informational at best; the rule must specify consequences for persistent underrepresentation. Pipeline limitations: judicial diversity is constrained by the legal profession’s feeder tracks; addressing judicial composition without addressing the pipeline may produce incomplete results.

CORT-LGOS-0007

Administrative law judges, independence protections against politically motivated removal

This position protects administrative law judges from being fired, reassigned, or punished by the executive branch as retaliation for their decisions. ALJs decide millions of cases affecting Social Security, immigration, and benefits, and their independence is essential to fair outcomes.

Administrative law judges may not be removed except for good cause shown before an independent review body; executive branch agencies may not remove, reassign, or salary-reduce ALJs as a tool to influence adjudication outcomes; ALJ independence is a statutory right enforceable by judicial review.

Administrative law judges decide millions of cases each year, Social Security disability claims, immigration hearings, securities enforcement proceedings, and agency benefits disputes. They exercise quasi-judicial authority and their impartiality is fundamental to fair administrative adjudication. The Supreme Court's decision in SEC v. Jarkesy (2024) disrupted ALJ structures, while executive orders have asserted authority to reclassify ALJs as at-will employees subject to presidential removal without cause. These converging pressures threaten to eliminate the independence of the administrative judiciary, making ALJ decisions responsive to political pressure from agency heads and the White House rather than law and evidence. JUD-LGO-007 establishes ALJ independence as a non-negotiable statutory floor: removal only for specific good cause adjudicated by an independent review body, and explicit prohibition on using removal, reassignment, or pay reduction to pressure adjudicative outcomes in pending or future cases. [ADVERSARIAL] Post-Jarkesy constitutional uncertainty: the Supreme Court’s 2024 decision in SEC v. Jarkesy called into question the constitutional legitimacy of adjudicative ALJ structures across multiple agencies; any statutory independence protection must navigate this holding. Agency restructuring workaround: the executive branch could eliminate ALJ positions entirely and transfer adjudicative functions to other personnel categories, technically complying with independence protections for the ALJ title while eliminating the positions those protections cover. “Good cause” definition: the standard will be litigated, administrations wanting to remove ALJs will define performance failures as good cause; the rule must specify that policy disagreement and adverse rulings do not constitute good cause. Cross-agency variation: immigration ALJs, Social Security ALJs, and securities enforcement ALJs operate under very different statutory frameworks; a single independence rule must accommodate or specifically address these differences. Independent review body composition: the body adjudicating good-cause removal claims is itself subject to political appointment, potentially making it a second political chokepoint.

CORT-LGOS-0008

This position creates an independent bipartisan commission to evaluate Supreme Court structure and recommend reforms to Congress. Any structural reform based on commission recommendations requires a two-thirds supermajority vote, ensuring broad consensus for major changes.

An independent bipartisan commission must evaluate Supreme Court structure, composition, size, and tenure and report reform recommendations to Congress; any structural reform enacted based on commission recommendations requires a ⅔ supermajority vote in both chambers.

Court-packing: expanding court size for partisan advantage, is one of the most serious threats to judicial legitimacy. No structured deliberative process exists for evaluating structural reforms across partisan lines before crisis forces improvised action. An independent commission, modeled on the Presidential Commission on the Supreme Court of the United States (convened 2021), provides a bipartisan deliberative foundation: commissioners drawn from legal academia, the bar, prior judicial service, and civil society; formal public hearings; published findings; and formal recommendations to Congress. The commission does not implement changes; it develops reform proposals with broad public legitimacy. The ⅔ supermajority requirement in JUD-SIZ-001 governs any structural change the commission recommends, ensuring that even commission-endorsed reforms reflect genuine democratic consensus rather than exploitation of a fleeting partisan majority. This is the procedural complement to JUD-SIZ-001: the commission provides the process; the supermajority provides the threshold. Together they make structural court reform available through democratic deliberation while foreclosing unilateral partisan manipulation. Cross-reference: JUD-SIZ-001 (supermajority requirement for court size changes); JUD-ETH-001 (term limits); JUD-LGO-001 through JUD-LGO-007 (legislative checks on the court). [ADVERSARIAL] Commission non-binding history: presidential commissions on court reform have a poor track record of producing legislative action, the 2021 Presidential Commission on the Supreme Court produced a comprehensive report that was shelved; another commission without binding authority may produce the same result. Bipartisanship definition: “bipartisan” is contested in a political environment with multiple factions within each party; commission composition fights could reproduce the same political conflicts the commission is meant to transcend. Reform scope limitation: the ⅔ supermajority requirement governing implementation may be so difficult to achieve that even well-reasoned reform proposals are dead on arrival, making the commission a legitimacy-building exercise rather than a path to actual reform. Strategic participation: a party opposed to reform can participate to shape recommendations toward the least threatening options, then block even those in the supermajority vote. Timeline: without a specified deadline for reporting and a mechanism for forcing a Congressional vote on recommendations, the commission’s work can be indefinitely delayed.

PROS Prosecutorial Oversight and Accountability 0/3 active
CRTS-PROS-0001

Independent State Prosecutorial Oversight Commission

This position establishes an independent state commission to oversee prosecutorial conduct and investigate misconduct, including Brady violations where prosecutors hide evidence that could prove a defendant's innocence. No such oversight currently exists in most states.

Every state must establish an independent prosecutorial oversight commission with subpoena power, staffed by members appointed through processes independent of district attorney associations; commission membership must include criminal defense attorneys, civil rights advocates, exonerees, and community members. The commission must investigate all Brady violations, knowing use of false testimony, and racial disparate-charging complaints; sustained findings must be publicly disclosed and referred to the state bar for disciplinary action. Prosecutors with three or more sustained misconduct findings in ten years are subject to mandatory decertification.

Legal basis: Brady v. Maryland, 373 U.S. 83 (1963) (establishing the constitutional duty to disclose material exculpatory evidence, violation of which is the primary conduct the oversight commission must address); Giglio v. United States, 405 U.S. 150 (1972) (extending Brady obligations to impeachment evidence); 42 U.S.C. § 1983 (federal civil rights statute authorizing civil liability for deprivation of rights under color of law, which prosecutorial misconduct can implicate -- but current absolute immunity doctrine neutralizes this for prosecutorial decisions); National Registry of Exonerations (2023 annual report, https://www.law.umich.edu/special/exoneration/Pages/about.aspx) (documenting the role of official misconduct, primarily Brady violations and false evidence, as a contributing factor in over half of all documented wrongful convictions). Empirical evidence: the National Registry of Exonerations has documented over 3,000 wrongful convictions since 1989; official misconduct (Brady violations, false evidence, witness tampering) contributes to more than half of exonerations in murder cases; no federal law requires independent oversight of prosecutorial conduct; bar association ethics processes are widely regarded as inadequate -- prosecutors self-report violations at low rates, bar associations lack investigative resources, and sanctions are rare even in cases of documented misconduct; a 2021 study by the Innocence Project found Brady violations in over 40% of documented wrongful conviction cases (Innocence Project, 2021, Prosecutorial oversight: A national dialogue in the wake of connick v. Thompson). Enforcement mechanisms: subpoena power ensures the commission can compel document production and witness testimony; referral to state bar creates a disciplinary track independent of prosecutorial self-policing; mandatory decertification for repeat violators removes prosecutors who demonstrate systematic disregard for constitutional obligations; public disclosure creates accountability and enables pattern identification across offices. PolicyOS compliance: KERN-0004 (no accountability mechanism for Brady violations means prosecutors can hide evidence with impunity -- absolute prosecutorial immunity prevents civil suits, criminal prosecution by colleagues is rare, and bar discipline is ineffective; independent oversight is the minimum required to make the Brady obligation something other than nominal); ENFA-0001 (oversight commissions must be structurally independent -- appointment through processes independent of district attorney associations is essential to prevent capture by the entities being overseen). Adversarial review: Gap -- state-level commissions address state prosecutors but leave federal prosecutors entirely outside the oversight structure; the federal system has no equivalent to the proposed state commissions -- U.S. Attorneys operate under DOJ supervision, which is inherently hierarchical and not independent; a companion federal oversight provision is needed. Loophole -- "independent of district attorney associations" in the appointment process does not prevent informal capture through governor appointments or legislative selections that are politically aligned with law enforcement interests; commission membership requirements (defense attorneys, civil rights advocates, exonerees, community members) are essential but could be satisfied formally while still resulting in a commission dominated by government-friendly perspectives; the statute should specify minimum ratios among member categories. Unintended consequence -- mandatory decertification for three sustained findings could create an incentive for prosecutors to contest every misconduct finding aggressively, converting the oversight commission into a litigation battleground rather than an accountability mechanism; clear, non-adversarial procedures for responding to findings are needed. Abuse path -- a politically hostile state legislature could fund the commission inadequately, limiting its staff and investigative capacity; the commission must have a statutory minimum budget tied to a percentage of the prosecutorial budget it oversees, not subject to annual discretionary appropriation.

CRTS-PROS-0002

Knowing Brady Violations Are a Federal Crime

This position makes it a federal crime for prosecutors to knowingly hide evidence that could prove a defendant is innocent. Current law grants prosecutors absolute immunity from civil lawsuits even for intentional misconduct, creating no meaningful deterrent.

A prosecutor who knowingly and willfully fails to disclose material exculpatory evidence to the defense commits a federal crime punishable by up to 5 years imprisonment and permanent bar from law practice. In any case where a Brady violation is found to have contributed to a wrongful conviction, the convicted person has a private right of action against the responsible prosecutor for actual damages, compensatory damages of $50,000 per year of wrongful incarceration, and attorneys' fees. Absolute prosecutorial immunity does not apply to conduct constituting a knowing Brady violation.

Legal basis: Brady v. Maryland, 373 U.S. 83 (1963) (establishing the constitutional disclosure obligation -- its violation is the predicate conduct for criminal liability under this position); Imbler v. Pachtman, 424 U.S. 409 (1976) (Supreme Court recognizing absolute prosecutorial immunity for acts taken in the judicial phase of criminal proceedings -- this position directly overrides Imbler for knowing Brady violations by statute); Connick v. Thompson, 563 U.S. 51 (2011) (reversing a $14 million wrongful conviction judgment against a prosecutor's office that had withheld blood evidence in a capital case -- underscoring the scope of protection absolute immunity provides even for documented misconduct); 18 U.S.C. § 1519 (obstruction of justice statute, providing a model for federalizing conduct that impedes legal proceedings -- knowing Brady violations similarly obstruct the judicial process). Empirical evidence: the Innocence Project and National Registry of Exonerations document Brady violations in over 40% of wrongful conviction cases; current absolute prosecutorial immunity means that even documented, knowing Brady violations rarely produce civil liability; criminal prosecution of prosecutors for Brady violations is extremely rare -- the DOJ has brought fewer than a handful of such cases since Brady was decided in 1963; the structural incentive created by immunity combined with no criminal liability for Brady violations is to treat disclosure as optional when evidence is harmful to the prosecution's theory. Enforcement mechanisms: federal criminal statute provides a deterrent threat independent of civil litigation; private right of action gives wrongfully convicted persons a direct remedy; permanent bar from law practice eliminates the possibility of repeat violations by removing the violator from the profession; elimination of absolute immunity for knowing Brady violations closes the Imbler loophole for the specific conduct addressed. PolicyOS compliance: KERN-0004 (the current framework, civil immunity plus rare criminal prosecution, creates a near-zero accountability environment for knowing Brady violations -- this position creates the minimum deterrence structure required to make the Brady obligation real rather than aspirational); ENFA-0001 (criminal liability and private rights of action are the enforcement mechanisms that courts cannot waive through immunity doctrine -- they must be statutory, not judge-made). Adversarial review: Gap -- "knowing and willful" is a high mens rea standard that requires proof of subjective awareness of the evidence and deliberate non-disclosure; most Brady violations occur through negligent record management, failure to review files, or willful blindness rather than deliberate knowing concealment -- the criminal standard will rarely be met, limiting the statute's scope to the most egregious cases; a separate provision establishing civil liability for negligent Brady violations is needed to address the broader compliance problem. Loophole -- "knowingly and willfully" will be contested in every case through claims of inadvertence, poor record management systems, or lack of awareness of the evidence's exculpatory significance; the statute should include a rebuttable presumption that a prosecutor knew of evidence in the prosecutor's own case files, shifting the burden to explain non-disclosure. Unintended consequence -- criminal liability for Brady violations could make prosecutors more aggressive in motion practice, fighting every Brady request as a litigation strategy to avoid creating a record that could be used against them; it could also make prosecutors over-disclose to avoid any possible liability, flooding defense counsel with irrelevant material ("document dumping") that buries material exculpatory evidence; the statute should address over-disclosure as well as under-disclosure. Abuse path -- a hostile administration could use the criminal Brady violation statute selectively, charging career prosecutors of the opposing party over close calls in disclosure judgment while protecting its own prosecutors from scrutiny; an independent oversight mechanism (such as the CRTS-PROS-0001 commissions) is essential to ensure non-selective enforcement.

CRTS-PROS-0003

Mandatory Annual Racial and Income Disparity Audit of Charging Decisions

This position requires prosecutors to conduct annual public audits showing whether charging decisions create racial or income-based disparities. Transparency helps identify and address patterns of discriminatory prosecution.

Every prosecutor's office with 10 or more full-time prosecutors must publish an annual charging disparity report showing charging rates, plea rates, and sentence outcomes by race, ethnicity, income level, and offense category. Offices showing disparate charging rates for similarly situated defendants, controlling for offense severity and criminal history, must submit a remediation plan to the state attorney general. Federal prosecutors' offices must report to DOJ, which must publish aggregate data publicly.

Legal basis: U.S. Const. amend. XIV, § 1 (Equal Protection Clause -- racial and income-based disparities in charging decisions may constitute equal protection violations when they reflect discriminatory intent, which mandatory reporting creates a record to identify and prove); 42 U.S.C. § 3789d (prohibiting federally funded criminal justice programs from discriminating on account of race -- mandatory disparity reporting creates the evidentiary base for enforcement); McCleskey v. Kemp, 481 U.S. 279 (1987) (the Supreme Court's rejection of a statistical pattern of racial disparity as proof of intentional discrimination in capital sentencing -- demonstrating that absent case-level documentation of intent, statistical patterns of disparity are insufficient under current doctrine; the remedy is case-level charging data that enables identification of individual prosecutors making discriminatory decisions). Empirical evidence: federal and state studies consistently document racial disparities in charging decisions after controlling for offense severity; the U.S. Sentencing Commission has documented that Black male defendants receive sentences approximately 20% longer than similarly situated white male defendants (U.S. Sentencing Commission, 2017, Demographic differences in sentencing: An update to the 2012 booker report); prosecutorial charging decisions, which precede sentencing, are even less transparent than sentencing and have received less systematic study; no federal statute currently requires charging disparity reporting; the absence of mandatory data collection has made it impossible to identify discriminatory patterns at the office level. Enforcement mechanisms: mandatory annual reporting creates a public record that advocates, journalists, and legislators can scrutinize; remediation plan requirement creates a legal hook for state attorney general oversight of offices with identified disparities; federal reporting to DOJ enables national pattern analysis; failure to report is an enforceable regulatory violation -- loss of federal criminal justice assistance funds is the appropriate sanction. PolicyOS compliance: KERN-0004 (charging decisions are entirely unreviewable by anyone outside the prosecutor's office under current law -- mandatory transparency is the minimum accountability mechanism for a government function with life-altering consequences); DEMO-0001 (the public has no ability to evaluate whether prosecution resources are deployed equitably without public data -- transparency is a democratic accountability prerequisite). Adversarial review: Gap -- controlling for "offense severity and criminal history" is methodologically contested -- what counts as "similar" situations is itself a policy judgment that can be made in ways that explain away disparities rather than identify them; the statute must specify the statistical methodology for disparity analysis and establish an independent technical standard, not leave it to each office to define its own comparison group. Loophole -- prosecutors can undermine the reporting requirement through discretionary charge reduction before the reporting date, artificially reducing the disparity measure for the annual report while maintaining discriminatory patterns throughout the year; reports should capture initial charges and final charges separately to identify disparities in plea bargaining as well as initial filing. Unintended consequence -- mandatory reporting may create an incentive for prosecutors to charge minority defendants less often for serious offenses (to reduce the disparity metric) while increasing prosecution for less serious offenses, or to increase serious charges against white defendants to equalize rates; reporting should capture absolute charging rates for all demographic groups, not just relative rates. Abuse path -- a prosecutor's office with a hostile relationship with community oversight groups could manipulate the data collection methodology to show compliance while maintaining actual disparities; independent auditing of the data collection methodology, not just the reported numbers, is needed.

JURY Jury System Reform 0/3 active
CRTS-JURY-0001

Eliminate Peremptory Strikes in Capital and Class A Felony Cases

This position eliminates peremptory strikes in capital and serious felony cases. Peremptory strikes have been used systematically to remove Black jurors from juries, despite Supreme Court rulings supposedly prohibiting racial discrimination.

Peremptory strikes -- the dismissal of prospective jurors without stated cause -- are abolished in capital cases and Class A felony trials. All jury strikes in such cases must be for cause, stated on the record, and subject to judicial review. In all other cases, the number of peremptory strikes is reduced to no more than 3 per side; any peremptory strike challenged under Batson must be supported by a race-neutral explanation subject to heightened judicial scrutiny with written findings.

Legal basis: Batson v. Kentucky, 476 U.S. 79 (1986) (holding that the Equal Protection Clause prohibits striking jurors solely because of race -- establishing the framework that this position strengthens through abolition rather than post-hoc challenge); J.E.B. v. Alabama ex rel. T.B., 511 U.S. 127 (1994) (extending Batson to gender-based peremptory strikes); U.S. Const. amend. VI (right to an impartial jury -- the Sixth Amendment's impartial jury guarantee requires that the jury selection process not systematically exclude jurors based on race or other protected characteristics); Arizona Supreme Court (2021) (Arizona became the first state to abolish most peremptory strikes in criminal cases, effective January 2022 -- providing a working domestic precedent for complete abolition). Empirical evidence: Batson challenges have proven ineffective -- the "race-neutral explanation" standard is so easily met by pretextual justifications that documented racial strikes continue despite the ruling; research by Grosso and O'Brien (2012, A stubborn legacy: The overwhelming importance of race in jury selection in 173 post-Batson North Carolina capital trials, Iowa Law Review) found that prosecutors used peremptory strikes to remove Black jurors at more than twice the rate of white jurors in North Carolina capital trials even after Batson; studies by Baldus et al. and others document that all-white juries convict Black defendants at significantly higher rates in capital cases (Baldus, Woodworth, et al., 2001, The use of peremptory challenges in capital murder trials, University of Pennsylvania Journal of Constitutional Law); England and Wales abolished peremptory strikes entirely in 1988 with no adverse effects on trial integrity. Enforcement mechanisms: abolition in capital and Class A felony cases -- categorical prohibition rather than case-by-case challenge, which has proven ineffective; cause requirement stated on the record -- creates a reviewable record for appeals; heightened Batson scrutiny with written findings -- prevents pro forma "race-neutral" justifications from satisfying judicial review; limiting to 3 strikes per side -- reduces the volume of pretextual strikes even in cases where full abolition does not apply. PolicyOS compliance: KERN-0004 (the jury selection process is a fundamental constitutional mechanism -- systematic racial exclusion from juries is a direct violation of equal protection that current Batson doctrine has failed to prevent; structural abolition is the only remedy that works); DEMO-0001 (representative juries are a democratic accountability mechanism -- exclusion of Black jurors from capital cases involving Black defendants creates a systematic bias in the exercise of the government's most severe power). Adversarial review: Gap -- abolition in capital and Class A felony trials leaves all other criminal cases using cause-plus-limited-peremptories; the same racial exclusion patterns documented in capital cases also occur in lesser felony and misdemeanor cases where peremptory strikes remain available; the 3-strike limit and heightened Batson scrutiny may be insufficient to prevent systematic exclusion in non-covered cases. Loophole -- requiring cause-based strikes without independent investigation means that each judge individually evaluates the proffered cause, and judges who are deferential to prosecutors will approve pretextual cause explanations the same way they approved pretextual Batson explanations; independent research on cause-strike approvals post-abolition is needed to verify the reform's effectiveness. Unintended consequence -- abolishing peremptory strikes removes the defense's ability to remove jurors who express potential bias that does not rise to a challengeable cause -- a defense attorney who believes a juror is hostile to their client but cannot prove legal bias loses a mechanism that currently protects defendants; this should be partially addressed by strengthening judicial review of cause-based strikes to be genuinely rigorous, not just formally satisfied. Abuse path -- judges could develop a practice of approving every cause-based strike in capital cases without meaningful scrutiny, converting the cause standard into a functional peremptory strike disguised as cause; independent review of cause strike approvals by courts of appeal must be de novo, not deferential to trial court findings.

CRTS-JURY-0002

Jury Pools Must Reflect County Demographics Within 10%

This position requires jury pools to reflect the racial and demographic makeup of the county within 10 percentage points. Representative juries are essential to fair verdicts and equal justice.

Courts must audit jury pool composition annually and compare it to the demographics of the eligible population in the jurisdiction. Any jury pool that deviates from eligible population demographics by more than 10% on any racial, ethnic, or income dimension triggers mandatory remedial action, including revised source list composition, updated summons procedures, and targeted outreach. Federal courts must report jury pool demographics to the Administrative Office of the Courts; state courts must report to the state court administrator.

Legal basis: U.S. Const. amend. VI (fair cross-section requirement -- Taylor v. Louisiana, 419 U.S. 522 (1975) held that the Sixth Amendment requires jury pools to represent a fair cross-section of the community; Duren v. Missouri, 439 U.S. 357 (1979) established the test for cross-section violations, requiring systematic exclusion of a distinctive community group); 28 U.S.C. § 1861 (Jury Selection and Service Act -- declaring the policy that all litigants are entitled to grand and petit juries selected at random from a fair cross-section of the community and prohibiting exclusion based on race, color, religion, sex, national origin, or economic status). Empirical evidence: despite the Taylor cross-section requirement, jury pools in many jurisdictions are substantially whiter and wealthier than the eligible population; source lists (voter registration, DMV records) systematically underrepresent communities of color, lower-income residents, and renters who move frequently; summons non-response rates are higher in underrepresented communities due to language barriers, lack of employer protections, and hardship from low jury pay; a 2020 study by the Brennan Center found that approximately 9% of federal jury pools had a statistically significant racial underrepresentation relative to the eligible population in their districts (Jury Representation in America, Brennan Center for Justice, 2021). Enforcement mechanisms: annual audits create a quantitative record of deviation from population demographics; mandatory remedial action triggers at 10% deviation -- prevents gradual drift below constitutional minimums; revised source lists, updated summons procedures, and targeted outreach are the three structural interventions most likely to address the documented causes of underrepresentation; reporting to Administrative Office of Courts / state court administrators enables oversight and national pattern analysis. PolicyOS compliance: KERN-0004 (juries that do not represent the community they purport to speak for lack the basic democratic legitimacy the Sixth Amendment jury right is meant to secure -- the cross-section requirement has proven inadequately enforced by voluntary compliance); DEMO-0001 (representative juries are a democratic institution -- their composition must reflect the community whose standards they are asked to apply). Adversarial review: Gap -- the 10% deviation threshold is a proposed standard, not a constitutionally derived number; what constitutes an acceptable level of demographic deviation has not been established by statute or court decision; the threshold needs methodological grounding and should be set in consultation with statisticians to represent a meaningful rather than arbitrary standard. Loophole -- "eligible population" for jury service excludes non-citizens, those convicted of felonies, and those under 18, which systematically excludes a significant portion of communities of color where incarceration rates have reduced the eligible jury pool; the statute should address felon disenfranchisement from jury service as a separate provision, since it compounds the underrepresentation problem in ways that source list changes cannot fix. Unintended consequence -- mandatory 10% deviation triggers could create pressure on courts to qualify jurors who might otherwise be excused for bias, in order to achieve demographic targets; jury pool composition goals must not override the individual right to challenge partial jurors for cause -- composition audits operate at the pool level, not the final jury selection stage. Abuse path -- courts could satisfy the reporting requirement without genuine remediation, documenting persistent deviations while citing resource constraints or legal complexity as reasons for non-compliance; the statute must include a clear remedy mechanism -- contempt of court for failure to implement required remedial actions after a deviation trigger.

CRTS-JURY-0003

Jury Duty Pay Must Not Impose Financial Hardship

This position ensures jury duty pay does not create financial hardship for hourly workers. Current federal jury pay of $50 per day excludes working-class people from jury service, making juries unrepresentative of the community.

Federal jury duty compensation must be raised to no less than $75 per day for the first 10 days and $125 per day thereafter; employers with more than 50 employees must continue full pay and benefits during jury duty. No employee may be disciplined, demoted, or terminated for serving on jury duty. Workers who lose wages during jury service for employers with fewer than 50 employees must receive supplemental compensation from the court to cover the difference between jury pay and their average daily wage up to $250 per day.

Legal basis: 28 U.S.C. § 1871 (current federal juror compensation statute, setting the rate at $50 per day for the first 10 days and $60 per day thereafter -- the existing authority that would be amended by this position); 28 U.S.C. § 1875 (federal statute prohibiting employers from disciplining or discharging employees for serving on federal jury duty -- the model for the employment protection expanded in this position); U.S. Const. amend. VI (the Sixth Amendment right to an impartial jury is structurally undermined when the jury pool systematically excludes hourly workers who cannot afford to serve -- compensation adequate to prevent hardship is a functional prerequisite for a fair cross-section jury). Empirical evidence: current federal jury pay is $50 per day for the first 10 days and $60 per day thereafter; $50 per day is below the federal minimum wage for a full day of work ($58 for an 8-hour day at $7.25/hr); hourly workers, who are disproportionately lower-income and disproportionately workers of color, face financial hardship from jury service that salaried workers do not experience; this produces systematic demographic skew in who actually appears for and completes jury service; many states set jury pay even lower -- some as low as $15 or $10 per day; the resulting jury pool skew compounds the source list underrepresentation documented in the CRTS-JURY-0002 position. Enforcement mechanisms: statutory compensation increase amending 28 U.S.C. § 1871 -- applies to all federal jury service immediately; employer pay continuation requirement for 50+ employee employers -- creates an enforceable obligation with existing employment law enforcement mechanisms; anti-retaliation provision -- consistent with existing 28 U.S.C. § 1875, extended and strengthened; supplemental court compensation for small-employer workers -- ensures low-income workers at small employers are not financially excluded from jury service. PolicyOS compliance: KERN-0004 (jury duty pay below the poverty line functionally excludes hourly workers from jury service -- a civic obligation should not impose catastrophic financial hardship on the citizens required to perform it); THRV-0001 (economic security is a foundation policy area condition -- requiring low-income workers to serve on juries at poverty-level compensation while salaried workers receive full pay is a structural inequality that this position directly addresses). Adversarial review: Gap -- the $250/day cap on supplemental compensation means workers earning above that level (approximately $31/hour) continue to face wage gaps even with court supplementation; the cap should be reviewed and indexed to median wage for the jurisdiction rather than a fixed national figure. Loophole -- employers with 50 or more employees who are required to pay full wages during jury duty could respond by pressuring employees not to appear, creating informal retaliation that the anti-retaliation statute must cover explicitly; the statute should extend anti-retaliation protections to include any adverse employment action within 12 months of jury service, with a presumption that adverse actions in that period are jury-related unless the employer demonstrates an independent documented basis. Unintended consequence -- raising federal jury pay to $75-$125 per day may create an incentive for some unemployed or underemployed individuals to seek jury service over other labor, reducing the random-selection nature of jury pools; the compensation increase is calibrated to prevent hardship rather than to make jury service financially attractive, and should remain below average daily wages for most occupations. Abuse path -- employers who would prefer to retain employees from jury service could formally comply with pay requirements while finding other means to discourage or shorten jury service; the statute should require employers to certify compliance with jury service obligations in annual EEO filings, creating a regulatory compliance record.

CRTS-EXPR-0001

Automatic Expungement of All Federal Marijuana Possession Convictions

This position automatically expunges all federal marijuana possession convictions. An estimated 40,000 people are currently incarcerated for federal marijuana offenses[8], and hundreds of thousands more have convictions that harm their employment and housing prospects.

All federal convictions for simple marijuana possession must be automatically expunged within 12 months of enactment, without requiring any petition, filing fee, or attorney. The DOJ must notify every affected person in writing that their record has been expunged; all federal databases, background check systems, and court records must be updated automatically. Expungement is complete and permanent; the record may not be accessed for any purpose including employment, housing, or professional licensing.

Legal basis: U.S. Const. amend. V (due process clause -- criminal records from offenses that have subsequently been decriminalized or descheduled impose ongoing punishment without a legitimate penological justification, raising due process concerns); First Step Act of 2018, Pub. L. 115-391 (federal legislation that reduced mandatory minimum sentences for certain drug offenses and expanded judicial discretion -- establishing congressional precedent for retroactive relief in federal drug convictions); MORE Act of 2022, H.R. 3617, 117th Cong. (passed the House with broad support, including automatic federal marijuana expungement provisions -- confirming legislative feasibility); Marijuana Opportunity Reinvestment and Expungement Act (establishing the congressional authorization framework for automatic federal expungement). Empirical evidence: an estimated 40,000 people are currently incarcerated for federal marijuana offenses (Bureau of Justice Statistics, 2021 federal justice statistics program data -- [VERIFY current year]); the U.S. Sentencing Commission estimates hundreds of thousands of persons have active federal marijuana possession convictions on their records affecting background checks and civil status; 21 states and the District of Columbia have enacted some form of marijuana expungement, demonstrating state-level feasibility; marijuana possession convictions disproportionately affect Black and Latino individuals -- federal data (ACLU, The War on Marijuana in Black and White, 2013) shows Black individuals are 3.73 times more likely to be arrested for marijuana possession than white individuals despite similar usage rates; criminal records for possession impose ongoing employment, housing, and professional licensing collateral consequences that continue to punish after the sentence is served. Enforcement mechanisms: 12-month automatic expungement timeline with DOJ implementation -- creates a legally enforceable deadline; mandatory written notification -- ensures affected persons know their records have changed and can rely on the expungement in future interactions; mandatory update of all federal databases and background check systems -- ensures expungement is practically complete, not just nominal; permanent and complete expungement with no exceptions -- prevents fragmented implementation where some databases are updated and others are not. PolicyOS compliance: KERN-0004 (the federal government continues to impose collateral consequences for possession of a substance that is now legal in 21 states -- the ongoing punishment is disproportionate and the criminal record serves no public safety function for simple possession); THRV-0001 (criminal records for marijuana possession create persistent barriers to employment, housing, and professional licensing that prevent economic stability and reintegration -- automatic expungement is a structural barrier removal measure, not a sentencing reduction). Adversarial review: Gap -- automatic expungement of "simple possession" convictions requires a clear statutory definition of what qualifies; many marijuana convictions involve charges that include possession with intent to distribute or trafficking charges alongside simple possession; the statute must specify that expungement applies to possession-only convictions and to any marijuana conviction where the facts would only support simple possession under current law, not just convictions formally styled as simple possession. Loophole -- employers could use the general practice of asking about non-expunged convictions as a filter, and then research whether the absence of a record might be due to expungement; "ban the box" protections (prohibiting employers from asking about criminal history in initial applications) are needed alongside expungement to prevent this workaround. Unintended consequence -- automatic expungement without individual review could remove records in cases where the conviction was for possession but the underlying conduct involved harm to other persons (e.g., possession by a person who caused an accident while impaired); the expungement scope should be limited to straightforward possession cases and should provide DOJ discretion to exclude cases involving documented harm. Abuse path -- federal law enforcement databases and intelligence files may retain marijuana possession data even after formal expungement, under law enforcement exemptions; the statute must explicitly require expungement from all federal databases including law enforcement intelligence files, not just court records and civilian background check systems.

CRTS-EXPR-0002

Automatic Expungement for Non-Violent Offenses After 5 Crime-Free Years

This position automatically expunges non-violent offense convictions after five crime-free years. People who have paid their debt to society and stayed out of trouble should not face lifelong barriers to jobs, housing, and education.

Any person convicted of a non-violent offense who has completed their sentence, paid all restitution owed, and remained free of new convictions for 5 years is automatically eligible for expungement; no petition, filing fee, or attorney is required. The court must expunge the record within 90 days of the 5-year eligibility date. Expunged records may not be disclosed to employers, landlords, or licensing agencies; use of an expunged record as the basis for denial of employment, housing, or a professional license is an actionable civil rights violation.

Legal basis: U.S. Const. amend. V (due process and liberty interests -- criminal records impose permanent punishment without a judicial finding that the ongoing harm is proportionate to a legitimate government interest in maintaining the record); Clean Slate Act proposals (introduced in multiple Congresses, most recently S. 1312, 118th Cong. (2023), providing a federal framework for automatic expungement of non-violent federal convictions -- establishing congressional authorization and feasibility); 18 U.S.C. § 3607 (existing federal expungement provision for first-offense drug convictions for persons under 21 -- providing precedent for federal statutory expungement authority); Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 (EEOC guidance on criminal records in hiring decisions establishes that blanket exclusion of applicants with criminal records can violate Title VII where it creates disparate impact -- expungement is necessary to make this protection meaningful). Empirical evidence: the Brennan Center for Justice estimates that over 70 million Americans -- roughly one in three adults -- have some form of criminal record; studies show that criminal records reduce callbacks for job applications by approximately 50% (Pager, D., 2003, The mark of a criminal record, American Journal of Sociology); the collateral consequences of criminal records are documented to affect housing (over 70% of public housing agencies use criminal records in admissions decisions), employment, professional licensing, educational access, and parental rights; the 5-year crime-free period requirement aligns with existing state clean slate laws (Pennsylvania Clean Slate Law, Michigan Clean Slate Law) that have been implemented without documented public safety consequences. Enforcement mechanisms: automatic eligibility trigger -- eliminates the petition burden that is the primary barrier to expungement under current voluntary systems; 90-day mandatory expungement deadline -- creates an enforceable timeline; civil rights violation for use of expunged records -- provides an individual private right of action for affected persons; prohibition on disclosure to employers, landlords, and licensing agencies -- closes the most harmful pathways for collateral consequences. PolicyOS compliance: KERN-0004 (criminal records impose lifelong punishment for conduct that occurred years in the past, without any individualized assessment of ongoing public safety justification -- the 5-year crime-free period provides a reasonable basis for concluding the risk has passed); THRV-0001 (barriers to employment, housing, and professional licensing from old criminal records directly undermine economic security and wellbeing -- automatic expungement removes structural barriers to reintegration). Adversarial review: Gap -- "non-violent offense" requires a statutory definition; many offenses that seem non-violent (fraud, identity theft, drug distribution) can involve serious harm to victims, and many victims may reasonably object to expungement of offenses that caused them significant financial or personal harm; the statute must define "non-violent" with specificity and address victim notification before expungement of offenses with identifiable victims. Loophole -- private background check companies aggregate and redistribute public record data; even after a record is expunged from official databases, private data brokers may retain and sell the information; the statute must explicitly extend expungement obligations to private background check companies and data brokers, prohibiting them from disclosing expunged records in violation of a private cause of action with statutory damages. Unintended consequence -- "completed their sentence, paid all restitution owed" creates a two-tier system where persons who cannot pay financial components of their sentence (fines, fees, restitution) are ineligible for expungement indefinitely; this disproportionately affects low-income individuals and perpetuates the criminal justice system's existing financial access inequalities; the statute should separate the restitution condition from the expungement eligibility for persons with documented inability to pay. Abuse path -- employers could use the absence of a background check result as a proxy indicator for an expunged record (noting that a 35-year-old applicant has no employment history from ages 25-30 suggests possible incarceration) -- expungement addresses the formal record but not the inference problem; the statute must be paired with ban-the-box requirements that prohibit criminal history inquiry until after a conditional offer of employment.

CRTS-EXPR-0003

Federal and State One-Stop Expungement Portal

This position creates a single online portal where people can apply to expunge eligible records from both federal and state systems. The current process is fragmented, expensive, and inaccessible to most people with records.

The DOJ must fund and maintain a federally integrated expungement portal through which any person may apply for expungement of federal records at no cost; states receiving federal criminal justice assistance funds must integrate their record-clearing systems with the portal or maintain a comparable one-stop state portal. No filing fee may be charged for any expungement or record sealing petition; legal aid organizations must be funded to assist applicants.

Legal basis: 34 U.S.C. § 10101 et seq. (Omnibus Crime Control and Safe Streets Act -- authorizing federal criminal justice assistance funds to states, the leverage mechanism by which this position requires state portal integration); 28 U.S.C. § 534 (authorizing the Attorney General to maintain national criminal justice information systems -- providing the DOJ authority over the federal database infrastructure that an expungement portal must connect to); Americans with Disabilities Act and Section 508 of the Rehabilitation Act (any federally funded portal must be accessible to persons with disabilities -- the expungement portal must meet WCAG 2.1 AA standards). Empirical evidence: voluntary expungement petitioning systems have failed -- even in states with clear eligibility criteria, only a small fraction of eligible persons actually petition for expungement; a RAND Corporation study found that in Michigan, only 6.5% of people eligible for expungement had received it after five years of eligibility (Prescott, J.J. & Starr, S.B., 2020, Expungement of criminal convictions: An empirical study, Harvard Law Review); the primary barriers are cost (attorney fees, filing fees, multiple court appearances), complexity (different procedures for different offense types across multiple jurisdictions), and lack of awareness that eligibility exists; a one-stop portal eliminates the procedural complexity barrier while zero filing fees eliminate the financial barrier; in states that have implemented automatic or simplified expungement, uptake rates are dramatically higher than voluntary petition systems. Enforcement mechanisms: federal criminal justice assistance fund conditions -- states must integrate or maintain comparable portals as a condition of receiving federal funds; legal aid organization funding -- ensures that persons who need human assistance with the portal application have access to it; zero filing fee mandate -- prohibits any governmental entity from charging fees as a condition of expungement access; the portal must provide real-time eligibility screening so applicants can determine whether they qualify before completing the process. PolicyOS compliance: KERN-0004 (the expungement right established in CRTS-EXPR-0001 and CRTS-EXPR-0002 is meaningless if the process for exercising that right is prohibitively complex or expensive -- procedural access is as important as substantive eligibility); THRV-0001 (the economic benefits of expungement are only realized by persons who successfully complete the process -- reducing procedural barriers directly improves outcomes for the population the policy is designed to help). Adversarial review: Gap -- a federally integrated expungement portal resolves access to federal records but does not address the fragmentation of state record systems; many individuals have records in multiple states, and "comparable one-stop state portal" is a minimum requirement that still requires applicants to navigate multiple state systems for multi-state records; the statute should establish a cross-state record integration standard that enables a single application to initiate expungement in all states where a person has eligible records. Loophole -- the "comparable one-stop state portal" standard is vague and could be satisfied by states creating minimalist portals that technically qualify as one-stop but provide inadequate guidance, language access, or accessibility; the statute must specify minimum standards for comparability, including multi-language support, accessibility compliance, and eligibility determination functionality, not just a digital filing system. Unintended consequence -- a federally maintained portal that stores records of expungement applications could itself become a database that agencies could access to identify persons who have sought expungement; the portal must be designed with privacy by design principles -- it should not retain application data beyond the time needed to process the application, and it must be explicitly prohibited from sharing portal usage data with law enforcement or background check companies. Abuse path -- an administration hostile to expungement could defund the DOJ portal or reduce its functionality, making it technically operational but practically unusable; the statute should establish minimum service level standards (response time, availability, processing time) enforceable by mandamus and should require annual independent accessibility and functionality audits.

Research & Context

The courts and judicial reform policy area emerged from recognition that the judiciary has become a primary battleground for imposing ideological agendas without democratic accountability. The Supreme Court's recent term exemplifies the problem: overturning 50 years of precedent on abortion rights (Dobbs), inventing broad presidential immunity without constitutional text or historical support, systematically dismantling regulatory authority, gutting voting rights protections, and imposing originalist interpretations that ignore modern society while selectively reading history to support preferred outcomes.

The Dobbs decision particularly crystallized the problem of an unaccountable court overturning settled precedent based on contested historical interpretation and textualist reading of a Constitution written when women couldn't vote, own property, or serve on juries. The majority opinion claimed abortion wasn't "deeply rooted in history", using selective 19th century history to deny rights in the 21st century, while ignoring that almost nothing about modern life (privacy, equality, technology, medicine) is "deeply rooted" in 1868. The decision demonstrated how interpretive methodology becomes ideology: textualism and originalism as tools to dismantle rights rather than neutral interpretive frameworks.

The presidential immunity decision similarly showed judicial overreach: inventing sweeping immunity for "official acts" without constitutional text, creating a tiered immunity system nowhere in the Constitution, and effectively placing presidents above the law based on policy preferences about executive function. The dissent noted this creates precisely the monarchical power the framers explicitly rejected. These are not marginal decisions or modest adjustments, they're fundamental restructurings of constitutional law by a court operating without meaningful constraints.

The term limits proposal addresses the root structural problem: lifetime appointment. The framers designed lifetime tenure to ensure independence from political pressure, before modern life expectancy made "lifetime" mean 30-40+ year careers. Current practice creates perverse incentives: presidents appoint the youngest possible ideologues to maximize decades of influence, confirmation battles become apocalyptic because each appointment reshapes law for a generation, justices strategically time retirement to ensure same-party replacement, and age-related decline becomes constitutional crisis as justices serve into their 80s rather than voluntarily retiring.

The 18-year staggered term proposal solves these problems: each presidential term includes two appointments (predictable, regular), appointments are still secure (18 years is longer than most careers), but no single president dominates the court for decades, strategic retirement timing becomes less important, and regular turnover ensures the court gradually reflects democratic change rather than being frozen in the ideology of past political moments. This doesn't make the court "political", it makes appointments regular rather than arbitrary based on when justices die or retire. The court's composition would still shift slowly, but it wouldn't be possible for one or two presidents to lock in a supermajority for a generation.

The binding ethics code addresses the reality that Supreme Court justices currently operate with fewer ethical constraints than any other federal judges. Lower court judges are bound by the Code of Conduct for U.S. Judges. Supreme Court justices have "voluntary" ethics guidance with no enforcement. The result has been predictable: undisclosed luxury gifts and travel from wealthy donors with interests before the court, real estate transactions with parties in pending cases, spouse involvement in political activities directly related to cases, outside income from ideologically aligned organizations, financial conflicts not disclosed or recused, and public statements indicating prejudgment on issues likely to come before the court. When challenged, justices simply assert they've done nothing wrong and refuse recusal, with no review or consequence possible.

The binding code would establish clear rules: financial disclosure requirements matching or exceeding those for other federal officials, prohibition on gifts above minimal value from anyone with potential business before the court, prohibition on outside income from ideologically aligned organizations, restrictions on political activity and public statements on issues likely to come before the court, mandatory disclosure of spouse's income and activities, clear recusal requirements for financial conflicts or family involvement, and enforcement mechanisms including ethics investigations, public censure, removal from specific cases, and potential impeachment for egregious violations. The goal is not to micromanage justices, but to establish baseline ethical standards comparable to those governing every other public official.

The recusal standards address the current voluntary system where justices decide their own conflicts. Recent examples include: Justice Thomas not recusing despite his wife's active involvement in efforts to overturn the 2020 election in cases about that exact topic, justices refusing recusal despite receiving gifts or payments from parties or ideological allies with interests in pending cases, justices hearing cases involving companies where they own stock, and justices remaining on cases where their prior public statements indicate they've prejudged the issue. When challenged, justices simply decline recusal with no review possible.

Mandatory recusal standards would establish clear triggers: direct financial interest in the outcome, immediate family member with financial or professional interest in the outcome, prior participation in the case (as attorney, consultant, lower court judge), public statements indicating prejudgment on the specific legal issue, receipt of substantial gifts or payments from parties or their representatives, and reasonable appearance of impartiality compromised. An independent review mechanism would assess contested recusal decisions, with public documentation of the basis for recusing or declining to recuse. Consequences for inappropriate refusal to recuse would include reversal on appeal, public censure, and potential disciplinary action for repeated violations.

The interpretive standards reform addresses how strict textualism has been weaponized to dismantle rights and undermine clear legislative intent. Textualism claims to interpret laws based solely on the text, ignoring context, purpose, legislative history, or consequences. In practice, this becomes a tool for results-oriented judging: selectively reading text in the most narrow way to reach preferred outcomes, ignoring obvious intent when inconvenient, using dictionary definitions from different eras to support conclusions, and claiming any consideration of purpose or consequence is "judicial activism."

The reform doesn't ban textualism or require ignoring statutory text. It requires courts to consider multiple interpretive tools: text (obviously), but also legislative intent, context, purpose, consequences, and common sense. When a statute says X, and all evidence shows Congress meant X to accomplish Y, and interpreting X to prevent Y makes no sense, courts should not contort text to reach absurd results. The goal is honest interpretation serving justice, not ideological outcomes dressed as neutral textualism.

The plain-language intent requirement encourages Congress to make interpretation easier by including explicit statements of purpose, intent, and context in legislation. Rather than leaving courts to guess at legislative intent from scattered floor statements, committee reports, and statutory text, Congress would include a "Plain Language Intent" section explaining: what problem the law addresses, what outcome it seeks to achieve, how provisions should be interpreted to accomplish that purpose, and what interpretations would subvert the law's intent. This doesn't bind courts to follow Congress's interpretation (separation of powers), but it creates a clear interpretive record making bad-faith distortion harder.

The venue reform addresses judge shopping: plaintiffs filing cases in districts chosen specifically because a single sympathetic judge can be assigned, then obtaining nationwide injunctions from that one judge affecting the entire country. Recent examples include immigration policy, environmental regulations, healthcare rules, and election laws all being blocked nationwide by single district judges in carefully chosen venues. The plaintiffs forum-shop, the judge issues a sweeping injunction, and national policy is paralyzed by one jurist's opinion.

Reform would require plaintiffs to demonstrate substantial local connection to the chosen venue (not just "harm could occur here" but actual localized harm or substantial local operations), limit the geographic scope of district court injunctions to the affected district or circuit (not nationwide from a single judge), or require multi-judge panels for cases seeking nationwide relief. Mandatory random case assignment (JUD-VEN-002) closes the related loophole of strategic filing timing within a district to target specific judges. Automatic reassignment as the enforcement remedy preserves the plaintiff's right to litigate while eliminating the benefit of gaming the assignment system.

Court structure reform addresses two distinct but related structural failures. First, the court-packing risk: Article III vests judicial power in "one supreme Court" and "such inferior Courts as the Congress may from time to time ordain and establish," but specifies no number of Supreme Court justices. Congress has changed the number seven times since 1789, most recently in 1869. A simple-majority Senate and president can legally add justices, neutralizing a court of the opposite ideological alignment within a single Congress. The Merrick Garland episode (2016) and the Amy Coney Barrett confirmation (2020) together illustrate the full arc of the problem: the Senate blocked a Democratic nominee for nearly a year on the grounds that a presidential election year should allow voters to decide court composition, then confirmed a Republican nominee eight days before a presidential election, establishing that procedural norms around court appointments have no enforcement mechanism whatsoever and will be abandoned whenever they are inconvenient to the Senate majority. JUD-SIZ-001 addresses this directly by requiring a supermajority or constitutional amendment to change court size, raising the threshold above what any single governing coalition can achieve unilaterally.

The 18-year staggered term proposal (JUD-SIZ-002) solves the lifetime-appointment problem with minimal disruption to judicial independence: 18 years is longer than most careers, each presidential term yields exactly two appointments, no single president can dominate the court for a generation, and strategic retirement timing becomes less consequential. Comparative evidence from peer democracies is instructive: Germany's Federal Constitutional Court justices serve single 12-year terms; the European Court of Human Rights and Court of Justice of the EU use similar non-renewable term structures. These courts are not uniformly seen as politically compromised, they are generally regarded as legitimate, independent, and high-quality. The U.S. resistance to term limits has no parallel in peer judicial systems and reflects institutional inertia rather than demonstrated superiority.

The shadow docket has grown dramatically as a vehicle for major policy decisions. The Supreme Court's use of the emergency docket, applications for stays, injunctions pending appeal, and similar emergency relief decided without full briefing, oral argument, or signed majority opinions, expanded from roughly 8 orders per term in the early 2000s to dozens of significant policy orders per term by the early 2020s[6]. Shadow docket orders have blocked major regulatory and executive actions, reinstated policies on immigration, public health, and environmental protection, and shaped national law without the transparency of the merits process. The unsigned, unreasoned nature of shadow docket orders makes judicial review and public accountability nearly impossible. JUD-SHD-001 addresses the core problem (no process, no reasoning) and JUD-SHD-002 addresses the geographic scope problem (nationwide injunctions with no expedited review).

The nomination and confirmation reforms address the breakdown of the Senate's advice-and-consent function. The Merrick Garland precedent established that the Senate can simply refuse to hold hearings indefinitely; 293 days elapsed between Garland's nomination and the 2016 election, during which no hearing was scheduled. The Amy Coney Barrett confirmation proceeded in 27 days. The gap between these two timelines demonstrates that procedural norms are applied selectively based on which party benefits. JUD-NOM-001's automatic confirmation default after 90 days inverts the incentive structure: the Senate can no longer benefit from obstruction without cost. Merit-based screening commissions (JUD-NOM-002) introduce professional quality assessment that is standard in many state judicial selection systems and in bar association ratings, which have historically improved confirmation quality. Pre-confirmation disclosure (JUD-NOM-003) addresses the pattern of financial ties that emerge after confirmation, the more cost-effective intervention is disclosure before the Senate votes, when scrutiny is highest and the nominee has maximum incentive to be forthcoming.

The oversight mechanisms establish independent review for judicial conduct without undermining judicial independence on legal decisions. The distinction is crucial: independence means judges can't be punished for their legal decisions, even unpopular or wrong ones (reversible on appeal, not through discipline). But independence doesn't mean zero accountability for unethical conduct, conflicts of interest, bias, or abuse of office. Oversight mechanisms would include: independent inspector general or ethics office to investigate conduct complaints, transparent process for reviewing ethics concerns with public documentation, power to recommend sanctions (censure, fine, removal from specific cases), and clear distinction between reviewable conduct (ethics, conflicts, bias) versus protected judicial decisions (legal interpretations, rulings).

The transparency requirements ensure courts operate visibly. Current practice already requires publishing opinions and disclosing some financial information, but compliance is inconsistent and enforcement is weak. Strengthened transparency would mandate: timely publication of all decisions with full reasoning (not just orders), financial disclosures covering all sources of income and substantial gifts with regular updates, documentation of recusal decisions and reasons, disclosure of contacts with parties or interested persons in pending cases, and public records of ethical complaints and their resolution. This allows public and professional scrutiny without interfering with judicial decision-making.

Throughout the policy area, the design challenge is balancing independence with accountability. Judges must be free to make unpopular decisions without fear of retaliation, that's judicial independence and it's essential. But judges must also follow ethical standards, avoid conflicts of interest, recuse when appropriate, and interpret law honestly rather than imposing ideological outcomes. The reforms target the accountability gap while preserving legitimate independence: term limits provide regular turnover without making judges responsive to immediate political pressure, ethics codes constrain conduct without dictating legal decisions, recusal standards prevent biased judges from hearing cases without preventing judges from having legal philosophies, interpretive standards require honest reasoning without mandating specific interpretations, and oversight mechanisms investigate unethical conduct without reviewing legal correctness.

The pressure-test lessons informing this policy area include: lifetime appointment plus polarization creates incentives for extreme ideological appointments; voluntary ethics standards without enforcement are worthless; interpretive methodologies become tools for imposing preferred outcomes unless constrained; strategic behavior (judge shopping, recusal refusal, retirement timing) will occur unless structurally prevented; transparency must be mandatory because voluntary disclosure produces selective information; and courts will accumulate power unless structurally limited, the current Supreme Court is extraordinarily activist in overturning precedent, inventing doctrines, and reshaping law, while claiming to practice judicial restraint.
