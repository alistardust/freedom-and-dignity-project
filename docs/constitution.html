Finishing What Roosevelt Started

New Bill of Rights

The original Bill of Rights limited what government could do to you. This one adds what it must guarantee.

The founding generation enumerated rights that mattered in 1791. The New Deal generation began to extend them — but never finished. The unfinished project is ours to complete.

This proposed New Bill of Rights is not a constitutional amendment in the conventional sense. It is a framework — a statement of what rights must be guaranteed before any person can be considered genuinely free. Every policy proposal in this platform is grounded in one or more of these rights categories.

"We have come to a clear realization of the fact that true individual freedom cannot exist without economic security and independence. 'Necessitous men are not free men.' People who are hungry and out of a job are the stuff of which dictatorships are made."

— Franklin D. Roosevelt, State of the Union Address, January 11, 1944[1]

FDR proposed a Second Bill of Rights in 1944. It was never enacted. The core idea — that political freedom means nothing if you lack the material conditions to exercise it — remains the animating premise of this platform, eighty years later.

The Rights Framework

I

Economic Rights

  • The right to a living wage — compensation sufficient to meet basic needs without government subsidy
  • The right to organize, bargain collectively, and strike without employer retaliation
  • The right to healthcare — universal access to medical treatment as a baseline, not a benefit
  • The right to housing — shelter as a floor of human dignity, not a commodity subject to pure market logic
  • The right to education — publicly funded access to the knowledge needed for civic participation and economic self-sufficiency
  • The right to retirement security — a dignified life in old age, not dependent on investment returns
II

Civil and Political Rights

  • The right to vote — protected, affirmatively secured, and free from structural suppression
  • The right to equal treatment under law — without discrimination on the basis of race, gender, religion, national origin, disability, or sexual orientation
  • The right to due process — including the right to a fair hearing before deprivation of life, liberty, or property
  • The right to accountable government — public access to government records, decisions, and the identities of the people making them
III

Digital and Privacy Rights

  • The right to privacy — including digital privacy and freedom from mass surveillance by government or private actors
  • The right to data ownership — persons own their personal data; collection, sale, or use requires meaningful consent
  • The right to algorithmic transparency — when an algorithm materially affects a person's life, they have a right to know and to challenge it
  • The right to internet access — as essential infrastructure for civic participation and economic life
IV

Environmental Rights

  • The right to a clean and stable environment — air, water, and land free from contamination that harms human health
  • The right to a livable climate — enforceable government obligations to prevent catastrophic environmental degradation
  • Environmental justice — the principle that environmental harms may not be concentrated on communities by race or income
V

Bodily Autonomy

  • The right to reproductive autonomy — freedom to make decisions about one's own body without government interference
  • The right to gender self-determination — legal recognition of gender identity without government barriers
  • Freedom from forced medical intervention — including forced sterilization, forced medication, and medical experimentation without consent
  • The right to die with dignity — the right to make end-of-life decisions for oneself
These rights are not aspirations. They are demands. The government that cannot guarantee them is not a government of free people — it is a government of people who have learned to call their constraints normal.
Freedom and Dignity Project — Platform Statement

Why This Framework

Rights frameworks serve two purposes: they define what cannot be taken away, and they define what must be actively provided. The American tradition has been strong on the first and weak on the second. The result is a system that protects formal liberty while tolerating conditions that make that liberty meaningless for millions of people.

The five rights categories above are drawn from four sources of democratic legitimacy: FDR's 1944 Second Bill of Rights proposal[1]; the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948)[2]; more than eighty years of subsequent constitutional law and scholarship; and the contemporary reality of living in a digital economy with a destabilizing climate.[3]

They are not radical. Most are taken for granted in peer democracies. That they remain contested in the United States is not a sign of American exceptionalism — it is a sign of how much work remains to be done.
