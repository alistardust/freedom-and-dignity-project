Purpose

Government must answer to the public, not wealthy donors, dark-money networks, monopolistic corporations, foreign influence operations, or shadow lobbying systems that undermine democratic legitimacy.

Core Principle

Political power must not be controlled by concentrated wealth, hidden influence, private access networks, or covert manipulation. Democratic governance requires that decisions serve the public interest rather than private financial gain or concealed agenda-setting by powerful actors.

The Problem It Solves

The American system nominally limits corruption, but in practice leaves extensive room for legalized influence-buying, hidden money, informal access, weak enforcement, and tech-enabled manipulation. Federal lobbying spending exceeded $4.44 billion in 2024 and rose past $5 billion in 2025—both record territory—demonstrating how organizations with money already have a huge structural advantage in shaping legislation and regulation[1]. Dark-money groups, nonprofits, and shell companies poured a record $1.9 billion into the 2024 federal cycle, up from $1 billion in 2020, meaning vast amounts of election influence can be exercised without voters knowing who is behind it[2]. The revolving door between government and regulated industries creates self-dealing risks, while Citizens United-style distortions allow unlimited independent expenditures that function as political spending in practice. This pillar addresses the danger that government becomes answerable to whoever can pay the most, access the right networks, or manipulate information systems—rather than serving the public will.

Key Reform Areas

Ban corporate political donations and eliminate Super PACs

Cross-Sectional Policy Areas

Anti-corruption rules apply across every branch — campaign finance, lobbying, judicial ethics, and executive self-dealing are tightly linked.

Design Logic — How These Positions Work Together

Anti-corruption rules must regulate flows and networks, not just channels, because if one money channel is capped, influence will reroute elsewhere. The rules are structured around five internal modules: (1) campaign money and political spending broadly defined; (2) lobbying and shadow influence that includes consultants, strategic advisors, think-tank-style paid campaigns, and lawyers functioning as lobbyists; (3) revolving door and self-dealing protections; (4) transparency, media, AI disclosure, and information integrity; and (5) enforcement architecture with independent anti-corruption bodies. The system must close coordination loopholes (shared vendors, data, strategic alignment), require beneficial ownership transparency to prevent shell-company concealment, implement near-real-time disclosure so voters have information when it matters most, and address both current weaknesses (dark money, record lobbying, legal-but-corrupt access systems) and future threats (AI-enabled manipulation, tech-firm pre-capture of policy, richer systems driving even more influence spending).

ETH Ethics and Lobbying 0/10 active
CRPT-ETHL-0001 Included

Ban lobbyists from governing industries

This rule prevents people who worked as lobbyists for an industry from then taking government jobs regulating that same industry. It stops conflicts of interest where someone might favor their former clients over the public.

No person who has registered as a federal lobbyist — or worked as an employee, officer, or consultant of a registered lobbying firm — for a regulated industry within the preceding five years may serve as a senior official, administrator, political appointee, or career Senior Executive Service member with supervisory or policymaking authority over the regulation, investigation, or licensing of that industry; this prohibition applies equally to presidential appointments and career-track SES positions; OPM and the relevant Inspector General must verify compliance before confirmation or appointment and must publish a public certification; violations subject the appointing official to civil penalties and removal from office.

Prevents regulatory capture where individuals who lobbied for an industry then move into positions regulating that same industry, creating direct conflicts of interest and undermining public trust in neutral governance. [ADVERSARIAL] Gap: the rule covers federal regulatory agencies but not White House offices (NSC, OMB, OSTP) that effectively direct regulatory policy without formal agency jurisdiction over a single industry. Loophole: the HLOGA definition of "lobbying" requires at least 20% of time spent on lobbying activities; structuring prior work as "consulting," "strategic advisory," or "public affairs" rather than registered lobbying evades the registration threshold entirely. Abuse path: administrations that share an industry's policy preferences can issue ethics waivers to override the restriction, as both the Obama and Trump administrations did for numerous appointees; without a categorical ban on waivers in this context, the rule retains a structural escape valve. Unintended consequence: overly broad application may deter qualified technical experts with legitimate private-sector backgrounds from public service — distinguishing genuine regulatory capture from legitimate subject-matter expertise requires a case-by-case ethics review process that must be adequately resourced.

CRPT-ETHL-0002 Included

Ban stock trading by officials

This rule bans government officials from trading stocks while in office. It prevents them from using insider information from their government work to make money in the stock market.

All covered officials (as defined in CRPT-ETHL-0003 through CRPT-ETHL-0009) are prohibited from purchasing, selling, or otherwise trading individual stocks, bonds, commodity futures, options, derivatives, or other financial instruments tied to individual issuers during their tenure in office; this prohibition applies to transactions executed personally or through a nominee, agent, or any entity in which the official holds a controlling interest; covered officials must divest all prohibited holdings within 180 days of assuming office or transfer them to a qualified blind trust; the only permitted investment vehicles while in office are those described in CRPT-ETHL-0010; violations are referred to the relevant ethics authority and to DOJ for criminal investigation.

Prevents conflicts of interest where officials can use insider knowledge from their government roles to trade stocks, or where financial holdings create incentives to favor certain industries or companies in policy decisions. [ADVERSARIAL] Gap: the STOCK Act (2012) already prohibits insider trading by members of Congress and requires disclosure within 45 days, but reporting penalties are $200 — functionally no deterrent; a 2022 investigation found 54 sitting members had filed disclosures late. Loophole: officials can trade through a spouse, family member, or informally coordinated entity, or can shift holdings to sector-concentrated funds that create conflicts functionally identical to individual stock ownership without triggering the literal prohibition. Abuse path: an official in a compliant posture still makes policy decisions affecting markets; the rule prevents direct enrichment from insider knowledge but does not on its own prevent policy favoritism toward industries where covered household members retain holdings (see CRPT-ETHL-0007 through 0009). Edge case: cryptocurrency and digital asset trading must be explicitly covered — "securities" under current law does not automatically capture all digital assets, and this gap must be closed expressly.

CRPT-ETHL-0003 Included

Applies to Congress

This rule applies the stock trading and lobbying bans to all members of Congress. It ensures that lawmakers cannot profit from the policy decisions they make.

The stock trading prohibition (CRPT-ETHL-0002) applies to all members of the U.S. Senate and House of Representatives, all congressional employees above a GS-15 equivalent salary threshold, and all congressional committee staff who have access to non-public legislative information; members and covered staff must comply within 180 days of taking office or employment; the prohibition runs continuously during service and for one year following departure; violations are referred to the relevant chamber ethics committee and to DOJ for criminal investigation.

Stock trading and lobbying bans apply to all members of Congress, preventing legislators from profiting from their policy knowledge or industry relationships. [ADVERSARIAL] Gap: congressional staff below the salary threshold may still receive material non-public information and trade on it; committee staff in particular often have advance knowledge of legislation that directly moves market prices for specific issuers. Loophole: a member can hold sector-concentrated index funds or family investment vehicles that create conflicts functionally equivalent to individual stock ownership without triggering the literal prohibition; disclosure without a real ban leaves the underlying conflict intact. Abuse path: the House Ethics Committee has historically been nearly inert — fewer than 40 formal investigations since 2009 on over 200 OCE referrals, and fewer than 20 formal reprimands — making the referral mechanism an inadequate deterrent without independent prosecutorial authority. Unintended consequence: disclosure requirements without a trading ban may amplify market distortion by allowing sophisticated investors to front-run member disclosures, concentrating rather than dispersing the information advantage.

CRPT-ETHL-0004 Included

Applies to judges

This rule applies the stock trading and lobbying bans to federal judges. It protects the independence of the courts by preventing financial conflicts from influencing legal decisions.

The stock trading prohibition (CRPT-ETHL-0002) applies to all Article III federal judges (district, circuit, and Supreme Court justices), all Article I judges, and all magistrate judges with authority to issue binding rulings; covered judges must divest all prohibited holdings within one year of confirmation and may hold only vehicles described in CRPT-ETHL-0010; a judge who fails to divest must recuse from all cases involving issuers in which the judge or an immediate family member holds a prohibited interest; knowing failure to recuse constitutes grounds for removal.

Stock trading and lobbying bans apply to federal judges, ensuring judicial independence and preventing financial conflicts from influencing legal decisions. [ADVERSARIAL] Gap: financial disclosure for federal judges has historically been incomplete; Supreme Court justices were not required to report certain gifts and travel until the code of conduct adopted in November 2023, and that code lacks an independent enforcement mechanism with real consequences. Loophole: judges hold family trusts and retirement accounts managed by family members that may contain individual stocks, and recusal obligations are self-assessed without independent review — a judge can simply decline to recuse without consequence unless an appeal is filed and granted on the issue. Abuse path: a judge can strategically recuse from the most visible conflicts while hearing related cases involving the same industry or legal framework without a direct holding link, capturing the reputational benefit of apparent impartiality while retaining the material one. Edge case: inherited holdings, spousal assets, and investments established before appointment require a clear transition timeline and Judicial Conference guidance; pre-appointment holdings must be addressed explicitly with a defined compliance window.

CRPT-ETHL-0005 Included

Applies to executive branch

This rule applies the stock trading and lobbying bans to officials in the executive branch. It prevents conflicts of interest in regulatory and enforcement decisions.

The stock trading prohibition (CRPT-ETHL-0002) applies to all Senate-confirmed executive officials, all members of the Senior Executive Service (SES), all Schedule C political appointees, all White House staff above a GS-13 equivalent threshold, and all agency employees with direct regulatory, enforcement, or contracting authority over any market sector in which they hold a prohibited financial interest; covered officials must divest within 180 days of assuming their position; the prohibition extends for one year following departure from the covered position.

Stock trading and lobbying bans apply to executive branch officials, preventing conflicts of interest in regulatory and enforcement decisions. [ADVERSARIAL] Gap: the executive branch employs hundreds of thousands of people; "regulatory or contracting authority" as a threshold is subject to narrow agency interpretation that may exclude employees who materially influence outcomes for specific industries without holding formal final-decision authority. Loophole: political appointees can seek recusal from specific matters while retaining holdings in the affected sector — recusal is voluntary and largely honor-system-based without independent monitoring. Abuse path: agency leadership can issue permissive ethics waivers, narrow the definition of "regulatory authority" to exempt favored personnel, or decline to refer violations to OGE; without independent audit of compliance, the rule depends on good-faith self-enforcement. Unintended consequence: the broadest possible application — all federal employees in any agency with market-facing authority — could create significant recruitment difficulties at agencies like the SEC, CFTC, and Treasury that require technical expertise from the financial sector; a tiered approach by seniority and decision-making authority is more workable.

CRPT-ETHL-0006 Included

Applies to president and VP

This rule applies the stock trading and lobbying bans to the president and vice president. It ensures the highest offices in the country are free from financial conflicts of interest.

The stock trading prohibition (CRPT-ETHL-0002) applies to the President and Vice President with the most stringent requirements: both must fully divest all individual security holdings within 90 days of inauguration and may hold only the permitted vehicles described in CRPT-ETHL-0010; no ethics waiver may be granted for the President or Vice President under any circumstances; compliance is verified by OGE and independently audited by a bipartisan commission within 180 days of inauguration and annually thereafter; failure to comply constitutes an impeachable offense and subjects the officer to civil forfeiture of any financial benefit derived from a prohibited holding.

Stock trading and lobbying bans apply to the president and vice president, ensuring the highest offices are free from financial conflicts and cannot use their positions for personal enrichment. [ADVERSARIAL] Gap: the President's constitutional authority makes most civil remedies effectively unenforceable during the administration's term — the only practical real-time check is impeachment, which requires congressional political will that may be absent when the President's party controls either chamber. Loophole: a President who structures business interests through family members, licensing agreements, royalty streams, or deferred compensation arrangements may avoid the technical scope of a securities trading prohibition while continuing to profit from office; the emoluments framework (CRPT-EMOS-0001) must operate in conjunction to close this gap. Abuse path: the President controls DOJ, can delay enforcement investigations, and can pardon co-conspirators if criminal process reaches family members; the bipartisan audit commission is meaningful only if it has guaranteed subpoena power and a public reporting pathway that does not depend on executive cooperation. Unintended consequence: mandatory divestiture of complex multi-jurisdiction business holdings on an accelerated timeline may require below-market forced sales; the existing certificates of divestiture program should be extended and the timeline made realistic with verified milestones.

CRPT-ETHL-0007 Included

Applies to immediate family

This rule extends conflict-of-interest protections to immediate family members of covered officials. It prevents officials from using family members as a workaround to avoid the rules.

The stock trading prohibition extends to immediate family members of all covered officials (as defined in CRPT-ETHL-0003 through CRPT-ETHL-0006), defined as: minor children residing in the official's household; adult children who are financially dependent on the official or receive more than 50% of their income from the official; and any household member whose investment accounts are co-mingled with or directly accessible to the covered official; covered family members are not prohibited from holding permitted collective investment vehicles as described in CRPT-ETHL-0010; the official must certify annually that immediate family compliance is being maintained.

Stock trading and conflict-of-interest rules extend to immediate family members of covered officials to prevent circumvention through family proxies. [ADVERSARIAL] Gap: the rule covers dependent and financially connected children but not financially independent adult children — yet adult children of senior officials may trade on access to non-public information even without formal financial dependence on the parent official, particularly when they hold senior advisory or informal roles. Loophole: officials can establish paper financial independence for adult children — separate accounts, separate income, separate residence — to formally exclude them from coverage while coordinating informally. Abuse path: the rule creates an incentive to structure indirect benefit-sharing: an official's adult child who receives no formal income from the official but is given business opportunities, introductions, or deals through the official's position is not covered unless the financial nexus can be documented. Edge case: domestic partners, siblings, and parents of the covered official are addressed in companion cards CRPT-ETHL-0008 and CRPT-ETHL-0009 — those must be read together with this rule to eliminate gaps in household coverage.

CRPT-ETHL-0008 Included

Applies to spouses and partners

This rule specifically includes spouses and domestic partners in the conflict-of-interest protections. It closes a common loophole where officials claim they have no control over their partner's finances.

The stock trading prohibition applies to the legal spouse or domestic partner of all covered officials, regardless of whether the couple maintains nominally separate financial accounts; "domestic partner" includes persons in a civil union, registered domestic partnership, or long-term cohabitation arrangement in which the couple shares significant financial interests; a covered official's spouse or domestic partner must divest prohibited holdings or transfer them to a qualified blind trust within 180 days of the official assuming office; the compliance obligation runs through the official, who is responsible for certifying annual household compliance.

Specifically includes spouses and domestic partners in conflict-of-interest protections to close common loopholes where officials claim no control over spouse's financial activities. [ADVERSARIAL] Gap: the "separate accounts" claim is the most common evasion strategy; officials routinely assert that their spouse's investments are independent and that they exercise no control — this claim is structurally implausible in most financially integrated marriages and should require the official to affirmatively prove separation, not the government to disprove coordination. Loophole: a couple can formally manage finances through separate legal accounts, satisfying the technical rule while continuing to share the financial benefit of prohibited investments through joint consumption, estate planning, and shared wealth accumulation. Abuse path: a covered official could technically comply by obtaining a legal separation from their spouse without divorce, removing the legal relationship that triggers coverage while maintaining the underlying partnership. Unintended consequence: applying a prohibition to a private individual who holds no government office and made no direct choice to enter public service implicates personal autonomy; compliance obligations must run through the official rather than being directly and independently imposed on the private spouse.

CRPT-ETHL-0009 Included

Applies to extended relationships

This rule extends scrutiny to broader family and financial relationships when there is evidence of coordination or benefit-sharing. It prevents officials from evading rules through distant relatives or associates.

Where there is documented evidence — through financial disclosure, public records, or enforcement investigation — of active financial coordination, profit-sharing, or asset co-management between a covered official and any person not covered by CRPT-ETHL-0007 or CRPT-ETHL-0008, the relevant enforcement authority may extend the prohibition to that person's holdings on a fact-specific basis; "extended relationships" include siblings, parents, adult children not otherwise covered, ongoing business partners, and any person who has received a material financial transfer from the covered official within the preceding two years [VERIFY threshold]; automatic application does not occur — a showing of actual coordination is required before extension.

Extends scrutiny to broader family and financial relationships where there is evidence of coordination or benefit-sharing, preventing evasion through extended networks. [ADVERSARIAL] Gap: the "documented evidence of coordination" requirement before extension is necessary to avoid constitutional overreach but creates an enforcement gap — sophisticated actors will avoid leaving a written record of coordination, and the rule cannot reach informal understandings or anticipated future benefits that leave no paper trail. Loophole: a covered official who routes indirect financial benefit through an extended family member or business partner can insulate the relationship by ensuring no formal written agreement exists, leaving enforcement dependent on inference from financial records that may not be fully accessible. Abuse path: this authority could be weaponized — an adversarial administration could initiate investigations into the extended family networks of political critics under this provision, creating a chilling effect on legitimate political activity by family members of officials. Edge case: "material financial transfer" requires a defined monetary threshold [VERIFY: likely $10,000 or more per year] indexed to the regulated sector value at stake; without a defined threshold, any gift or routine family transaction creates ambiguous coverage.

CRPT-ETHL-0010 Included

Allow only blind collective investment

This rule allows covered officials to hold investments only in blind trusts or broad index funds where they cannot influence individual holdings. It prevents officials from directing investments to benefit themselves.

Covered officials and their covered family members (as defined in CRPT-ETHL-0003 through CRPT-ETHL-0009) may hold investments only in: (a) broad-based index funds tracking the entire U.S. or global market that hold at least [VERIFY: 100] constituent securities and are not sector-specific ETFs; (b) U.S. Treasury securities, TIPS, and U.S. savings bonds; (c) FDIC-insured money market and deposit accounts; and (d) assets held in a qualified blind trust meeting OGE standards with an independent trustee who has no prior financial relationship with the official or their family; real property used as a primary or personal-use residence is excluded from the prohibition; all other investment holdings must be divested within the applicable compliance period.

Permits covered officials to hold investments only in blind trusts or broad-based collective investment vehicles (like index funds) where they cannot influence or know specific holdings, ensuring complete separation from potential conflicts. [ADVERSARIAL] Gap: "broad-based index fund" is not self-defining; sector-specific ETFs — energy sector ETFs, healthcare ETFs, financial sector ETFs — could be argued to qualify as "index funds" even though they create concentrated exposure to sectors directly regulated by the covered official; the rule must explicitly prohibit sector-specific funds and require true market-wide diversification. Loophole: the blind trust exception requires an "independent trustee," but independence is not self-enforcing — a trustee with ongoing business relationships with the covered official or their family is not functionally independent; OGE's historical review of trustee independence has been superficial and must be strengthened. Abuse path: a covered official can establish a nominally compliant blind trust while structuring the initial asset transfer to concentrate in preferred holdings before forfeiting visibility into specific positions; the composition of assets transferred into the trust must be independently reviewed and constrained at inception. Unintended consequence: prohibiting all individual security ownership — including long-held diversified portfolios — may require forced sales at suboptimal market timing; the existing certificates of divestiture program should be extended to all covered officials to address the tax-basis impact of compelled divestiture.

CRPT-FINC-0002 Partial

Ban Super PACs

Eliminates Super PACs that allow unlimited political spending by individuals, unions, and corporations under the guise of "independence," closing a major channel for concentrated wealth to influence elections.

CRPT-FINC-0003 Included

Limit individual donations

This rule sets strict limits on how much money individuals can donate to political campaigns. It prevents wealthy people from having outsized influence through large contributions.

Individual aggregate contributions to any single federal candidate committee, national party committee, or political action committee in a single federal election cycle may not exceed limits set by the FEC and indexed annually to inflation; "bundling" of contributions by registered lobbyists and their associated persons must be disclosed in real-time FEC filings identifying the bundler; foreign nationals are categorically prohibited from making any contribution or expenditure in connection with a federal election; online platforms processing political contributions must implement foreign-donation detection and verification protocols; civil penalties for knowing violations are set at three times the excess contribution amount.

Establishes strict caps on individual political donations to prevent wealthy individuals from exercising outsized influence through large contributions, ensuring more equal political voice. [ADVERSARIAL] Gap: the Supreme Court in McCutcheon v. FEC (2014) struck down aggregate contribution limits across all candidates in a cycle as unconstitutional; any aggregate cap must be designed in light of that ruling and will face immediate constitutional challenge — per-candidate limits survive, but cross-candidate aggregates do not under current doctrine. Loophole: wealthy donors can route contributions through multiple family members, through bundling by associated persons, and through nominally independent expenditure accounts to effectively concentrate political influence above the formal per-candidate limit without technically violating it. Abuse path: contribution limits without accompanying spending limits leave the field open to unlimited independent expenditures — a candidate who cannot accept large direct donations can still benefit from unlimited coordinated-looking "independent" spending that their allies can effectively direct without formal coordination. Unintended consequence: hard contribution limits tend to push money into harder-to-track channels — dark money, Super PACs, and social welfare organizations — rather than eliminating the underlying political spending; robust disclosure requirements may be a more durable and constitutionally stable reform in a post-Citizens United legal environment.

CRPT-FINC-0004 Included

Limit political ad spending

This rule considers limiting how much can be spent on political advertisements. It aims to prevent elections from becoming contests of who can spend the most money.

All political advertising expenditures in connection with federal elections above $1,000 must be disclosed to the FEC in real-time filings identifying the purchaser, amount, platform, and targeting parameters; broadcasters, cable operators, satellite providers, and online platforms with more than [VERIFY: 50 million] U.S. monthly active users must maintain a publicly searchable political ad archive including purchaser identity, amounts paid, audience targeting criteria, and message content; foreign-owned or foreign-directed entities are categorically prohibited from purchasing political advertising on any platform; the FEC must update electioneering communication rules to cover all digital advertising formats including paid search, social media, streaming, and algorithmically promoted content.

Considers limits on advertisement spending and broadcast/streaming political ad saturation to prevent elections from becoming pure spending contests and reduce the advantage wealth provides in shaping public discourse. [ADVERSARIAL] Gap: the Buckley v. Valeo (1976) line of cases treats campaign expenditure limits as restrictions on protected political speech, making hard spending caps constitutionally vulnerable; disclosure requirements — which Citizens United upheld even while striking spending limits — are the more legally defensible and durable reform instrument and should be the primary mechanism here. Loophole: online platforms implement ad disclosure rules inconsistently, and platforms in emerging media formats (podcasts, newsletters, streaming, algorithmically amplified content) are not covered by broadcast-era electioneering communication rules; the rule must extend expressly to all digital advertising channels regardless of format. Abuse path: political messaging structured as "issue advertising" rather than express advocacy for or against a specific candidate has historically evaded electioneering communication rules; the Citizens United decision made this distinction largely unenforceable as applied to corporate and 501(c)(4) spending, and the rule must be drafted to capture functional electioneering regardless of how it is labeled. Unintended consequence: ad spending limits systematically advantage incumbents with free media coverage and name recognition over challengers; any spending restriction regime must account for this structural bias and include provisions to ensure challenger access to political communication.

CRPT-FINC-0005 Included
🔵 Proposal — Under Review

Mandatory disclosure of 501(c)(4) and dark-money political spending — close the anonymous-donor loophole

This rule requires nonprofit organizations that spend money on elections to publicly disclose their donors. It closes the "dark money" loophole that allows anonymous political spending.

Any 501(c)(4) "social welfare" organization, 501(c)(6) trade association, or other tax-exempt entity that makes independent expenditures or electioneering communications exceeding $10,000 in aggregate during a federal election cycle must publicly disclose the identity of all donors who contributed $1,000 or more to the organization in that cycle, within 48 hours of each expenditure. Disclosure must be filed with the FEC in a publicly searchable, machine-readable database. Organizations may not use conduit contributions, pass-through accounts, or multi-layered entity structures to obscure the identity of the ultimate human donor. Violations carry civil penalties of three times the undisclosed amount and potential criminal referral for knowing violations.

The current system allows 501(c)(4) organizations to spend unlimited sums on elections without disclosing donors to the public, creating the primary dark-money vehicle in U.S. politics. The Brennan Center documented record dark-money spending of $1.9 billion in the 2024 federal cycle.[2] The IRS "primarily engaged in social welfare" standard has proven unenforceable as a practical limit on political spending. Donor disclosure does not restrict political speech protected by the First Amendment — it ensures voters know who is funding the messages they receive and holds dark-money donors accountable to the public whose votes they are trying to influence. [ADVERSARIAL] Gap: the proposed $10,000 donor disclosure threshold applies to individual contributions but not to the portion of an overall contribution that is fungibly allocated to political spending — a 501(c)(4) can accept one large general-fund donation and allocate only a fraction to elections, obscuring which donors effectively funded the political activity. Loophole: a dark-money network can use multiple layered entities — one 501(c)(4) contributing to another 501(c)(4) which then makes the expenditure — and the 48-hour disclosure rule captures the immediate spending entity but not the ultimate human donor unless the conduit prohibition is aggressively and independently enforced. Abuse path: a future administration hostile to disclosure can defund or staff-starve FEC enforcement, rendering the rule nominal; the FEC's structural deadlock problem — three commissioners per party, requiring bipartisan agreement to act — means enforcement often fails exactly when violations are most politically charged. Unintended consequence: aggressive disclosure requirements that expose donors to harassment or retaliation may chill legitimate political participation by unpopular minority viewpoints; the rule must include a hardship exemption pathway for donors who can demonstrate a credible threat of retaliation, consistent with NAACP v. Alabama (1958).

FAR Foreign Agent Registration and Influence 0/3 active
CRPT-FARS-0001 Included

Strengthen FARA enforcement with mandatory registration and real penalties

This rule strengthens the Foreign Agents Registration Act by requiring mandatory criminal penalties for people who fail to register when working for foreign governments. It closes loopholes that let foreign influence go undisclosed.

The Foreign Agents Registration Act (22 U.S.C. §§ 611–621) must be strengthened with mandatory criminal referral for willful non-registration, expanded DOJ enforcement capacity with dedicated investigative staff, and mandatory public disclosure in a real-time searchable database. Fines must be set at levels that create genuine deterrence. The NSD/FARA Unit must be resourced to initiate registrations where agents fail to self-register.

The DOJ Inspector General found in 2016 that FARA enforcement was inadequate, with few criminal prosecutions and widespread non-compliance among persons acting as unregistered foreign agents.[3] High-profile prosecutions of political consultants working for foreign governments highlighted how registration requirements had become effectively optional for well-connected actors. Robust FARA enforcement is the first line of defense against foreign governments purchasing political influence through domestic intermediaries. [ADVERSARIAL] Gap: even with expanded NSD staffing, FARA enforcement depends primarily on voluntary self-registration — proactive identification of unregistered agents requires ongoing intelligence and law enforcement attention that competes with counterterrorism and other national security priorities. Loophole: agents can structure their work to stay below the FARA threshold by characterizing activities as "legal representation," "academic research," or "commercial consulting" rather than political influence activity; the statute's "political activities" definition has proven difficult to apply at the margins of these categories. Abuse path: FARA enforcement historically tracks political winds — prosecutions have spiked during periods of congressional or media attention and receded otherwise; without a mandatory audit-and-enforcement cycle independent of political pressure, the statute will remain selectively enforced. Edge case: non-profit organizations, think tanks, and academic institutions that receive foreign government funding and engage in policy advocacy may be functionally acting as foreign agents but are rarely treated as such; the rule must clarify whether and how FARA applies to these entities and at what funding threshold.

CRPT-FARS-0002 Included

Close the "lobbying exemption" that allows foreign agent work without FARA registration

This rule closes a loophole that allows people working for foreign governments to avoid registering as foreign agents. It ensures all foreign lobbying is disclosed to the public.

Persons acting on behalf of foreign principals must register under FARA rather than the lesser Lobbying Disclosure Act. The LDA exemption from FARA registration — which applies when foreign-agent activity is characterized as "lobbying" — must be eliminated for all work on behalf of foreign governments, political parties, or state-owned enterprises. Only work entirely on behalf of foreign commercial entities without a government-influence purpose may remain in the LDA category.

The "LDA exemption" allows many persons representing foreign government interests to register under the lighter-touch Lobbying Disclosure Act instead of FARA, which has more stringent disclosure, labeling, and semi-annual reporting requirements. This loophole was documented by DOJ's own Inspector General as a significant enforcement gap. Closing it does not restrict legitimate lobbying; it ensures that influence activity on behalf of foreign governments is subject to the transparency Congress specifically created for that purpose. [ADVERSARIAL] Gap: closing the LDA exemption for foreign government work still leaves foreign commercial entities — companies, sovereign wealth funds, and state-owned enterprises structured as nominally private corporations — potentially eligible to use the LDA pathway even when their activities directly advance foreign government interests. Loophole: a foreign-government-linked agent can structure the engagement as representing a nominally private foreign commercial client rather than the foreign government directly, maintaining LDA eligibility even if the foreign government is the ultimate directing principal. Abuse path: the distinction between "foreign government interest" and "foreign commercial interest" is fact-intensive and can be litigated; well-resourced registrants will contest every marginal case, creating enforcement delays that allow the underlying influence activity to continue during litigation. Unintended consequence: stricter FARA requirements may create disproportionate compliance burdens for allied governments and international organizations with legitimate U.S. engagement interests; the rule should include a streamlined compliance pathway for registrants with demonstrably low-risk profiles while maintaining full disclosure requirements.

CRPT-FARS-0003 Included

Ban former senior officials from registering as foreign agents for five years

This rule bans former senior government officials from working as foreign agents for five years after leaving office. It prevents officials from immediately cashing in on their government connections by working for foreign governments.

Research & Context

The structural weaknesses in American anti-corruption systems are not hypothetical—they are operating at record scale right now. Reuters reported that federal lobbying spending exceeded $4.44 billion in 2024, then rose past $5 billion in 2025, both record territory. That does not prove every lobbyist is corrupt, but it does show that organizations with money already have a huge structural advantage in shaping legislation and regulation[1]. The Brennan Center reported that dark-money groups, nonprofits, and shell companies poured a record $1.9 billion into the 2024 federal cycle, up from the previous record of $1 billion in 2020[2]. That means a very large amount of election influence can still be exercised without voters knowing who is behind it. These numbers are not isolated accidents—they are signs of a system whose incentives are moving in the wrong direction, where the richer and more polarized the system gets, the more valuable influence becomes, which drives even more spending on lobbying and electioneering.

The basic pattern is that nominally regulated systems leave multiple pathways for "legal but corrupt" influence. Citizens United bars government from prohibiting independent expenditures by corporations and unions, while current FEC rules still allow substantial political giving and reporting structures that do not fully solve hidden influence. If you regulate registered lobbyists, influence moves into shadow lobbying: consultants, lawyers, trade groups, think tanks, former staffers, media surrogates, donors, and informal policy networks who shape outcomes without always triggering classic lobbying definitions. Reuters' reporting on the overall scale and growth of lobbying underscores the incentive for this ecosystem to expand and professionalize. Similarly, if you ban obvious quid pro quo corruption, influence simply shifts to access-buying without explicit deals—the public rarely sees a written contract saying "donation for favor." Instead, the practical exchange is access, agenda-setting, favorable staffing, favorable rulemaking, softer enforcement, or delay. That is why this pillar must not be limited to bribery-style corruption but must use Brennan's better definition: corruption as decisions motivated by money or undue private gain.

Brennan warned in early 2025 that federal contracts, tariffs, regulations, and taxes can be used to benefit donors, senior officials, and even the president—exactly the kind of "technically legal but democratically poisonous" influence system this pillar must target. The revolving door and self-dealing risk inside government creates scenarios where public office is used to enrich self, family, donors, or business networks. The only way to counter this is with mandatory disclosure of financial interests, real divestment or blind trusts, actual recusal requirements with teeth, and criminal and financial penalties for violations. Cooling-off periods must be robust enough that former officials cannot immediately monetize their insider knowledge and relationships with regulated industries.

Looking forward, the biggest near-term threat is AI-enabled manipulation. Brennan warned in 2025 that AI did not fully disrupt the 2024 election, but its effects are likely to be greater going forward. Reuters reported that by March 2026, AI deepfakes were already blurring reality in U.S. midterm campaigns, with weak or patchwork regulation. That creates a new corruption vector: not just money buying ads, but money buying cheap, scalable, plausible deception. This is corruption becoming informational, not just financial—coordinated persuasion campaigns using AI to generate fake video, fake audio, fake testimonials, fake grassroots support, and synthetic media at industrial scale. A second future threat is that tech and AI firms can shape the rules before the public even understands the stakes. Reuters and other reporting show major industries are ramping up lobbying around AI policy, meaning the next generation of governance fights may be pre-captured by firms with the biggest lobbying budgets, data access, and platform control.

The enforcement lesson from anti-corruption work generally is that a rule without credible enforcement becomes a speed bump. If you create an enforcement body, bad actors will test whether it is captured, deadlocked, underfunded, or politically intimidated. That is why enforcement architecture must include an independent anti-corruption enforcement body with protected funding that does not depend on partisan appropriations, subpoena power, mandatory public reporting, automatic triggers for investigation, criminal penalties and financial penalties, whistleblower protection, and structures that ensure enforcement does not deadlock or depend on partisan goodwill. Real-time or near-real-time disclosure is also critical, especially close to elections and for major issue campaigns. Existing reporting rules matter, but lagged transparency still leaves voters in the dark when it counts most—reporting that happens after an election often does not solve the democratic harm.

This pillar also recognizes that concentration of private economic power can become a democratic-governance problem. Strengthening antitrust enforcement, breaking up monopolies, and preventing excessive consolidation in key industries, supply chains, and essential services is necessary to protect competition and small actors, and to limit the ability of a handful of corporations to dominate daily life, pricing, production quality, access, or distribution. When monopolistic power becomes entrenched, it translates directly into political influence that ordinary citizens cannot match.

The pressure-test lessons that must be incorporated: "independent" spending is easy to fake through vendors, data, or informal signaling, so anti-coordination rules must be strict; shell-company and proxy-ownership rules must be closed tightly with beneficial ownership tracing; registered-lobbyist rules alone are not enough because shadow lobbying can replace them; anti-corruption rules must address "legal but corrupt" systems built around access and agenda control, not only explicit bribery; disclosure that arrives too late does not protect democracy; and AI and platform-based manipulation are future-proofing necessities, not optional extras. This pillar has been pressure-tested against current American weak points (record dark money, record lobbying, Citizens United loopholes, revolving door risks) and likely future threats (AI manipulation, tech-firm lobbying dominance, escalating influence spending in polarized systems). The result is a comprehensive framework that closes loopholes, regulates flows and networks rather than just channels, and builds enforcement structures capable of withstanding legal and political challenges.
