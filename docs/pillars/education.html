Purpose

Establish universal access to high-quality education as a guaranteed right, ensuring every person—regardless of wealth, geography, race, or disability—can access the learning they need for economic mobility, civic participation, and full human development. This pillar addresses structural inequity in school funding, predatory student lending, secular public schooling, and the full pipeline from K-12 through higher education and lifelong learning.

Core Principle

Educational opportunity must not be determined by the wealth of one's family or zip code. Every student deserves high-quality instruction, safe learning environments, and the ability to access higher education and workforce training without being burdened by predatory debt or arbitrary exclusion.

The Problem It Solves

  • Public school quality is directly tied to local property wealth, producing dramatically unequal educational outcomes by geography, race, and income. Schools in lower-income areas systematically lack resources, qualified teachers, and physical infrastructure.
  • Higher education costs have risen far faster than wages, trapping millions in decades of student debt from institutions that frequently fail to deliver the promised economic returns—especially for-profit colleges.
  • Vocational and technical pathways are systematically undervalued and underfunded, forcing students toward costly four-year degrees even where trade credentials better serve their goals.
  • Civic education, media literacy, and science education are under political attack, threatening students' ability to reason, evaluate evidence, and participate in democratic life.

Key Reform Areas

Universal K-12 funding equity — break the property-wealth-to-school-quality pipeline

Education reform intersects with workforce pipelines, technology access, equitable school funding through taxation, and civil rights in access and opportunity.

Design Logic — How These Positions Work Together
  • Funding equity requires breaking the link between local property wealth and school quality through state and federal equalization mechanisms, with additional resources for high-need schools.
  • Debt elimination and free community college address the higher education access crisis directly—eliminating the predatory loan trap and ensuring vocational and community college pathways are accessible to all.
  • Secular public schooling and evidence-based curriculum protect both religious freedom (by not imposing religion) and scientific literacy (by requiring empirical instruction).
  • Governance accountability ensures that communities have input without enabling discrimination, resource inequity, or political indoctrination.

States may nominally remove statutory restrictions while funding agencies that interpret management rights broadly to exclude the same subjects through administrative interpretation; federal enforcement must reach both statutory and de facto exclusions. Teachers in non-unionized states will not benefit from expanded scope of bargaining because there are no bargaining representatives to negotiate; scope expansion is only meaningful when paired with organizing protections in EDUC-TCBU-0001 through EDUC-TCBU-0004. Scope expansion without corresponding funding increases may result in bargaining over class size or workload that districts are genuinely unable to finance; state adequacy funding must accompany scope expansion. Research basis: Teachers work an average of 53 hours per week compared to 44 for comparably educated workers, with a significant portion spent on administrative tasks and non-instructional duties that could be addressed through bargaining (Diliberti, M. K., and Schwartz, H. L., 2024, Teacher well-being and intentions to leave in 2024, RAND Corporation).

EDUC-TCBU-0006 Included

Teachers who try to organize their colleagues or speak up through their union can face discipline, reassignment, or termination. This rule creates strong anti-retaliation protections so teachers can exercise their organizing rights without fear, and gives them the right to sue if their employer retaliates.

Federal law must prohibit any school district, charter school, or state education agency from disciplining, terminating, reassigning, reducing the pay of, or otherwise retaliating against any teacher for: (a) participating in or supporting union organizing activity; (b) serving as a union representative or officer; (c) filing grievances or unfair labor practice charges; (d) testifying or participating in any labor board proceeding; teachers who experience retaliation have a private right of action in federal district court with a statute of limitations of four years; remedies must include reinstatement, back pay, compensatory damages, punitive damages of up to three times back pay for willful violations, and mandatory attorney fees to prevailing plaintiffs; the employer bears the burden of proving by clear and convincing evidence that an adverse employment action was entirely unrelated to protected activity when the adverse action occurs within two years of protected activity; state Public Employment Relations Boards must accept and adjudicate retaliation complaints within 90 days with interim injunctive relief authority.

Adversarial review: The mixed-motive problem, where an employer has both protected and unprotected reasons for an adverse action, is a major enforcement challenge; the burden-shifting framework must be clearly drafted to prevent employers from manufacturing pretextual reasons for adverse actions taken in response to organizing. School districts will use normal evaluation and performance management processes to generate pretextual documentation for retaliation; a pattern of negative evaluations beginning after protected activity is itself evidence of retaliation and must be recognized as such. Teachers in at-will employment states or during probationary periods have the weakest protection, as employers can claim non-renewal without stated reason; the rule must explicitly cover non-renewal decisions as potential retaliation. Collective bargaining representatives in non-unionized states lack institutional protection during early organizing; the individual private right of action is essential in those contexts. EDU-TPPL Teacher Shortage and Pipeline Investment 0/5 proposed
EDUC-TPPL-0001 Included

Teacher residency programs are like medical residencies: student teachers spend a full year working alongside experienced teachers in real classrooms, with pay, before taking on their own classrooms. Mentor teacher supplements may be captured by administrators who designate themselves as supervisors; the rule must specify that mentors must be classroom teachers with defined duties. Districts under budget pressure may cut program fidelity by reducing mentoring time or shortening clinical hours; outcome-based renewal criteria must measure program fidelity, not just aggregate retention rates. The share of people who start a teacher preparation program and actually finish it has dropped from 41% to 27% in recent years. Many programs are under-resourced and fail to prepare teachers for the real demands of the classroom. This rule sets quality standards, requires programs to report completion rates, and ties federal investment to outcomes.

The Department of Education must establish minimum quality standards for all state-approved teacher preparation programs receiving federal funding under Title II of the Higher Education Act, including: (a) a minimum program completion rate of 40% of enrolled candidates measured three years after initial enrollment; (b) mandatory clinical practice of no fewer than 400 hours in a school setting, with no fewer than 200 hours in the subject area and grade level the candidate is being prepared to teach; (c) annual outcome reporting including first-year teacher employment rates, three-year teacher retention rates, and subject area placement rates, published publicly; programs failing to meet the 40% completion rate for two consecutive years must submit a required improvement plan; programs failing the rate for four consecutive years lose state approval eligibility for three years; Congress must appropriate no less than $50 million annually for Teacher Preparation Program Improvement Grants; programs with completion rates above 55% and three-year retention rates above 70% receive a quality bonus of up to $3,000 per completer.

Adversarial review: The 40% completion rate threshold may disproportionately penalize programs serving high-risk student populations who face greater barriers to completion, including financial pressures and family obligations; disaggregated completion data and context-sensitive monitoring are necessary. Small rural preparation programs may serve important specialized functions but have completion rates below threshold due to cohort size; a minimum cohort size for rate calculation is needed. Programs may inflate completion rates by reducing rigor or removing students unlikely to complete; monitoring should include graduate exam pass rates and employer satisfaction data, not just completion rates alone. The 400-hour clinical practice requirement may still be inadequate to prepare teachers for the full complexity of urban high-poverty schools; there is a case for 600-hour minimums based on medical residency analogues. Research basis: Teacher preparation program completion rates dropped from 41.2% in 2014-15 to approximately 26.9% in 2020-21; Title II data document approximately 590,158 total enrollees across 2,219 provider organizations (NCSL, 2024, Teacher shortage areas by state; U.S. Department of Education, 2024, Title II of the Higher Education Act).

Students do better academically and are more likely to graduate when taught by teachers who share their racial or ethnic background. Currently about 52% of students are students of color but only about 22% of teachers are. This rule invests in targeted programs to close that gap by recruiting, supporting, and retaining teachers of color.

Congress must establish a National Teacher Diversity Initiative funded at no less than $150 million annually, providing: (a) targeted scholarships of up to $20,000 for teacher candidates of color enrolled in accredited preparation programs, with no service requirement for candidates from high-need communities who subsequently teach in their home communities; (b) first-year induction mentoring pairing new teachers of color with experienced teachers of color in the same subject area and school type; (c) a Teacher of Color Retention Fund providing districts per-teacher grants of $5,000 annually for five years for each teacher of color hired and retained in a high-need school; the Department of Education must publish an annual National Teacher Diversity Report disaggregating teacher race, ethnicity, gender, and language proficiency by district, subject, and school poverty level; states receiving federal education funding must adopt and implement a state teacher diversity plan within three years, including measurable representation goals; districts where the share of teachers of color is more than 25 percentage points below the share of students of color must develop a targeted retention plan addressing pay equity, mentoring resources, administrative support, and assignment practices; the initiative must track representation at the school level, not just the district level, to prevent de facto segregation of diverse teachers into low-need schools within diverse districts.

Diversity initiatives focused on recruitment without addressing retention will not close the gap; Black and Native American teachers have lower retention rates than White teachers in many states, meaning recruitment must be paired with targeted retention investment. Teachers of color assigned predominantly to high-need schools face greater institutional challenges including lower administrative support and higher student poverty, creating a self-reinforcing retention disadvantage; assignment equity, not just recruitment equity, is essential. Financial incentives alone do not address the cultural isolation and professional marginalization many teachers of color report in predominantly White faculties; mentoring and professional community investments are essential complements. Research basis: In 2022-23, 52% of public school students were students of color, but only 21-22% of teachers were people of color; students of color demonstrate better academic outcomes, lower exclusionary discipline rates, and higher aspirations when taught by a same-race teacher (TNTP, 2024, Teacher diversity data; Economic Policy Institute, 2024, Improving teacher diversity is key to reducing racial disparities in academic outcomes); Black teachers' average salaries trail White teachers' by approximately $5,682 (RAND Corporation, 2024, Teacher pay: What K-12 teachers earn and what they think about it).

EDU-TWRK Teacher Retention and Working Conditions 0/6 proposed
EDUC-TWRK-0001 Included

Congress must fund class size reduction in kindergarten through grade 3 and in Title I schools at $3 billion annually, targeting maximums of 18 students per class in K-3 and 22 students in grades 4-12

Research shows that smaller classes, especially in early elementary school, produce large and lasting gains for students, especially students of color and students in poverty. This rule funds class size reduction where the evidence is strongest, sets enforceable maximums, and prevents states from gaming the system by shifting overcrowding elsewhere.

Congress must appropriate no less than $3 billion annually for the Class Size Reduction Initiative, providing weighted-formula grants to states and districts to reduce class sizes in: (a) all kindergarten through grade 3 classrooms to a maximum of 18 students per class; (b) all Title I-eligible schools in grades 4 through 12 to a maximum of 22 students per class; grants must be used exclusively to hire additional qualified teachers, not to pay existing teachers more, and not to fund administrative positions; states receiving grants must demonstrate within two years that class sizes in targeted grades and schools have reached target maximums in at least 75% of classrooms; states may not respond to grant receipt by increasing class sizes in non-targeted schools or grades to offset the cost; the Department of Education must audit state compliance with the anti-offset provision annually; districts must give priority in new teacher hiring to fully certified teachers; grant appropriations must be accompanied by corresponding increases in teacher pipeline funding to ensure qualified teachers are available.

Adversarial review (PAOS-TEST-0008): Class size reduction is the most expensive working conditions intervention and requires not only funding but a commensurate increase in qualified teacher supply; in the context of ongoing teacher shortages, reducing class sizes without addressing pipeline supply will create additional hiring pressure that districts meet with uncertified teachers, defeating the purpose. The $3 billion annual appropriation is likely insufficient to fund class size reduction at scale nationally; this amount should be treated as a minimum floor. Districts may comply by averaging class sizes within a school or grade while allowing individual classrooms to exceed the maximum; the rule must specify that the maximum applies to each individual classroom, not averages. Reduction mandates may trigger union negotiations over hiring criteria and scheduling that could delay implementation; labor relations planning must accompany implementation. Rural districts with small enrollments may already have small class sizes but still need additional qualified teachers; the grant formula must account for rural contexts. Research basis: The Tennessee STAR randomized experiment found students in classes of 13-17 significantly outperformed those in regular classes of 22-26, with benefits persisting into later grades; the Black-White achievement gap was reduced by as much as 56% for students who began school in small classes (Finn, J. D., and Achilles, C. M., 1990, Answers and questions about class size: A statewide experiment, American Educational Research Journal, 27(3), 557-577; Mosteller, F., 1995, The Tennessee study of class size in early school grades, Future of Children, 5(2), 113-127).

EDUC-TWRK-0002 Included

Public school teachers must be guaranteed a minimum of 90 minutes of protected, non-supervisory planning time per instructional day as a condition of states receiving federal Title I and Title II-A funding

Teachers do hours of work outside of teaching, including lesson planning, grading, and family communication. Many get little or no protected time during the school day to do this work, forcing it into evenings and weekends. This rule guarantees a protected block of daily planning time during school hours.

States receiving Title I or Title II-A federal funding must demonstrate within three years of enactment that all public school teachers employed in grades K through 12 receive a minimum of 90 minutes per instructional day of protected planning time during the school day; planning time must be free from supervisory duties, lunchroom monitoring, hallway supervision, and any other non-instructional assignment; teachers who teach three or more distinct preparation courses must receive an additional 20 minutes of planning time per additional preparation beyond two; planning time may not be used by administrators to schedule mandatory meetings except for once-weekly team meetings of no more than 45 minutes; violation of protected planning time constitutes a grievable working condition under any applicable collective bargaining agreement; in the absence of a collective bargaining agreement, violations are subject to complaint to the state education agency with remedies including compensatory time and civil fines of $500 per violation per teacher affected; the Department of Education must include planning time compliance as a component of its Title I monitoring visits.

Adversarial review: The 90-minute planning time minimum will be argued by districts to be operationally unworkable given scheduling constraints, particularly for specials teachers and those with large numbers of separate preparations; the rule must include transition timelines and technical assistance for complex scheduling contexts. Planning time mandates increase the number of staff needed to cover classes during planning periods, creating additional hiring demand in a context of shortage; districts must receive funding to support this staffing need. Administrators may schedule mandatory professional development during planning time to fill the period, effectively eliminating its protective purpose; the strict limitation on mandatory meetings is essential and must be actively enforced. Substitute shortages mean planning periods are frequently interrupted by emergency coverage requests; the rule should specify that teachers may not be required to cover classes during planning except in declared emergencies, with compensatory time required for any such coverage. The union grievance mechanism is only available in bargaining states; teachers in non-bargaining states need an alternative enforcement pathway through the state education agency that is operationally functional and not merely nominal.

EDUC-TWRK-0003 Included

All school districts receiving Title I funding must conduct biennial Teacher Administrative Burden Audits and implement reduction plans when teachers average more than eight hours weekly on non-instructional administrative tasks

Teachers spend hours each week on paperwork, data entry, and compliance tasks that have little connection to student learning. This rule requires districts to regularly review what administrative work they are imposing on teachers and eliminate tasks not supported by evidence of benefit to students.

All school districts receiving Title I federal funding must, beginning within 18 months of enactment and every two years thereafter, conduct a Teacher Administrative Burden Audit measuring the average weekly time teachers spend on: (a) standardized data entry and reporting to district systems; (b) compliance documentation not required by federal law; (c) non-instructional supervisory duties assignable to non-teaching staff; (d) mandatory professional development sessions not chosen or rated as valuable by participating teachers; districts must publish audit results publicly and present them to the school board; districts where teachers average more than eight hours per week on non-instructional administrative tasks must develop and implement a reduction plan within one year, targeting no more than six hours per week; the Department of Education must publish a model audit protocol and review a random sample of 10% of audits annually; districts that do not publish audits as required must lose eligibility for new federal school improvement grants; teachers must be included in the audit design and review process, with union representatives serving as co-equal partners where applicable.

Adversarial review: What constitutes non-instructional administrative burden is contested; districts will argue that much documentation teachers find burdensome is required by federal law or legal compliance, including IEP documentation and Title I reporting; the audit must distinguish genuinely reducible burden from legally required documentation. Union co-governance of audits is only available in bargaining states; non-bargaining states must require teacher advisory committees as a substitute mechanism. Districts may respond to audit requirements by re-labeling administrative tasks as instructional or by underreporting administrative time; independent verification mechanisms are needed. Audit publications may create public pressure on districts that is disproportionate to actual reducibility when federally mandated reporting is the primary source; the audit must disaggregate federally mandated from locally mandated tasks. Research basis: RAND surveys find teachers work an average of 53 hours per week compared to 44 for comparably educated workers, with a significant portion spent on administrative tasks reported as disconnected from student learning (Diliberti, M. K., and Schwartz, H. L., 2024, Teacher well-being and intentions to leave in 2024, RAND Corporation).

EDUC-TWRK-0004 Included

All school districts receiving federal funding must provide teachers and school staff with comprehensive Employee Assistance Program coverage including at least 12 free counseling sessions annually, burnout screening, and peer support networks

More than half of teachers report burnout, roughly twice the rate of other working adults. This is both a humanitarian problem and a driver of the teacher shortage. This rule requires schools to provide real mental health support to teachers, not just nominal EAP programs that few people use.

All public school districts receiving Title I funding must provide all teachers and certificated staff with Employee Assistance Program coverage that includes: (a) a minimum of 12 free outpatient mental health counseling sessions per year with licensed mental health professionals, with no copay, coinsurance, or deductible; (b) annual voluntary burnout and occupational stress screening administered confidentially by an independent contractor, not the district; (c) access to a peer support network of trained peer support specialists who are themselves current or former educators; (d) referrals to community mental health resources for staff who need support beyond the EAP benefit; EAP services must be actively promoted at the start of each school year and during high-stress periods, without stigmatizing language; districts must not use EAP participation data to make employment decisions; EAP providers must be independent of the district administration and must maintain confidentiality; districts must report annually to the state education agency the percentage of staff who utilized EAP services, without identifying individual users; state agencies must identify districts with utilization rates below 5% for targeted outreach.

Adversarial review: EAP coverage requirements impose costs on school districts; districts may comply minimally by contracting with low-quality providers offering telephonic counseling rather than in-person therapy, which is less effective for occupational burnout; quality standards for EAP providers should be specified. Teachers may not utilize mental health services due to stigma or concerns about confidentiality in small communities; the confidentiality protections must be rigorous and teachers must have reason to trust them, including third-party confidentiality verification. The 12-session minimum may be insufficient for teachers experiencing serious burnout or trauma; the rule should include a pathway for extended benefits when clinically recommended. Administrative reporting requirements impose additional burden on districts inconsistent with the burden reduction goals in EDUC-TWRK-0003; the reporting should be streamlined and technology-enabled. Peer support specialists require their own training and support; the rule should specify what training constitutes qualification for this role and who funds it. Research basis: RAND surveys document that 53-60% of teachers report burnout, approximately twice the rate of comparable working adults, and this burnout has not returned to pre-pandemic levels; burnout is a primary driver of attrition intentions (Diliberti, M. K., and Schwartz, H. L., 2023 and 2024, Teacher well-being surveys, RAND Corporation).

EDUC-TWRK-0005 Included

All public schools must employ at least one licensed school counselor per 250 enrolled students, with counselors' caseloads consisting of no less than 80% direct student service activities rather than administrative or disciplinary duties

Most schools have far too few counselors. When students cannot get mental health support, teachers end up absorbing that need on top of their regular teaching workload. This rule requires schools to meet the professional standard of one counselor per 250 students and ensures counselors are actually providing counseling, not doing paperwork.

All public schools receiving federal Title I funding must demonstrate within five years of enactment that they employ, on a full-time equivalent basis, at least one licensed school counselor for every 250 enrolled students; counselors must hold state licensure as school counselors or a recognized equivalent; counselors' professional time must consist of no less than 80% in direct student service activities including individual counseling, group counseling, crisis intervention, college and career advising, and family consultation; no more than 20% of counselor time may be allocated to administrative, scheduling, or disciplinary processing duties; schools that are more than 20% above the 1:250 ratio must receive priority in allocations from the federal Student Support and Academic Enrichment grants; the Department of Education must include counselor ratios in the Civil Rights Data Collection, disaggregated by school poverty level, racial composition, and geographic type; states with average counselor ratios above 1:400 must develop state implementation plans for reaching the 1:250 standard within eight years; telehealth counseling via licensed remote counselors is expressly recognized as counting toward the ratio requirement in rural contexts.

Adversarial review: The 1:250 ratio is the American School Counselor Association's recommended maximum; current national averages are far above this ratio, and full compliance would require hiring tens of thousands of additional counselors not currently in the pipeline; the rule requires corresponding investment in counselor preparation programs and must not create unfunded mandates. Counselors hired to meet ratio requirements must be meaningfully qualified; schools under budget pressure may use the counselor title for staff primarily focused on scheduling, which is already widespread; rigorous licensure requirements must be enforced. Counselors working in high-poverty schools with high trauma loads experience their own burnout and attrition; the rule should address counselor working conditions alongside student-to-counselor ratios. The telehealth provision for rural contexts must specify minimum technical standards and privacy protections for minors receiving counseling over digital platforms. Concentration of school counselor ratios in the Civil Rights Data Collection increases public accountability but may not reach non-Title-I schools unless the collection is expanded. Research basis: ASCA recommends a maximum of 250 students per counselor; most schools far exceed this ratio, placing additional mental health burden on classroom teachers by default (American School Counselor Association, 2023, School counselor ratios, schoolcounselor.org).

EDUC-TWRK-0006 Included

Public school teachers may not be assigned non-instructional supervisory or extracurricular duties exceeding five cumulative hours per week during the instructional year without additional compensation at 1.5 times their hourly rate of pay

Teachers are routinely assigned to lunchroom supervision, hallway duty, test administration, and other tasks unrelated to classroom instruction, consuming time that could be spent on planning and professional development. This rule caps these duties and requires additional pay when they exceed reasonable limits.

All public school teachers employed in grades K through 12 at publicly funded schools may not be assigned non-instructional duties including lunchroom supervision, hallway monitoring, bus duty, test administration not required by federal law, building security assignments, substitute coverage during planning periods, and non-subject-area committee assignments in excess of five cumulative hours per week during the instructional year; teachers assigned non-instructional duties exceeding five hours per week must receive additional compensation at a rate of not less than 1.5 times their hourly rate of pay, calculated by dividing annual salary by 2,080; this additional compensation must be identified as a separate line item in teacher employment agreements; school districts must notify teachers of their full non-instructional duty assignment at the beginning of each semester; changes to duty assignments adding more than two hours per week may not be made without 10 business days' notice; teachers may not be assigned non-instructional duties during their protected planning period established under EDUC-TWRK-0002; violations are subject to grievance under collective bargaining agreements or complaint to the state education agency with remedies including compensatory pay.

Adversarial review: The five-hour weekly cap may be too generous in schools with severe staffing shortages where teachers are already filling multiple essential roles; a tighter cap of three hours would better reflect the research on workload and burnout. The 1.5x overtime rate for non-instructional duties above the cap may create budget pressure causing districts to assign duties below the cap while eliminating other benefits; the rule must prevent offsetting adjustments. Some non-instructional duties, particularly committee work and professional learning communities, have documented positive effects on teacher practice and school culture; a blanket limit that includes voluntary professional activities could undermine effective professional learning; the rule should distinguish between voluntary professional activities and mandatory compliance tasks. Teachers in non-bargaining states lack a grievance mechanism; the state education agency complaint pathway must be staffed and functional with published response time standards. The definition of non-instructional duties will be contested by districts; activities will be relabeled as "instructional" to escape the cap; a clear definitional list in the implementing regulation is necessary. Compensatory pay requirements may cause districts to eliminate extracurricular programs rather than pay for them, creating a tradeoff between teacher workload protection and extracurricular access that must be addressed through parallel extracurricular staffing investment.

In cities like San Francisco, Boston, and Seattle, teacher salaries are not enough to afford housing near the schools where teachers work. One-third of California public school employees are rent-burdened, spending more than 30% of their income on housing. This rule creates a federal program to help teachers afford to live in the communities where they teach.

Congress must establish a Teacher Housing Assistance Fund within the Department of Housing and Urban Development, in coordination with the Department of Education, providing: (a) housing grants of up to $12,000 per year for no more than five years for qualifying teachers who are rent-burdened, defined as spending more than 30% of gross income on housing costs, and who teach in a Title I school in a high-cost metropolitan area where median gross rent exceeds 40% of a teacher's median annual salary; (b) down payment assistance of up to $25,000 for qualifying teachers purchasing a primary residence in the school district where they teach; teachers must have completed at least two years of teaching in the district to receive down payment assistance; grants and assistance must be structured as forgivable loans with forgiveness at a rate of 20% per year of continued employment in the district; the program must be jointly marketed through teacher preparation programs and school district onboarding; annual funding must be no less than $500 million; priority in grant awards must go to teachers in rural and high-poverty communities and teachers of color given documented disparities in wealth accumulation.

Adversarial review (PAOS-TEST-0008): The $12,000 annual housing grant is meaningful in moderate-cost markets but insufficient in the highest-cost markets like San Francisco or New York, where the gap between teacher salary and housing cost may exceed $30,000 per year; the grant maximum should be indexed to area median income relative to median teacher salary rather than a fixed dollar figure. Down payment assistance forgivable over five years may constrain teachers in difficult working conditions they would otherwise leave; forgiveness conditions must not discourage teachers from departing schools where conditions are harmful to their wellbeing. The program targets Title I schools and high-cost markets but many rural markets with teacher shortages are not high-cost; a separate rural housing provision is addressed in EDUC-THSG-0004. Housing grants and down payment assistance are taxable income unless structured carefully; the legislation must include an explicit exclusion from gross income for federal income tax purposes. Program administration across two federal departments creates coordination risk; a single designated program administrator with shared data agreements should be specified. Research basis: In California, more than one-third of public school employees are rent-burdened, spending over 30% of their income on housing; housing insecurity is a recognized driver of teacher shortages in high-cost regions (The Conversation, 2024, Americas teachers are being priced out of their communities, theconversation.com).

EDUC-THSG-0002 Included

The federal teacher salary floor must include a geographic cost-of-living adjustment, with states in metropolitan areas above 115% of national cost of living required to provide salary supplements maintaining real purchasing power of the floor

A teacher earning $60,000 in rural Kansas has very different buying power than a teacher earning $60,000 in San Francisco. A salary floor set without accounting for cost of living is not a real floor in high-cost areas. This rule requires that salary floors be adjusted for cost of living so that teachers everywhere have comparable purchasing power.

The federal teacher salary floor established under EDUC-TCHS-0001 must be adjusted annually using a geographic cost-of-living multiplier derived from the Bureau of Labor Statistics Consumer Price Index for metropolitan statistical areas and from USDA Rural Urban Commuting Area codes for rural areas; in any metropolitan statistical area where the area cost-of-living index exceeds 115% of the national average, the applicable minimum teacher salary floor must be at least 115% of the nominal national floor; states with high-cost metropolitan areas must certify annually to the Department of Education that average teacher salaries in those areas meet the adjusted floor or that the state is funding a geographic supplement to close the gap; state geographic supplements must be in addition to base salary and may not be included in calculations of whether the base salary floor has been met; the Bureau of Labor Statistics must publish a teacher-specific cost-of-living index for all metropolitan statistical areas annually, reflecting the cost of housing, transportation, and childcare in contexts relevant to teacher salary adequacy.

Adversarial review: Geographic cost-of-living adjustments are technically complex and create incentives for jurisdictional gaming; districts near high-cost area boundaries will be affected differently from those in the core of high-cost metros, requiring careful geographic demarcation. States may argue that the cost-of-living adjustment is an unfunded federal mandate; the federal floor compliance mechanism must provide sufficient funding for states to meet adjusted floors. The teacher-specific cost-of-living index does not currently exist; BLS would need to construct it, requiring an appropriation and timeline that may delay implementation; the rule should include an interim methodology using existing BLS metropolitan area CPI data. Rural teachers face a different type of cost-of-living challenge, including housing illiquidity and limited spousal employment, not captured by standard cost indices; the USDA rural area adjustment must be specifically designed to account for these rural-specific costs. States may respond to geographic supplements by reducing base salary investments in non-supplement areas, creating a two-tier system that reduces the overall trajectory of teacher compensation improvement.

EDUC-THSG-0003 Included

School districts must be authorized to develop subsidized workforce housing on district-owned or leased land for teachers and staff, with federal Educator Workforce Housing Capital Grants of $300 million annually for high-cost or high-poverty communities

Several school districts in California and other high-cost states have started building subsidized apartments on school district property that teachers can rent at below-market rates. This is one of the most effective ways to keep teachers in communities they cannot otherwise afford to live in. This rule authorizes and funds more of these programs.

School districts are authorized under federal law to develop, construct, manage, or partner with housing authorities or nonprofit developers to develop subsidized workforce housing on district-owned, district-leased, or district-controlled land for teachers, counselors, administrators, and other school staff; Congress must appropriate no less than $300 million annually for Educator Workforce Housing Capital Grants, awarded to school districts that serve high-cost metropolitan areas where median rent exceeds 40% of average teacher salary, enroll a student population more than 40% Title I-eligible, or have documented teacher vacancy rates above 10% in the preceding year; grant recipients must demonstrate that projects will remain available to school district employees for no fewer than 25 years; rent in district-developed housing must be capped at 30% of teacher household income; priority in housing allocation must go to first-year and second-year teachers, teachers in shortage subject areas, and teachers of color in communities where the profession is racially unrepresentative; districts may not condition tenancy on continued employment beyond a two-year initial lease term.

Adversarial review: District-developed housing creates a landlord-tenant relationship between the employing district and the teacher-tenant, generating conflicts of interest in which a district might leverage housing to suppress labor activity or enforce work requirements; robust tenant protection laws must apply fully to district-developed housing, enforced by an agency independent of the school district. The $300 million annual appropriation is insufficient to address the scale of the housing crisis in high-cost markets; it should be treated as a seed investment requiring state and private capital leverage. District-developed housing requires real estate development expertise that most school districts lack; partnerships with housing authorities and nonprofit developers must be encouraged and the grant program should fund project management capacity-building. Concentration of teachers in district housing creates geographic vulnerability: if teachers lose their jobs, they lose their housing simultaneously; the two-year post-termination lease guarantee is minimal and should be extended to at least one year. Research basis: California, Colorado, and Ohio have begun developing subsidized housing on school district-owned land; California's rent burden crisis, affecting one-third of school employees, makes this a recognized emergency rather than a marginal benefit (The Conversation, 2024, theconversation.com/americas-teachers-are-being-priced-out-of-their-communities).

EDUC-THSG-0004 Included

Federal law must provide a dedicated rural teacher retention package including an $8,000 annual service supplement, down payment assistance of $15,000, spousal employment partnership grants, and $3,000 relocation assistance for teachers in rural and remote districts

Rural districts face a different housing problem than cities: housing markets are too thin for young professional families, there are few jobs for teachers' partners, and communities are isolated. This rule creates a rural-specific teacher support package addressing these distinct challenges that urban housing programs do not cover.

Congress must establish a Rural Teacher Retention Grant program, available to districts classified as rural under USDA Rural Urban Commuting Area codes, providing: (a) a rural service supplement of $8,000 per year for teachers who have completed at least two years in the rural district, indexed annually to inflation using the Employment Cost Index; (b) down payment assistance of up to $15,000 for teachers purchasing a primary residence within the district's service area; (c) a spousal employment partnership grant of up to $10,000 per district per year to fund partnerships with local employers and remote-work employers to identify employment opportunities for teacher spouses or domestic partners; (d) relocation assistance of up to $3,000 for teachers who relocate from urban or suburban areas to accept positions in rural districts with vacancy rates above 8%; annual funding must be no less than $150 million; priority must go to districts with vacancy rates of 10% or more, districts serving high proportions of Native American students on or near tribal lands, or districts with starting teacher salaries more than 15% below the state average; the Department of Education must publish rural teacher vacancy data annually and assess whether the rural supplement is adequate to close the rural-urban pay gap.

Adversarial review: Rural districts vary enormously in their specific housing and labor market challenges; a one-size-fits-all rural package may not address the unique challenges of remote reservation schools, isolated mountain communities, or Appalachian coal country; the program should allow flexibility in how grants are used while maintaining minimum standards. The spousal employment partnership program assumes rural employers want to participate; in many rural communities there is simply insufficient professional employment regardless of program design, and this subsidy may not overcome that structural reality. Rural teachers who receive relocation assistance and housing support may still leave after the early service period if isolation and lack of amenities becomes unsustainable; financial incentives alone cannot overcome all non-financial barriers to rural teaching. The rural supplement creates a pay differential between rural and urban teachers in the same state that may be appropriate on cost-of-living grounds but may generate political resistance from non-rural teacher associations. Native American and tribal school contexts have unique sovereignty, governance, and cultural competency dimensions that the rural package does not fully address; tribal schools should be able to apply for enhanced grants with additional flexibility for culturally responsive programming.

EDU-TPSF Public Service Loan Forgiveness Reform 0/5 proposed
EDUC-TPSF-0001 Included

Public Service Loan Forgiveness must be codified as a statutory entitlement for qualifying public school teachers, with automatic enrollment, automatic processing, and elimination of procedural disqualifications unrelated to actual qualifying service

The PSLF program has historically been a nightmare to navigate. Before recent reforms, the rejection rate was over 98%. This rule makes PSLF simple and automatic for public school teachers, so teachers get the forgiveness they earned without having to navigate a bureaucratic gauntlet.

Congress must codify Public Service Loan Forgiveness as a statutory entitlement for all public school teachers employed full-time by a public school or nonprofit charter school; the Department of Education must: (a) automatically identify and notify qualifying borrowers using data from the TEACH Grant service requirements system and school employer tax records within 90 days of enactment; (b) automatically apply PSLF upon verification that a borrower has made 120 qualifying monthly payments under any qualifying repayment plan while employed full-time at a qualifying employer, without requiring a separate forgiveness application; (c) count qualifying payments made under any income-driven repayment plan toward the 120-payment threshold; (d) provide automatic forgiveness within 60 days of qualifying payment threshold verification; (e) notify qualifying borrowers at least one year before they reach the 120-payment threshold; the Department must maintain a searchable database of qualifying public school employers updated monthly; FFEL loans must be eligible for PSLF through a streamlined administrative consolidation process taking no more than 30 days and triggering no restart of the qualifying payment count; failure to apply forgiveness within 60 days of qualification must entitle the borrower to a refund of payments made after the qualification date.

Adversarial review (PAOS-TEST-0008): Auto-enrollment requires access to employment and loan data across multiple federal agencies and loan servicers; data-sharing agreements and systems integration are technically complex and historically unreliable; the 90-day implementation timeline should be extended if it incentivizes a rushed, error-prone rollout. The elimination of procedural disqualifications must be drafted carefully to not inadvertently eliminate legitimate screening for service requirements; the rule must distinguish between procedural barriers (wrong form, wrong servicer, wrong repayment plan) and substantive service requirements. Future administrations could reverse auto-enrollment policies without statutory protection; Congress must require the automation in statute, not merely authorize it by regulation. The SAVE plan's income-driven repayment structure is subject to ongoing federal litigation as of 2025; the PSLF statute must be divorced from dependence on any specific repayment plan and must specify that PSLF eligibility applies regardless of which qualifying IDR plan the borrower used. Charter school eligibility is contested; non-profit charters are already eligible but for-profit charters are not; the rule must address the management organization structure, which often interposes a for-profit entity between a non-profit charter and its teachers. Research basis: As of May 2024, nearly 876,000 public service workers had received loan forgiveness under PSLF following administrative reforms beginning in 2021, compared to approximately 7,000 before reform, illustrating how much program design affected actual access (Biden White House, 2024, Fact sheet: Biden-Harris Administration announces new actions to support and strengthen the teaching profession, bidenwhitehouse.archives.gov).

EDUC-TPSF-0002 Included

Congress must establish a PSLF Correction and Restitution Program providing automatic forgiveness and payment refunds for teachers wrongly denied PSLF due to servicer errors, administrative failures, or technical disqualifications unrelated to their actual public service

For years, PSLF rejected nearly every applicant, often because of administrative errors, wrong loan types, or wrong repayment plans that applicants had no way of knowing about. Teachers who followed the rules but were still denied deserve to have those wrongs corrected. This rule creates a process to identify and fix historical denials.

Congress must establish a PSLF Correction and Restitution Program providing: (a) automatic forgiveness of remaining loan balances for any borrower who made 120 or more payments on federal student loans while employed full-time at a qualifying public school, and who was denied PSLF due to: (i) servicer error in loan classification, payment counting, or employer certification; (ii) enrollment in a non-qualifying repayment plan that the servicer failed to redirect upon request; (iii) FFEL or Perkins loan status that the servicer failed to advise the borrower to consolidate; (iv) technical employer certification errors not attributable to the borrower; (b) refund of all payments made after the borrower's 120th qualifying payment date, with simple interest at the federal funds rate; (c) automatic credit reporting correction removing any negative marks attributable to the erroneous denial period; the Department of Education must identify all borrowers potentially eligible for retroactive relief and process retroactive forgiveness without requiring individual applications; borrowers who abandoned the program before 120 payments but have contemporaneous documentation of qualifying employment must have a documented claim process with a 90-day resolution period; Congress must appropriate dedicated enforcement staffing to process retroactive claims within three years of enactment.

Adversarial review: Identifying all wrongly denied borrowers requires access to complete historical servicer records, many of which are in poor condition or have been transferred between servicers; a comprehensive audit is technically feasible but expensive and requires an independent contractor with adequate appropriated funding for data reconstruction. The no-application-required design requires accurate borrower identification, which will have gaps; a complementary opt-in claim process is necessary as a fallback. Loan servicers that caused many original denials through errors or inadequate counseling face potential liability; servicer indemnification agreements with the government may limit the government's ability to recover retroactive costs. The refund-with-interest provision will be resisted as too large to fund in a single appropriation; a multi-year phased payment structure may be necessary. Borrowers who abandoned the program early and lack documentation of qualifying employment face a much harder path to retroactive relief; alternative documentation options such as tax records, school employment verifications, and self-attestation subject to audit must be included. Research basis: Prior to administrative reforms beginning in 2021, PSLF had a rejection rate exceeding 98%; the program was functionally inaccessible to most eligible teachers despite their qualifying service (Biden White House, 2024, bidenwhitehouse.archives.gov).

EDUC-TPSF-0003 Included

Teachers in federally designated shortage areas who teach in Title I schools must qualify for full federal loan forgiveness after five years of qualifying service rather than the standard ten years, with graduated partial forgiveness at three and four years

The standard PSLF program requires 10 years of payments before forgiveness. That is a long commitment to ask a special education or math teacher to make when they could earn much more elsewhere. This rule cuts the timeline in half for teachers in the hardest-to-fill positions, and adds graduated forgiveness to reward teachers who leave before five years.

Any teacher employed full-time in a federally designated National Teacher Shortage Area subject as defined under EDUC-TPPL-0003 who teaches at a Title I-eligible school qualifies for full forgiveness of remaining federal student loan balances after 60 qualifying monthly payments under a qualifying income-driven repayment plan; the accelerated timeline applies to all loan types that qualify for standard PSLF, including Direct Loans and consolidated FFEL or Perkins loans; in addition to full forgiveness at 60 payments, teachers who serve three years receive 50% forgiveness of remaining balance, and teachers who serve four years receive 75% forgiveness, with the graduated amounts applied automatically upon departure from qualifying employment; borrowers who qualify for accelerated forgiveness must be notified by their loan servicer no later than the 36th qualifying monthly payment; the shortage area designation must be updated annually, but borrowers who began qualifying service in a designated shortage area retain eligibility for accelerated forgiveness for the duration of their qualifying service in that subject, even if the area is later removed from the designation; Note: the SAVE plan's IDR calculation methodology is subject to ongoing federal litigation as of 2025 and this rule must not be conditioned on any specific repayment plan remaining available.

Adversarial review: The five-year forgiveness timeline, while a strong recruitment incentive, may perversely cause teachers to see it as a terminal point rather than an ongoing benefit; graduated forgiveness at three and four years directly addresses this by rewarding any completion of service rather than only a five-year all-or-nothing outcome. Teachers who switch from shortage subjects to non-shortage subjects mid-career would lose accelerated eligibility; career transition pathways should not be penalized and the rule should provide for partial credit toward the accelerated timeline when teachers switch. The shortage area designation may lag actual labor market conditions; the lock-in provision for designations in force at the time of initial service is essential. Employers may rely on accelerated PSLF as a substitute for salary increases; disclosure requirements must make clear that PSLF is a federal benefit, not employer compensation. The SAVE plan litigation caveat is critical; any dependence on a specific repayment plan that may be struck down by the courts would render the accelerated PSLF inaccessible to the teachers it is designed to help.

EDUC-TPSF-0004 Included

Borrowers with Federal Family Education Loan or Perkins loan debt must be provided a no-penalty, no-restart consolidation pathway into Direct Loans for PSLF eligibility, with all prior qualifying service payments credited retroactively upon consolidation

Millions of teachers have older student loan types called FFEL and Perkins loans that do not qualify for PSLF. To get PSLF they must consolidate these loans into a Direct Loan, which used to restart the payment clock at zero and cost them credit for years of qualifying service. This rule fixes that by giving those payments retroactive credit and making consolidation simple.

All borrowers holding Federal Family Education Loan or Federal Perkins Loan debt who are employed or have been employed as public school teachers at a qualifying employer must be: (a) automatically notified within 90 days of enactment that their loans are not eligible for PSLF in their current form and that consolidation into Direct Loans is available; (b) offered a streamlined one-step administrative consolidation process, completed within 30 days of application, at no cost to the borrower; (c) provided retroactive credit for all prior qualifying monthly payments made while employed at a qualifying employer, regardless of loan type, toward the PSLF 120-payment or accelerated 60-payment threshold upon consolidation; the retroactive credit provision may not result in a payment count restart upon consolidation; borrowers who consolidated prior to enactment and lost credit for prior payments are eligible for retroactive restoration under EDUC-TPSF-0002; the Department of Education must publish plain-language guidance on the consolidation process within 60 days of enactment, in all languages represented by more than 1% of current federal student loan borrowers.

Adversarial review: Retroactive credit for all prior payments requires accurate recordkeeping of historical payment histories that may not be consistently maintained across servicer transitions; the 90-day notification requirement must be accompanied by a mandate for servicers to reconstruct and audit historical records with adequate appropriated funding. FFEL loans held by commercial lenders require those lenders' cooperation in the consolidation process; some commercial holders may resist or delay consolidation; the rule must include regulatory authority to compel timely processing. Borrowers who have already paid off their FFEL loans are not helped by this rule; borrowers near the end of repayment who would benefit from consolidation must be prioritized in outreach. The one-step administrative process must integrate loan servicer systems that are historically incompatible; a shared data standard must be specified. Consolidation may affect interest rates if the new Direct Loan bears different rates than the original FFEL loans; the rule must specify that consolidation must not increase the borrower's interest rate. Language accessibility in all languages representing more than 1% of borrowers is important but requires significant translation resources that must be explicitly appropriated by Congress.

EDUC-TPSF-0005 Included

States operating teacher loan forgiveness programs must coordinate with federal PSLF and may receive federal matching funds of up to $100 million annually for programs providing forgiveness timelines shorter than the federal standard for high-need schools

Many states have their own teacher loan forgiveness programs but they are inconsistent, complicated, and often overlap with federal programs in confusing ways. This rule creates a federal matching fund to encourage states to build better programs and coordinates the federal and state programs so teachers can use both without confusion.

Congress must establish a State Teacher Loan Forgiveness Partnership Fund providing federal matching grants of up to $100 million annually in aggregate to states that: (a) operate state teacher loan forgiveness programs providing at least $5,000 in loan forgiveness per year for each year of qualifying teaching service for up to five years; (b) prioritize forgiveness for teachers in high-need subjects and Title I schools; (c) coordinate state program eligibility criteria with federal PSLF eligibility criteria to ensure teachers can access both programs simultaneously without double-counting requirements; (d) provide clear, plain-language enrollment guidance and automatic enrollment where data permits; states receiving matching funds must publish annual program outcome data including number of recipients, average forgiveness amount, subject and school type distribution, and demographic breakdown; federal matching must be dollar-for-dollar for state forgiveness amounts up to $5,000 per teacher per year; states with no state teacher loan forgiveness programs must develop a program plan within three years as a condition of eligibility for new Teacher Quality Partnership grants.

Adversarial review: Federal matching funds conditioned on state program design may be challenged as coercive use of the Spending Clause; the matching fund design, where states choose to participate, is constitutionally stronger than a mandate and is preferred here. State programs that rely on annual appropriations rather than permanent entitlements create uncertainty for teachers making multi-year career decisions; federal matching should require state programs to make multi-year commitments. States may design programs that nominally coordinate with PSLF but use different qualifying employment definitions that leave teachers unable to access both programs simultaneously; a federal model framework for coordination that states must adopt is necessary. The annual 50-state comparison publication may create political pressure in low-performing states that results in program elimination rather than improvement; the data must be presented with context about state fiscal capacity. Forgiveness programs primarily benefit teachers with substantial debt; teachers who attended low-cost institutions receive no benefit; the program complements but must not substitute for salary and working conditions investments. States receiving matching funds must be audited for whether coordination actually reduces borrower confusion or merely adds a parallel bureaucracy.

EDU-TPEQ Teacher Pay Equity and Salary Transparency 0/4 proposed
EDUC-TPEQ-0001 Included

States must require all public school districts to conduct biennial teacher pay equity audits comparing salaries by race, gender, and experience, with mandatory correction plans for gaps exceeding $2,000 between groups with equivalent credentials

Even though teacher pay is set by formal salary schedules meant to be objective, Black teachers earn about $5,600 less than White teachers on average, and female teachers earn about $4,000 less when all compensation is included. This rule requires districts to audit and publish their pay data so that disparities can be seen and corrected.

States receiving federal Title I or Title II funding must require all public school districts to conduct a Teacher Pay Equity Audit every two years, analyzing teacher base salary, supplemental pay (including coaching, extracurricular, and extra-duty pay), and total compensation by race, gender, years of experience, certification level, and school assignment; audit methodology must be approved by the state education agency and must control for experience and credentials when comparing salaries across demographic groups; audit results must be published on the district's public website within 60 days of completion; districts where audits identify a pay gap of more than $2,000 between teachers of different racial or ethnic groups with equivalent credentials and experience must submit a pay equity correction plan within one year, identifying the source of the disparity and a timeline for correction; districts that do not publish audits on time or fail to submit correction plans must lose eligibility for new federal professional development grants; the Department of Education must aggregate state-reported audit data into an annual national teacher pay equity report disaggregated by state, district size, and school poverty level.

Adversarial review (PAOS-TEST-0008): District-level pay equity audits that control only for credentials and experience will not capture all sources of disparity; the assignment to higher-paying positions such as department heads, coaches, and lead teachers is itself a potential site of discrimination that the audit must capture at the position level, not only the individual level. The $2,000 gap threshold for triggering a correction plan is relatively high; gaps below that threshold may still reflect discrimination; the threshold should be reviewed after three audit cycles for adequacy. Districts may manipulate audit methodology, particularly the experience controls, to explain away disparities; the state agency must have authority to reject methodologically deficient audits and require re-audit. Pay equity correction plans will generate political opposition in districts where disparities are large; the correction plan must include an appeal process but must not allow appeals to delay implementation of reasonable corrective measures for more than 60 days. Supplemental pay equity is particularly important: coaching and extracurricular stipend assignments are a major source of the gender pay gap and are often allocated through informal processes with limited oversight; the audit must cover all forms of additional compensation paid through the payroll system. Research basis: RAND's 2024 survey found Black teachers earn an average base salary of approximately $65,013 while White teachers earn approximately $70,695, a gap of over $5,600, with White teachers also receiving larger annual raises on average (RAND Corporation, 2024, Teacher pay: What K-12 teachers earn and what they think about it); male teachers receive on average approximately $4,000 more annually than female teachers with similar experience and credentials, partly reflecting differential allocation of coaching and supplemental pay (Brookings Institution, 2024, Gender wage gaps in public schools, brookings.edu/articles/gender-wage-gaps-policy-brief/).

EDUC-TPEQ-0002 Included

All public school districts must assign coaching, extracurricular, and supplemental duty positions through a transparent, posted application process with written documentation of selection decisions to ensure equal access to supplemental earnings

A significant part of the gender pay gap in teaching comes from coaching and extra-duty pay that tends to go to male teachers through informal assignment processes. This rule requires that these positions be posted publicly and assigned through a fair, documented process so that female and minority teachers have equal access to earning opportunities beyond base salary.

All public school districts must post all compensated supplemental assignments, including athletic coaching positions, extracurricular advisorships, department head positions, and instructional coach positions, as open positions for a minimum of 10 business days before filling, with published pay rates, time commitments, and selection criteria; selection decisions for supplemental positions must be documented in writing with stated reasons; any teacher who was not selected for a supplemental position may request a written explanation of the selection decision; districts must include supplemental pay assignments in their biennial Teacher Pay Equity Audits required under EDUC-TPEQ-0001; districts where the share of supplemental pay going to male teachers exceeds their share of the overall faculty by more than 15 percentage points must submit a supplemental pay equity plan; districts must track and publish the racial and gender breakdown of supplemental assignment recipients annually; informal supplemental assignments that are paid but not posted are prohibited; violations are subject to teacher complaint to the state education agency and to collective bargaining grievance where applicable.

Adversarial review: The 10-business-day posting requirement will be argued to create operational difficulties for extracurricular positions that must be filled quickly due to resignations; a 5-business-day emergency posting process with documentation of the emergency is a reasonable exception. Written documentation requirements for selection decisions will increase administrative burden on principals; the rule must specify a minimum rather than an extensive documentation format to make compliance feasible. Districts may respond to posting requirements by pre-selecting candidates informally and then posting positions as a formality; documentation of the selection process must include evidence of genuine outreach to qualified candidates. The 15-percentage-point disparity trigger for a supplemental pay plan may be set too high given that supplemental pay is a substantial portion of total compensation; the threshold should be reviewed for adequacy after initial implementation. Male teachers who rely on coaching pay as a substantial portion of their income may organize politically against this rule; the rule is not about reducing their pay but about ensuring equal access to the assignments, and this distinction must be clearly communicated in implementation guidance.

EDUC-TPEQ-0003 Included

All public school districts must publish their complete teacher salary schedules, including all supplemental pay rates, on a publicly accessible WCAG 2.1 AA-compliant website within 30 days of adoption or modification

Many districts do not publish their full teacher salary information in accessible form. Teachers should be able to see exactly what they are paid and how that compares to colleagues, and prospective teachers should be able to compare opportunities across districts. This rule requires full, accessible public disclosure.

All public school districts must publish within 30 days of adoption or modification, and maintain continuously accessible on their public website: (a) the complete teacher base salary schedule, disaggregated by years of experience and credential level, in downloadable spreadsheet format; (b) the complete schedule of all supplemental pay for all compensated assignments including coaching, department head, extracurricular, and extra-duty positions; (c) the total compensation range for each teacher employment classification; (d) a comparison of starting teacher salary to the state average and national average using the most recent NEA salary benchmark data; state education agencies must compile and publish on a statewide public portal the salary schedules of all districts in the state in a searchable, comparable format updated within 60 days of any district modification; the Department of Education must compile and maintain a national teacher salary comparison database drawing on state-submitted data, updated annually; districts that fail to publish their salary schedules within 30 days of adoption must lose eligibility for new federal teacher quality grants; the state portal must meet WCAG 2.1 AA accessibility standards.

Adversarial review: Districts may publish salary schedules that are technically complete but practically opaque, using complicated lane and step designations that make actual pay impossible to understand without expert interpretation; the publication must include a plain-language explainer of how the schedule works and what a typical teacher can expect to earn at each stage. Salary transparency may have unintended competitive effects, allowing high-paying districts to reduce raises when they are already competitive; state-level salary transparency that reveals the full distribution of compensation is a more effective tool than individual district-level disclosure. Districts may comply by publishing outdated or incorrect salary information; a certification requirement by the superintendent or CFO with penalties for false certification is necessary. The national comparison database depends on voluntary, accurate state reporting; data quality monitoring and standardization of reporting formats must be established before the database is treated as authoritative. Accessibility requirements must be framed as a legal obligation under Section 508 of the Rehabilitation Act, not an optional enhancement, and compliance must be verified by the state portal administrator.

EDUC-TPEQ-0004 Included

Congress must fund Teacher Salary Schedule Decompression Grants of $250 million annually to help districts reform schedules so that teachers with 15 years of experience earn at least 175% of starting salary, reducing mid-career attrition caused by wage compression

The typical teacher salary schedule means a 30-year veteran earns less than twice what a brand-new teacher earns. In most professions, earnings grow much more steeply with experience. This extreme compression is a reason experienced teachers leave the classroom for higher-paying alternatives. This rule funds salary decompression to make mid-career teaching financially competitive.

Congress must appropriate no less than $250 million annually for Teacher Salary Schedule Decompression Grants, available to states and districts that: (a) reform their teacher salary schedules to ensure that a teacher with 15 years of experience earns at least 175% of the district's starting salary; (b) reform their salary schedules to ensure that a teacher with 25 years of experience earns at least 220% of the starting salary; (c) index all steps in the salary schedule to inflation using the Employment Cost Index on an annual basis, with annual adjustments capped at 4%; grant eligibility requires that the district not reduce its starting salary or total compensation as part of the schedule reform; states must document and publish salary schedule ratios for all districts annually; districts where the experienced-to-starting salary ratio is below 1.5:1 receive priority in grant competitions; the Department of Education must publish an annual analysis of salary compression across districts and states; states receiving decompression grants must maintain the reformed salary structure for at least five years, with pro-rata grant repayment required for any early reduction.

Adversarial review: The 175% and 220% experience premium targets are higher than current national averages and require meaningful additional investment; districts may resist by asserting that experienced teachers are not demonstrably more effective than mid-career teachers, a genuinely contested empirical question; the policy rationale is recruitment and retention rather than merit measurement and should be framed accordingly. Grant programs targeting experienced teacher pay may be captured by districts that increase experienced teacher pay while reducing new teacher recruitment investment; the grant must require maintenance of competitive starting salaries as a condition. Salary decompression may exacerbate within-district inequities if pay increases go to senior teachers concentrated in well-resourced schools; experience premium reform must be paired with assignment equity provisions ensuring experienced teachers are distributed across school needs. The 4% annual ECI indexing cap is important for fiscal predictability in high-inflation environments and must be specified clearly. Districts without collective bargaining lack institutional structures to implement salary schedule reform through negotiation; technical assistance from the state agency must be available to these districts. Research basis: The national ratio of average maximum to average starting teacher salary is approximately 1.8:1, compared to steeper trajectories in most other professional fields; RAND surveys found only about one-third of teachers say their pay is adequate, with teachers estimating raises of approximately $16,000-17,000 would be needed to feel adequately compensated (Diliberti, M. K., and Schwartz, H. L., 2024, Teacher well-being and intentions to leave in 2024, RAND Corporation).

Research & Context

The United States spends more per pupil on average than most OECD countries, yet produces dramatically unequal outcomes correlated strongly with race and income. A 2019 EdBuild report found that non-white school districts receive $23 billion less in funding annually than predominantly white districts, despite serving similar numbers of students.[3] Property-tax-based school funding is the structural driver: districts with higher property values raise more revenue at lower rates, perpetuating advantages regardless of state aid formulas.

Student debt has surpassed $1.7 trillion,[1] and federal reserve research shows that debt burdens delay homeownership, family formation, and wealth accumulation disproportionately for Black and Latino borrowers. The for-profit college sector accounts for 11% of enrollment but 44% of federal student loan defaults, according to the Senate HELP Committee's 2012 investigation.[4] Predatory institutions systematically overpromise economic returns, target vulnerable populations, and leave graduates with worthless credentials and crushing debt.

American students consistently trail OECD peers in science literacy and civic knowledge. Studies by the National Assessment of Educational Progress (NAEP) show that fewer than a quarter of 8th graders score proficient in civics.[2] The erosion of science education standards—particularly in states that have introduced non-scientific frameworks into biology and earth science curricula—has documented negative effects on workforce preparation in science, technology, engineering, and medicine.

Research on integration shows that socioeconomically diverse schools produce better outcomes for low-income students without harming higher-income peers.[5] The Century Foundation and The Educational Opportunity Project at Stanford (SEDA dataset) document that school district boundaries and attendance zones remain among the most powerful drivers of educational inequality in the country, with segregation by income and race often as stark as it was in the 1970s.

Early childhood interventions have among the highest documented returns of any educational investment. Longitudinal studies show that high-quality pre-K programs produce lasting gains in educational attainment, health, and earnings while reducing incarceration rates — with the largest returns for children from low-income families.[6] Despite this evidence, the United States invests far less in early childhood care and education than peer nations, and access to quality childcare is distributed primarily by family income.
