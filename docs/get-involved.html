The Freedom and Dignity Project is an open effort to build something durable: a modern constitutional framework that protects human rights, limits the concentration of power, and guarantees the material conditions for every person to live with dignity. It is not a campaign. It is not a party. It is a framework — built in the open, available to anyone, meant to last.

At this moment, the project is early. That is not a caveat — it is an invitation. Early means the important decisions are still being made, the policy catalog is still being built, the research is still being gathered. Early means your contribution does not disappear into a bureaucracy. It shapes the thing itself.

There is work here for researchers, writers, policy experts, lawyers, designers, engineers, community organizers, and people who have simply read the platform and want to make it better. There is also work for people who have never used GitHub in their lives. Whatever your skills, there is a role for you.

"To build a durable, modern democratic system that protects human rights, limits the concentration and abuse of power, ensures fair and equal representation, and guarantees the material conditions necessary for people to live with dignity and participate fully in society."

That is the mission. These pages are an attempt to make it real. None of it happens without people — people who read carefully, who push back honestly, who bring knowledge and perspective the founder does not have, and who are willing to do the unglamorous work that turns ideas into something the world can actually use.

What This Project Is Built On

These are not marketing values — they are the actual constraints the project holds itself to, derived from the source material and carried through every policy position, system rule, and editorial decision.

Love
This project is motivated by love — for people, for community, for what this country can actually be. Not sentiment. The kind of love that requires something of you.
Equality
Equal rights, equal treatment, equal dignity — regardless of identity, background, or circumstance. Not equality of outcome, but equality before the law and equality of opportunity.
Freedom
Real freedom requires stability, material security, and freedom from coercion. You cannot be free if you are sick, homeless, or starving.
Dignity
Every person has inherent worth that the law must protect. Not earned. Not contingent. The law is not doing its job if it allows people to be treated as if they do not matter.
Truth
A commitment to facts, evidence, and honest claims. Every factual assertion in this project must be verifiable. If it cannot be sourced, it does not belong here.
Science
Evidence-based policy, research-driven positions, openness to revision when facts change. Belief is not a substitute for evidence. Conviction is not a substitute for proof.
Transparency
In government, in this project, in how decisions are made. Every policy position is traceable to its source. The version history is public. Nothing is hidden.
Accountability
No one is above the law. Power must answer to people. This applies to governments, to institutions, and to this project itself — including when it gets things wrong.
Justice
Fair, consistent, humane. The law must apply equally. Justice delayed is justice denied. Justice applied selectively is not justice — it is power wearing a robe.
Stability
Freedom requires stability. People cannot exercise rights under constant economic crisis. The social floor is not charity; it is the precondition for everything else.
Solidarity
This isn't left vs. right. It's about whether the system works for people at all. The question is not which team wins — it's whether ordinary people can live with dignity.
Durability
Build things that last. Rights that can't be eroded overnight. Policy designed for the long run. The point is not to score a moment — it's to build something that holds.

The System Rules

The system rules are not policy positions. They are the foundational constraints — the operating system that all policy runs on top of. Just as an OS sets the rules under which all applications must operate, the system rules set the constraints under which all domain-specific policies must behave.

They exist for specific reasons: to ensure that policies across 23 different pillars do not contradict each other; to guarantee that no policy may degrade fundamental rights in order to achieve a policy goal; to require that every rule is traceable to its source; to prevent geography from determining access to rights; to distribute risk so no single point of failure can collapse the whole; and to require human judgment where it cannot be replaced by automation.

A few examples — representative, not exhaustive:

  • SYS-AI-002 Human judgment is required for materially harmful decisions. The absence of an AI recommendation cannot justify denial of a right or benefit.
  • SYS-AI-003 A lack of AI recommendation must not be used as evidence of unfitness. Algorithmic silence is not a verdict.
  • SYS-FED-001 High-risk systems must not be fully centralized. Oversight must be distributed; no single point of failure is acceptable.
  • SYS-FND-004 Universal equal rights must be guaranteed as a baseline national standard. States may expand rights; they may not contract them below the floor.
  • SYS-GEO-001 Geography must not determine access to rights or care. Where you were born — or where you can afford to live — cannot decide what rights you have.

New rules follow the existing ID schema: PREFIX-SUBFAM-NNN. Proposing a new system rule is a significant act — it requires discussion in a GitHub Issue before it can be formalized into the catalog.

Contributing via GitHub

The project lives on GitHub. You do not need to be a developer to contribute — opening an Issue (a bug report, a question, a piece of feedback) requires no coding at all. But if you want to contribute content or code directly, here is how the process works.

What is Git, and why do we use it?

Git is a tool that tracks changes to files over time. Think of it like "Track Changes" in a word processor — but for an entire project. Every change is recorded, labeled with a message explaining what changed and why, and can be reversed if something goes wrong. It also lets multiple people work on the same project without overwriting each other's work.

GitHub is a website that hosts projects that use Git. It is where this project lives publicly. You do not need to install Git or know anything technical to read the project, open an issue, or leave feedback. You only need it if you want to download the project to your computer and make changes directly.

The easiest contribution path — no coding required: Go to the Issues page and open a new issue. Write what you found, what you think, or what you want to flag. That's it. That is a real contribution and it is read.

For those who want to go deeper, GitHub works like this:

1
Fork the repository
A fork is your own personal copy of the project. On the project's GitHub page, click the "Fork" button in the top right. GitHub creates a copy under your account. You can make changes to your fork without affecting the main project.
2
Clone it to your computer
Cloning downloads your fork so you can edit files locally. If you are new to this, GitHub Desktop is a free app that handles this visually — no command line required. For a broader introduction, GitHub's own Hello World guide is a good starting point.
3
Make your changes
Edit the files you want to improve. For content changes to policy pages, that means editing the HTML files in docs/pillars/. For research and citations, follow the APA 7th edition standard used throughout the project. Every factual claim needs a verifiable source.
4
Open a pull request
A pull request is a formal proposal to merge your changes into the main project. GitHub walks you through this once your changes are committed to your fork. Include a clear description of what you changed and why. Policy changes should cite the source material — either from the project's chat logs or from external primary sources.
5
What happens after you submit
A project maintainer will review your pull request — reading your changes, checking sources, and possibly asking questions or suggesting edits. This is normal and not a rejection; it is the process working as intended. All changes go through human review before they are accepted into the project. If your contribution was drafted with AI assistance, note that in the PR description so the reviewer knows to look closely at sources. Once approved, your changes are merged and go live on the site.

Contribution Guidelines

Content Standards
  • Every factual claim must have a verifiable source in APA 7th edition format
  • Do not add policy positions without tracing them to the platform's values and system rules
  • Use precise, plain language — no weasel words, no unattributed statistics
  • New system rules must follow the existing ID schema: PREFIX-SUBFAM-NNN
  • Do not reproduce substantial verbatim text from copyrighted sources
Code Standards
  • Study existing pages before adding new ones — follow conventions exactly
  • All pages must link to assets/css/style.css and assets/js/app.js
  • Navigation must use the .nav-links pattern; app.js handles active state
  • Run npm run test:unit before any PR — all tests must pass
  • Run npm run test:e2e after any HTML, CSS, or JS change
Review Process
  • All PRs are reviewed by project maintainers
  • Policy changes require citing source material from the chat logs or external primary sources
  • Structural changes — new pillars, new rule families — require discussion in an Issue first
  • Adversarial review is applied: we actively try to disprove claims before accepting them
Source Quality
  • Primary sources first: federal statutes, court opinions, official government data
  • Then: peer-reviewed academic research
  • Then: established non-partisan research institutions (Pew, CBO, KFF, Brookings)
  • Advocacy sources: cite only for attribution, never as neutral fact
  • Verify every URL before submitting — AI hallucinated citations are not acceptable
On AI-Generated Contributions

Using AI tools — like GitHub Copilot, ChatGPT, or similar assistants — to help draft or research contributions is allowed. It is also not required. Many of the most valuable contributions to this project need no AI tooling at all: policy review, writing, organizing, legal work, and testing can all be done entirely by you.

The rule is simple: all AI-generated content must be reviewed by a human before it is submitted. AI tools can and do produce plausible-sounding but incorrect information — fabricated citations, wrong dates, misattributed quotes, subtly wrong statistics. Every factual claim in a pull request must be independently verified against a real, accessible source. "The AI said so" is not a citation.

If you used AI assistance, note it in the PR description. This is not a mark against you — it helps reviewers know where to focus their attention.

Setting Up a Local Environment

If you want to run the site on your own computer — to preview your edits in a browser, run the automated tests, or work on the code — this section walks you through that setup.

If this section feels technical and intimidating, skip it entirely. You can contribute policy feedback, write research, test the site from GitHub Pages, or open issues without any of this. This section exists for people who want to work on the code or run tests locally.

What you will need

Git
The version control tool — the thing that tracks changes.
Mac: Open your terminal and run xcode-select --install. Git is included.
Windows: Download and install Git for Windows.
Linux: Run sudo apt install git (Debian/Ubuntu) or sudo dnf install git (Fedora).
No terminal? GitHub Desktop is a free visual app that handles Git without any command line.
Node.js (version 18 or newer)
Node.js is what runs the test tools. Download it at nodejs.org — choose the version labeled LTS (long-term support). The installer also installs npm, which you will use to install project dependencies. You only need to do this once.
A code editor (recommended, not required)
Visual Studio Code is free, works on all platforms, and is what most contributors use. For simple HTML edits — fixing a sentence, adding a citation — any text editor will do, including Notepad.

Step by step: getting the project running locally

1
Fork and clone the repository
First, fork the project on GitHub (see the Contributing section above). Then download your fork. In a terminal:

git clone https://github.com/YOUR-USERNAME/freedom-and-dignity-project.git

Replace YOUR-USERNAME with your GitHub username. This creates a folder on your computer with all the project files in it.
2
Install dependencies
Navigate into the project folder: cd freedom-and-dignity-project

Then install the project's tools: npm install

This downloads Vitest (unit tests) and Playwright (browser tests). It only needs to be done once.
3
Preview the site in your browser
Start a local web server: npm run serve

Then open http://localhost:5500 in your browser. You will see the project running on your machine. Refresh the page after saving any HTML or CSS file to see your changes. Press Ctrl+C in the terminal to stop the server.
4
Run the unit tests
Before submitting any pull request, run: npm run test:unit

This runs 40 fast automated checks verifying the data structures are consistent. All tests should pass. If any fail, read the error message, find what changed, and fix it before submitting.
5
Run the end-to-end tests (after HTML, CSS, or JS changes)
If you changed any HTML, CSS, or JavaScript, also run: npm run test:e2e

This launches a headless Firefox browser and walks through the site automatically, checking that pages load correctly, links work, and key elements are present. If a test fails, the message will point you to the exact page and element causing the problem.

If you get stuck at any step, open a GitHub Issue describing what you tried and what happened. Getting setup friction down is important to us, and your report helps the next person.

Using AI Tools — Optional

AI tools like GitHub Copilot can be useful for navigating a large codebase, drafting policy language, or researching policy areas. They are entirely optional. Policy review, writing, organizing, legal work, and testing can all be done without any AI tools. Do not feel pressure to use them.

  • Human review required All AI-generated content must be reviewed by a human before it is submitted. AI output is a draft — a starting point — not a finished contribution.
  • Verify every source AI tools frequently generate plausible-looking citations that do not exist, or that exist but do not say what is claimed. Open every URL. Read the source. Confirm it supports the claim before including it.
  • Disclose AI use If your pull request was drafted with significant AI assistance, note it in the PR description. It helps reviewers know where to focus their scrutiny. It is not a penalty.
  • AI ≠ authority AI tools can misstate platform positions, conflate policy concepts, and produce confident-sounding errors. The canonical positions for this project live in the pillar pages and source chat logs — not in what the AI believes them to be.

Setting Up GitHub Copilot

If you want to use Copilot when working on this project, here is how to do it usefully. This is optional.

1
Get access to Copilot
GitHub Copilot is available in VS Code, JetBrains IDEs, Neovim, and the browser. It requires a GitHub account and has a free tier. GitHub's full setup guide is here. The Copilot Chat extension is especially useful for asking questions about an unfamiliar codebase.
2
This project's instructions are already set up
The repository includes .github/copilot-instructions.md, which gives Copilot context about the project's conventions, values, and rules. When you clone the repo and open it in VS Code, Copilot uses these instructions automatically. No configuration needed.
3
Writing good instructions for your own projects
For your own repositories, create .github/copilot-instructions.md. Write it in plain language: what the project is, conventions to follow, what to avoid. The more specific you are, the more useful Copilot becomes. GitHub's documentation on custom instructions explains the full format.
4
Copilot as an autonomous agent
Copilot can be assigned tasks in GitHub Issues and pull requests, where it researches, writes, and commits code on its own. It works best on well-defined, bounded tasks (writing a test, fixing a specific bug, expanding a section). For judgment-heavy work — deciding on policy positions, evaluating sources, structural decisions — human judgment is required. The Copilot agent documentation explains setup and expectations. The same human-review rule applies to everything it produces.

Practical tip: AI is most useful on this project for navigating the HTML structure, understanding ID schemas, and drafting first versions of policy language. It is least reliable for citation research — always verify sources yourself, regardless of how confident the AI sounds.
