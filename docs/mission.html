A Third Bill of Rights for the 21st Century

Our Mission

This is not a political party. It is a framework — a set of structural demands that any person, party, or movement can adopt, build on, and organize around.

The Problem

The U.S. Constitution is a remarkable document. Written in 1787, ratified in 1789, it is one of the oldest operating constitutional frameworks in the world. The framers built in mechanisms for change because they knew they would be wrong about some things. They were right about that.

But the Constitution was written for a different era. The amendment process — requiring a two-thirds supermajority of both houses of Congress or a convention called by two-thirds of state legislatures, followed by ratification by three-fourths of the states — was deliberately designed to be difficult.[1] That difficulty made sense in 1787. In today's politically polarized America, it has become effectively impossible in practice. The last amendment was ratified in 1992.[2] In the more than three decades since, the world has changed beyond recognition: the internet, social media, artificial intelligence, mass surveillance, global climate change, global oligarchy — and the constitutional framework governing the most powerful nation in human history has not kept pace.

What the founders could not fully foresee: organized political parties weaponizing procedural rules to paralyze government, money in politics flowing at unlimited scale after Citizens United,[3] social media platforms engineered to maximize outrage rather than inform, a 24-hour news cycle that punishes complexity and rewards conflict, and the rise of what can only be called proto-authoritarian politics in a country that believed itself immune. The checks and balances built into the Constitution can be dismantled faster than they can be restored. Impeachment — a last-resort democratic safeguard — has become purely partisan theater.

The vulnerabilities were always there. We have simply run out of the luxury of ignoring them.

What This Is

This is a platform to modernize American government — to finish what Franklin Roosevelt started in his 1944 State of the Union address, when he proposed a Second Bill of Rights.[4]

FDR argued that political freedom alone is not enough. "True individual freedom cannot exist without economic security and independence," he said. "Necessitous men are not free men." He proposed eight new rights: to a useful and remunerative job, to adequate earnings, to a decent home, to medical care, to education, to protection from the economic fear of old age, sickness, accident, and unemployment. Congress never passed them. Roosevelt died sixteen months later. Eighty years on, they remain unfinished business.

"True individual freedom cannot exist without economic security and independence. Necessitous men are not free men." — Franklin D. Roosevelt, January 11, 1944[4]

This platform finishes Roosevelt's work — and goes further. The rights challenges of the 21st century require protections the founders and even FDR could not have imagined: digital rights and freedom from algorithmic manipulation; the right to breathable air and a stable climate; the right to clean water — to drink, to use, and to pass on to the next generation — which has gone largely unaddressed in federal law and is now under acute threat from industrial pollution, drought, and privatization; bodily autonomy secured by law; immigration dignity; and protection from the surveillance capitalism that now mediates nearly every aspect of daily life. A Third Bill of Rights for a different century.

This is not a political party. It does not endorse candidates or run for office. It is a framework — a set of structural demands that any person, party, or movement can adopt, quote, build on, and organize around. The ideas belong to anyone who wants to use them.

What It Does

Six core commitments organize this platform:

1
Restores and protects democratic institutions. Elections must be free, fair, and trusted. The vote is sacred. Gerrymandering, voter suppression, and electoral manipulation are attacks on democracy itself — not merely policy disagreements about which reasonable people differ. They are structural threats, and they require structural answers.
2
Restores checks and balances. Presidential power has expanded far beyond what the founders intended, through executive orders, emergency declarations, and decades of deference by Congress. No one — no president, no party, no court — should be able to dismantle the mechanisms that hold power accountable. This platform establishes the structural limits that prevent authoritarian capture of democratic government.
3
Ensures the rule of law — no exceptions. Not for the wealthy. Not for the powerful. Not for anyone who holds or has held high office. The principle that no one is above the law must have teeth — real enforcement mechanisms, independent prosecutors, and judicial accountability — not just rhetoric deployed selectively when politically convenient.
4
Expands and protects rights. Civil rights. Economic rights. Digital rights. Bodily autonomy. Climate rights. Immigration dignity. The right to clean water and clean air — as fundamental, non-negotiable human rights, not privileges contingent on wealth or geography. The right to healthcare, housing, education, and a dignified retirement. Rights that exist only on paper, without legal enforcement and structural protection, are not real rights.
5
Restores and strengthens the social safety net. A society that allows its members to fall through cracks in the floor — to lose healthcare because of job loss, to lose housing because of medical debt, to grow old in poverty after a lifetime of work — is not a free society. Freedom requires material security as its foundation. Economic precarity is not a personal failure; it is a structural condition that policy can address.
6
Makes government accountable, transparent, and honest. Corruption — whether legal or illegal — has hollowed out democratic governance. Lobbying, revolving doors, dark money, conflicts of interest, regulatory capture: the infrastructure of legalized corruption must be dismantled. Government must be accountable to citizens, not to the concentrated economic interests that fund political campaigns.

Why It Matters Now

The vulnerabilities that have always existed in the American democratic framework have never been so clearly demonstrated as they are in this moment. Americans across the partisan divide recognize that something has broken, or is breaking — that the system is under stress in ways that feel unfamiliar and serious. They disagree, often sharply, about what has broken and why. This platform does not require agreement on the cause. It requires only recognition that structural problems require structural solutions, not merely different personalities in power.

Political polarization in the United States has reached historic levels.[5] The share of Americans who hold consistently liberal or consistently conservative views has doubled over the past two decades. The share who view the opposing party not merely as wrong but as an active threat to the nation has grown dramatically. This is not only a cultural phenomenon — not simply a matter of heated rhetoric that will cool on its own. It is a structural phenomenon. Systems under this degree of internal stress expose their weakest points. Design flaws that were tolerable under normal political conditions become load-bearing problems under pressure.

The system can be broken. It can be broken faster than anyone expected. That is not a partisan statement. It is an observation about structural design. No constitutional framework — not even this one — is self-executing or self-repairing. Democratic institutions survive because the people who operate within them choose to respect them. When that choice becomes conditional, the institutions become fragile. We are not powerless in the face of that fragility. We can identify the structural weaknesses. We can propose remedies. We can build a coalition around fixing them. That is the purpose of this platform.

No System Is Perfect

This platform is imperfect. We hold it to rigorous standards — meaning we actively try to disprove our own claims, acknowledge contradicting evidence, and revise when we are wrong. That is not a weakness. It is the only honest way to do this work.

A platform that cannot be challenged, revised, and improved is not a democratic platform. Everything here is a proposal — grounded in research and argument, open to scrutiny. The source material, the policy catalog, the canonical policy IDs, the version history — all of it is tracked and publicly traceable. If something in this platform is wrong, you can find where it came from and why it was included.

We believe in the ideas here. We hold them to a standard of evidence. Those two things are not the same, and the distinction matters. Conviction is necessary for political action. Evidence is what distinguishes conviction from ideology. We are trying to hold both at once.

The 22 Pillars

This platform is organized into 22 policy pillars, grouped under five structural foundations. Each pillar represents a distinct policy domain with its own canonical rules, research synthesis, and constitutional analysis. Together they form a comprehensive structural agenda — not a wish list, but a set of concrete, defensible proposals with legal grounding and evidentiary support.

Accountable Power
Executive power limits, anti-corruption, checks and balances, term limits
Clean Democracy
Elections and representation, campaign finance, legislative reform
Equal Justice
Civil rights, policing and accountability, courts, immigration
Real Freedom
Healthcare, bodily autonomy, digital rights, information integrity, gun policy
Freedom to Thrive
Labor, housing, education, environment, taxation, consumer rights
