What Is a Foundation?

The five foundations are not policy positions — they are structural commitments. Each names a category of systemic failure that must be addressed before specific policies can take root and last. They answer the question: what has to be true about our institutions, our laws, and our economy for a free society to actually exist?

Foundations → Pillars

Each foundation generates a set of policy pillars — specific areas where the structural commitment becomes concrete demands. A pillar is not free-standing; it is an expression of the foundation it rests on. Read a foundation to understand the architecture. Read its pillars to understand the specific fights.

Foundation I

Accountable Power

No one is above the law. Power without accountability is not leadership — it is dominance.

Why This Is Foundational: Before any policy can take hold and last, power must answer to someone. When executives override law without consequence, when courts serve ideology rather than justice, when agencies are captured by the industries they regulate — every other reform is temporary. Accountable Power is the prerequisite. It is the structural architecture that makes everything else possible and durable. The five pillars beneath it define the specific mechanisms: how executive power is constrained, how institutional checks are maintained, who can hold office and for how long, how courts are structured, and how federal agencies are governed and held to account.

What It Demands

  • Enforceable limits on executive power, with independent oversight
  • Immunity for public officials ended — no one is above prosecution
  • Transparent lobbying, procurement, and regulatory processes
  • Judicial accountability: ethics rules, recusal standards, term limits for federal judges
  • Checks and balances restored and made structurally resistant to capture

What It Rejects

  • Presidential immunity doctrines that put the executive above the law
  • Signing statements that effectively nullify legislation
  • Agency heads who serve at the pleasure of the president using that power to bury enforcement
  • Judicial lifetime appointments without accountability mechanisms
  • Corporate officers who externalize harm and walk away unpunished

Foundation II

Clean Democracy

Your vote must mean what it says. One person, one vote — not one dollar, one vote.

Why This Is Foundational: Policy reflects the preferences of those with the power to enact it. If elections can be purchased, if maps are drawn to predetermine outcomes, if the information environment is controlled by companies with financial stakes in specific political results — the people cannot actually govern themselves. Clean Democracy is the process foundation: the mechanism by which a legitimate majority translates its will into law. Without it, every other foundation is subject to capture and reversal by whoever controls the machinery. The four pillars beneath it address elections directly, the corruption rules that must constrain public officials, the corporate consolidation that corrupts both markets and politics, and the information environment on which democracy depends.

What It Demands

  • Public financing of elections — small-dollar matching, public vouchers
  • Dark money ended through disclosure requirements and structural limits
  • Independent redistricting commissions — gerrymandering ended
  • Antitrust enforcement capable of breaking up Big Tech, Big Agriculture, Big Pharma, Big Media
  • Revolving door banned: mandatory cooling-off periods, enforceable and long

What It Rejects

  • Citizens United — corporations are not people and money is not speech
  • Dark money as a legitimate vehicle for political influence
  • Revolving door as accepted practice
  • Gerrymandered maps drawn to produce predetermined outcomes
  • Market concentration that gives a handful of companies control over public discourse

Pillars within Clean Democracy

Elections & Representation Every eligible voter must be able to vote, every vote must count, and every district must be fairly drawn. Foundation III

Equal Justice

Justice that bends for the powerful isn't justice. It's a protection racket.

Why This Is Foundational: Rights and protections that apply differently based on race, income, or origin are not rights — they are privileges. Equal Justice is the membership foundation: it defines who is actually inside the polity and entitled to its protections. Before any policy question can be answered, this question must be settled — to whom does the law actually apply? The three pillars beneath it address the specific systems where that question is being answered in practice right now, often badly: policing that must protect rather than prey, immigration built on dignity and due process, and civil rights enforcement that matches the scale of modern discrimination.

What It Demands

  • End qualified immunity — officers are accountable for civil rights violations
  • Independent oversight of law enforcement at every level
  • Criminal justice reform: end cash bail, mandatory minimums that produce racial disparity
  • Humane immigration: a functional path to status, family unity, and due process
  • Civil rights enforcement with resources sufficient to make it real

What It Rejects

  • Qualified immunity as a structural shield for officer misconduct
  • Mass detention of immigration cases without due process
  • A sentencing system that produces radically different outcomes by race
  • Prosecutorial discretion used to protect the powerful and punish the weak
  • Underfunded public defense that makes justice contingent on wealth

Pillars within Equal Justice

Equal Justice & Policing The law must apply equally. Policing must protect communities, not prey on them. Foundation IV

Real Freedom

Freedom isn't just the absence of government interference. It's the presence of real choices.

Why This Is Foundational: Freedom in the 21st century faces threats the founders could not have imagined. The most comprehensive intrusions on daily life come not only from government but from private entities — surveillance capitalism, algorithmic manipulation, the amplification of violence and fear through commercial platforms. Real Freedom updates the constitutional promise for an era when corporations have unprecedented power to shape private life and make consequential decisions about who gets hired, insured, or policed. The three pillars beneath it address where freedom is actually under threat today: in digital life, in bodily autonomy, and in the governance of technology at scale.

What It Demands

  • Comprehensive federal privacy law — your data is yours, not a commodity
  • Algorithmic transparency and accountability for automated decision-making
  • Explicit protection of bodily autonomy as a constitutional right
  • Meaningful gun reform: universal background checks, red flag laws, restrictions on weapons designed for mass casualties
  • AI regulation with democratic oversight — not self-regulation by the companies building it

What It Rejects

  • Surveillance capitalism as a legitimate business model
  • Government use of commercially available data to surveil citizens without warrant
  • A healthcare system that makes reproductive decisions for patients
  • Unlimited access to weapons of war by private individuals
  • AI systems deployed at scale without accountability for their effects

Foundation V

Freedom to Thrive

You can't be free if you're sick, homeless, or in debt.

Why This Is Foundational: FDR said it plainly in 1944: necessitous men are not free men. If people are too sick to act, too indebted to organize, too economically precarious to take political risk — every other freedom is abstract. Freedom to Thrive is the material foundation: the floor of economic and physical security below which democracy becomes theater. This is the work Roosevelt named and Congress refused to finish. The three pillars beneath it — healthcare, taxation, and the environment — are the specific policy mechanisms through which that floor is built and maintained for every person in this country.

What It Demands

  • Universal healthcare access — no person bankrupted for getting sick
  • A living wage indexed to productivity and cost of living, actually enforced
  • Wealth taxation that prevents compounding fortunes from becoming permanent aristocracy
  • Climate policy that treats the environment as infrastructure, not an optional concern
  • A food system that produces food that nourishes people and supports the farmers who grow it

What It Rejects

  • Medical bankruptcy as an acceptable outcome in a wealthy nation
  • Wages that have not kept pace with worker productivity for fifty years
  • A tax code where the wealthiest pay lower effective rates than the working class
  • Environmental deregulation in service of short-term corporate profit
  • An agricultural system controlled by four companies that produces food optimized for shelf life rather than nutrition
