# PolicyOS Definitions v1

Generated: 2026-04-26

## Purpose

This document defines terms used throughout PolicyOS and all documents that
derive from it: the platform values, system principles, and authoring OS.

Definitions here are operative, not encyclopedic. Each definition states what
a term means within this platform's framework — including what it explicitly
includes or excludes, and why the precision matters.

## Status

This is a prose-first document. Each definition carries a pre-assigned ID to
enable future machine-readable referencing once the PolicyOS principles and
authoring OS layers are stable. IDs are stable — definitions may be revised
but IDs will not be reassigned.

## ID format

Definition IDs use the form `DEFN-XXXX-0001` where `DEFN` is the domain and
`XXXX` is a short code for the concept. This is consistent with the PolicyOS
v2 ID format (`XXXX-XXXX-NNNN`) and is intended to migrate into the DB at
the PolicyOS canonicalization milestone.

## How to use this document

When a PolicyOS rule, principle, or value uses a defined term, it should cite
the definition ID in a comment or annotation: e.g., `(see DEFN-PERS-0001)`.
This creates a traceable link between operative language and its definition,
and flags the definition as load-bearing for that rule.

## Definitions

---

### DEFN-PERS-0001 — Person

For the purposes of PolicyOS and all documents that derive from it, **"person"**
means a living human being.

This definition is intentional and explicit. "Person" in this platform does not
extend to:

- corporations, associations, or other legal entities created by law
- AI systems, automated decision systems, or other non-human agents
- abstractions such as markets, states, or institutions

Rights, protections, duties, and dignitary claims in this platform attach to
living human beings. When this platform speaks of protecting "persons," it means
human beings — not legal constructs or entities granted legal personality by
statute or judicial decision.

This definition does not depend on citizenship, legal immigration status,
documentation, residence, or administrative classification. A person is a person
regardless of what any authority says about their legal status.

Personhood under this platform attaches at birth. Fetuses, embryos, and the
unborn are not "persons" within this framework. Policies that would assign
personhood to the unborn in ways that constrain or override the bodily autonomy,
rights, or decision-making authority of living persons are inconsistent with this
platform's values — particularly Value 3 (Real Liberty).

### DEFN-FHUM-0001 — Fully human

To be treated as **"fully human"** means to be recognized as possessing inherent
dignity that is:

- **Non-contingent**: not earned, not conditional on behavior, status, wealth,
  productivity, or social standing.
- **Non-revocable**: not forfeit through conviction, administrative
  categorization, incapacity, or institutional designation.
- **Irreducible**: not reducible to economic utility, labor capacity, data value,
  risk score, or instrumental use.

Being "fully human" in this platform's terms means at minimum:

- being recognized as a subject — a person with interior experience, capacity
  for suffering and flourishing, relational ties, and stakes in the future
- being entitled to humane treatment regardless of institutional context, not
  only despite it
- being entitled to recognition as a holder of rights, not merely as a recipient
  of managed services or a risk to be contained

This standard is explicitly designed to include people who have historically been
subjected to partial or conditional humanity by law, institution, or social
practice: people who are incarcerated; people experiencing homelessness; people
with disabilities, psychiatric conditions, or cognitive differences; undocumented
people; people receiving public assistance; people in institutional care; and
others whose claim to full humanity has been treated as contingent or qualified.

No policy that relies on the implicit treatment of any of these groups as less
than fully human satisfies the floor of Value 1 (Human Dignity).

### DEFN-HARM-0001 — Harm

**"Harm"** means a concrete negative effect on a person's wellbeing, dignity,
liberty, material security, or equal standing.

Harm includes physical, psychological, economic, and dignitary harm. It is not
limited to harm caused by direct state action — foreseeable third-party harm
that policy creates, enables, or fails to prevent where there is a duty to act
is within scope.

The fact that a harm is diffuse, statistical, or distributed across a large
population does not make it less real or less policy-relevant. Harm that falls
disproportionately on already-disadvantaged groups receives heightened weight
under this platform's values.

Harm to future persons from current policy decisions — ecological, economic,
structural — is within scope.

### DEFN-COER-0001 — Coercion

**"Coercion"** means the use of force, threat, deprivation, or constraint —
by public or private actors — to compel or suppress a person's choices in ways
that override meaningful agency.

Coercion includes:

- **Physical coercion**: force, threat of force, or physical constraint.
- **Economic coercion**: conditioning access to essential needs on submission,
  compliance, or surrender of rights.
- **Administrative coercion**: using procedural complexity, documentation
  requirements, or bureaucratic burden as a mechanism of control or exclusion.
- **Structural coercion**: designing systems so that a person has no real
  alternative — leaving formal freedom intact while eliminating all real options.

A system that leaves a person formally "free to choose" while eliminating every
meaningful alternative is coercive under this definition. The distinction between
persuasion and coercion turns on whether meaningful, real alternatives remain.

---

### DEFN-ESYS-0001 — Essential systems

**"Essential systems"** are systems, services, or institutions whose denial or
failure would foreseeably result in material harm, loss of dignity, or
foreclosure of meaningful agency for persons who depend on them.

Examples include: healthcare, housing, food access, water, sanitation,
education, legal process, income support, public safety, and electoral
participation.

A system's essentialness is determined by what actually happens to real people
when it fails — not by whether it is publicly or privately operated, and not
by whether a formal substitute theoretically exists elsewhere.

---

### DEFN-DEPR-0001 — Deprivation

**"Deprivation"** means the enforced absence of basic conditions required for
a dignified, functional life — including adequate food, shelter, healthcare,
safety, income, and access to essential systems.

Deprivation may be caused by:

- direct state action (e.g., stripping benefits, punitive denial)
- policy design that produces foreseeable scarcity or exclusion
- failure to act where there is a duty to secure access

The fact that deprivation arises from "market conditions" or the absence of
affirmative government action does not exempt it from PolicyOS review where
policy choices created, maintain, or could remedy those conditions.

### DEFN-ACCT-0001 — Meaningful accountability

**"Accountability"** is meaningful when it has all of the following:

1. An identifiable, attributable decision-maker whose actions can be traced.
2. An accessible review path for affected persons — accessible in practice,
   not only in theory.
3. Enforceable standards with real consequences for violation.
4. Actual consequences proportional to harm.

Accountability that is formally available but practically inaccessible does not
satisfy this definition. Accountability that results only in symbolic consequences
— acknowledgment without remedy, review without recourse — does not satisfy this
definition. Under-enforcement that becomes de facto nullification of a standard
is an accountability failure regardless of whether the formal mechanism exists.

---

### DEFN-CAPT-0001 — Capture

**"Capture"** is the process by which a system meant to serve the public interest
is redirected — through sustained influence, structural dependency, personnel
placement, funding relationships, or economic power — to serve the interests of
a subset of actors instead.

Capture may be:

- **Regulatory**: an oversight body that primarily serves the interests of the
  industries or actors it is meant to regulate.
- **Political**: institutions or processes redirected to serve incumbents,
  funders, or organized minorities at the expense of the public.
- **Interpretive**: definitional drift — the gradual reinterpretation of rules
  or standards in ways that systematically favor powerful actors while
  maintaining formal compliance.

Capture need not involve explicit corruption. Systematic structural bias that
produces the same result as corruption — the consistent redirection of public
systems toward private benefit — counts as capture under this definition.

---

### DEFN-CPOW-0001 — Concentrated power

**"Concentrated power"** is the accumulation of decision-making authority,
economic control, informational advantage, or institutional influence by a
single actor or small group of actors in ways that:

1. reduce meaningful alternatives for others;
2. insulate the actor from accountability; or
3. enable the actor to set the terms of participation for others.

Concentration may be public (state power), private (corporate or economic),
or hybrid (public-private arrangements that shift accountability away from
either). The relevant question is not the formal character of the actor but
whether the accumulation of power produces domination, eliminates recourse,
or forecloses alternatives for others.

---

### DEFN-GFTH-0001 — Good faith / bad faith

**"Good faith"** means acting with genuine intent to fulfill the purpose of a
rule, standard, or obligation — not merely its formal letter.

**"Bad faith"** means deliberately exploiting gaps, ambiguities, or technicalities
to achieve outcomes that contradict the evident purpose of a rule. Bad faith
includes:

- systematic under-enforcement used as de facto nullification
- procedural obstruction used as substantive denial
- definitional manipulation to progressively narrow protections
- compliance theater — formal conformance that preserves the form of a rule
  while defeating its substance

A pattern of bad faith is a structural failure, not merely an individual one.
Policy that is systematically vulnerable to bad-faith exploitation fails the
durability floor of Value 11 (Durability Against Capture and Neglect).
