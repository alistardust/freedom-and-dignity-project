# PolicyOS 1.0 Rules Proposal

Generated: 2026-04-26

## Purpose

PolicyOS is the rule layer that governs how the platform should be designed
across all pillars. It is not a policy domain. It is the operating logic for
how laws, institutions, enforcement systems, and technical systems should work
so the platform remains coherent, rights-preserving, enforceable, and resistant
to abuse.

This draft presents a proposed PolicyOS 1.0 structure and rule set.

## Core idea

PolicyOS is based on several simple claims:

- policy should behave like a well-designed system
- rights should be real in practice, not only formal on paper
- power should always be attributable, reviewable, and constrained
- systems should fail well instead of hiding abuse or collapsing into opacity
- enforcement should have teeth, remain proportionate, and trigger repair when systems fail
- no one should lose access to rights because of geography, administrative burden, hidden logic, or concentrated power

## Relationship to the foundations

PolicyOS is built from the five foundations:

- `Accountable Power` supplies accountability, review, auditability, anti-centralization, and corrective action
- `Clean Democracy` supplies anti-capture, public-interest regulation, resistance to concentrated private power, and anti-extraction design
- `Equal Justice` supplies equal treatment, challenge rights, anti-bias, and human accountability in consequential decisions
- `Real Freedom` supplies explicit usable rights, no hidden rules, explainability, auditability, and limits on consequential AI and surveillance
- `Freedom to Thrive` supplies practical access, anti-deprivation logic, material preconditions for freedom, and protection of health, habitability, and public welfare

## Canonical structure

PolicyOS 1.0 uses six canonical families:

- `KERN` — the universal kernel, mandatory for all pillars
- `GEOG` — the geography and practical-access overlay
- `FEDR` — the federalism and anti-centralization overlay
- `REGD` — the regulatory-design and anti-capture overlay
- `ENFA` — the enforcement and audit architecture overlay
- `AIGV` — the AI-governance overlay

Only `KERN` is universal. The overlays apply where relevant to the design area.

## What does not belong in canonical PolicyOS

Some content belongs elsewhere:

- broad value commitments belong in foundation prose
- movement identity, communications posture, and historical origin belong in strategy or project prose
- highly domain-specific mechanics belong in pillar rules

Examples of pillar-level material include elections-specific administration
details, EPA-specific audit cadence and metrics, and domain-specific corporate
liability mechanics beyond the shared enforcement architecture.

## Inheritance model

Every pillar should inherit:

- `KERN`

Pillars should inherit overlays only where the design area requires them:

- `GEOG`
- `FEDR`
- `REGD`
- `ENFA`
- `AIGV`

## Proposed rules

### `KERN` — universal kernel

1. `PLOS-KERN-0001`
Platform design must remain complete, internally consistent, and auditable
across pillars, implementation rules, and enforcement design.

2. `PLOS-KERN-0002`
No person, office, agency, court, corporation, or contractor may be placed
beyond the law or beyond accountability under the law.

3. `PLOS-KERN-0003`
Fundamental rights attach to all persons under United States jurisdiction and
may not be reduced except where a distinction is explicit, narrow, reviewable,
and rights-preserving.

4. `PLOS-KERN-0004`
No system may concentrate unchecked power in any public or private actor
without independent oversight, enforcement, review, and removal mechanisms.

5. `PLOS-KERN-0005`
All exercises of state or delegated authority must be attributable, logged,
auditable, and reviewable.

6. `PLOS-KERN-0006`
Government authority may not be delegated to private actors in ways that reduce
accountability, transparency, due process, or rights protection.

7. `PLOS-KERN-0007`
Any system that affects rights, liberty, safety, access, or eligibility must be
transparent, auditable, and explainable to affected people and to the public at
the level appropriate to the risk.

8. `PLOS-KERN-0008`
No system may use hidden rules, inaccessible information, trade-secret shields,
or black-box procedures to determine consequential outcomes.

9. `PLOS-KERN-0009`
Public decision-making must be grounded in verifiable evidence and clearly
defined standards rather than secrecy, arbitrary discretion, or ideology
masquerading as process.

10. `PLOS-KERN-0010`
No system may make or materially influence adverse decisions affecting rights,
liberty, or essential access without meaningful human accountability and review.

11. `PLOS-KERN-0011`
Systems may assist in analysis, triage, and efficiency, but they may not
replace accountable human judgment in consequential decisions.

12. `PLOS-KERN-0012`
Systems must be designed to prevent bias amplification, structural unfairness,
and asymmetrical process design, including fast-approval and slow-denial
patterns.

13. `PLOS-KERN-0013`
Every consequential decision system must include mechanisms for challenge,
appeal, correction, pause, and independent review.

14. `PLOS-KERN-0014`
Access to rights and essential public systems may not be defeated in practice
through administrative burden, delay, opacity, cost, or procedural complexity.

15. `PLOS-KERN-0015`
Systems must be designed to prevent foreseeable abuse, exploitation,
corruption, and rights-violating failure modes rather than merely punishing
them after harm occurs.

16. `PLOS-KERN-0016`
No system may rely on deprivation, coercion, fear, or chronic precarity as a
primary mechanism of compliance or control.

17. `PLOS-KERN-0017`
Incentive structures may not reward denial, extraction, concealment, or
deprivation of rights or essential services.

18. `PLOS-KERN-0018`
Laws and policy rules must be written in clear, enforceable language with
defined scope, intent, triggers, and application standards.

19. `PLOS-KERN-0019`
Every consequential law or policy framework must include a plain-language
summary of purpose, design logic, and real-world effect.

20. `PLOS-KERN-0020`
Rules must be reviewed before enactment for conflicts, loopholes, exploit
paths, contradictory incentives, and foreseeable abuse patterns.

21. `PLOS-KERN-0021`
Systems must be periodically reviewed for obsolescence, ambiguity,
under-enforcement, and changed technological or social conditions.

22. `PLOS-KERN-0022`
Laws and systems must be designed to adapt to technological and societal change
without requiring crisis-driven overhaul.

23. `PLOS-KERN-0023`
No system may persist in a known broken or harmful state due to political
inertia, administrative convenience, or procedural barriers to repair.

24. `PLOS-KERN-0024`
Systems must be stress-tested against bad actors, misuse, edge cases, gaming,
fraud, and failure scenarios before and after deployment.

25. `PLOS-KERN-0025`
Systems must include safeguards against gaming, manipulation, and strategic
exploitation.

26. `PLOS-KERN-0026`
When systemic failure patterns are identified, mandatory corrective action,
public reporting, and independent review must be triggered.

### `GEOG` — geography and practical-access overlay

1. `PLOS-GEOG-0001`
Fundamental rights and essential services may not vary by geography in ways
that create unequal classes of people.

2. `PLOS-GEOG-0002`
Where systems are decentralized, national standards must guarantee baseline
equality, enforceable access, and rights protection.

3. `PLOS-GEOG-0003`
Practical access to healthcare, justice, education, voting, and other core
public systems may not be undermined by distance, delay, administrative burden,
or local noncompliance.

4. `PLOS-GEOG-0004`
Where local access fails, cross-jurisdiction access and travel support must be
available when necessary to make the right usable in practice.

### `FEDR` — federalism and anti-centralization overlay

1. `PLOS-FEDR-0001`
High-risk systems should not create single-point centralization where
distributed control and independent counterweights are needed to prevent abuse
or systemic failure.

2. `PLOS-FEDR-0002`
Where authority is distributed across levels of government, national baselines
must protect equal rights and access, while administration may remain
decentralized only where that structure reduces concentration risk without
undermining enforceability, auditability, reviewability, or practical equality.

3. `PLOS-FEDR-0003`
High-risk public systems must include multiple independent oversight layers and
safeguards against both subnational abuse and federal abuse.

4. `PLOS-FEDR-0004`
Legal and institutional design must balance anti-centralization with
enforceable national equality baselines rather than treating either local
control or federal control as an absolute good.

### `REGD` — regulatory-design and anti-capture overlay

1. `PLOS-REGD-0001`
Regulation must protect safety, quality, fairness, durability, and the public
interest; it may not be weakened solely to reduce cost or increase private
profit.

2. `PLOS-REGD-0002`
Regulatory systems must distinguish between genuine protections and rules that
mainly create delay, exclusion, arbitrary gatekeeping, or artificial scarcity.

3. `PLOS-REGD-0003`
Removal or weakening of safeguards must be evidence-based and must preserve or
improve the underlying protection in practice.

4. `PLOS-REGD-0004`
Regulatory processes may not be structured to create opacity, arbitrary
discretion, or exploitative gatekeeping that enables abuse.

5. `PLOS-REGD-0005`
Regulatory systems must include anti-capture safeguards, independent oversight,
public transparency, and limits on industry influence over rulemaking and
enforcement.

6. `PLOS-REGD-0006`
Rules that are symbolic, systematically under-enforced, or widely violated in
practice are system failures and must be corrected.

7. `PLOS-REGD-0007`
Regulatory frameworks must be periodically reviewed to remove rules that do not
serve a legitimate public-interest function while strengthening those that do.

### `ENFA` — enforcement and audit architecture overlay

1. `PLOS-ENFA-0001`
High-risk systems must maintain auditable records, standardized reporting, and
evidence trails sufficient for investigation, challenge, appeal, and
enforcement.

2. `PLOS-ENFA-0002`
Where risk, scale, or public harm warrants it, compliance systems may require
both internal controls and independent third-party audit or review.

3. `PLOS-ENFA-0003`
Enforcement systems should monitor for fraud, concealment, recurring
violations, and systemic abuse patterns rather than relying only on
complaint-driven detection.

4. `PLOS-ENFA-0004`
Repeated or systemic violations must trigger escalating intervention, which may
include corrective orders, fines, operating restrictions, structural remedies,
suspension, or removal of authority where proportionate.

5. `PLOS-ENFA-0005`
Liability may extend to responsible individuals where deliberate misconduct,
gross negligence, fraud, or failure of oversight is established with due
process and clear evidentiary standards.

### `AIGV` — AI-governance overlay

1. `PLOS-AIGV-0001`
AI systems that materially affect rights, safety, access, or public
decision-making must maintain provenance records sufficient for audit,
challenge, accountability, and incident reconstruction.

2. `PLOS-AIGV-0002`
AI-generated or AI-modified content used in consequential contexts must be
clearly labeled where feasible, and unlabeled use in high-risk contexts is
prohibited.

3. `PLOS-AIGV-0003`
AI may not silently replace legally or professionally required human judgment
in medicine, law, education, public administration, or other protected domains.

4. `PLOS-AIGV-0004`
High-impact AI systems must be subject to pre-deployment assessment, ongoing
monitoring, incident reporting, and mandatory disclosure of material failures or
rights violations.

5. `PLOS-AIGV-0005`
AI governance must include third-party auditability, independent review, and
post-deployment corrective action; self-attestation alone is insufficient.

6. `PLOS-AIGV-0006`
High-capability AI obligations must scale with capability, autonomy, deployment
scope, and misuse or catastrophic-risk potential.

7. `PLOS-AIGV-0007`
Frontier or high-impact AI systems may not be deployed where catastrophic,
systemic, or unreviewable risks cannot be sufficiently mitigated.

8. `PLOS-AIGV-0008`
Affected individuals, regulators, and courts must be able to review
rights-impacting AI systems without trade-secret claims swallowing
accountability.

9. `PLOS-AIGV-0009`
AI policy must distinguish assistive use, advisory use, and delegated decision
use, with stricter safeguards as autonomy and consequence increase.

10. `PLOS-AIGV-0010`
Public procurement of AI must require rights-impact review, interoperability
review, data-governance review, and post-deployment accountability obligations.

11. `PLOS-AIGV-0011`
Release of high-capability AI models, weights, or tools must consider misuse
risk, safety mitigations, and public-interest obligations proportional to
capability.

12. `PLOS-AIGV-0012`
AI systems may not be used to impersonate identifiable people in ways that
facilitate fraud, coercion, denial of rights, or severe reputational harm.

13. `PLOS-AIGV-0013`
AI may be used to expand accessibility and disability support where it
increases autonomy and access without replacing legally required accommodations
or rights protections.

14. `PLOS-AIGV-0014`
AI systems must be built with non-optional safety, security, and
misuse-prevention controls that cannot be disabled, bypassed, or overridden in
ordinary operation.

15. `PLOS-AIGV-0015`
Any override of AI safety controls must be narrowly defined, federally approved
in advance, time-limited, risk-ranked, scope-limited, mitigation-bound, and
publicly disclosed except for tightly justified security confidentiality.

16. `PLOS-AIGV-0016`
AI developers and deployers must actively design, test, and update safeguards
against misuse by bad actors and against newly identified failure patterns.

17. `PLOS-AIGV-0017`
A constitutionally established and funded public authority must regulate
high-impact AI systems to prevent harm, misuse, and systemic risk.

18. `PLOS-AIGV-0018`
That authority must have power to establish, update, and enforce binding rules
governing AI safety, deployment, auditing, procurement, and misuse prevention.

19. `PLOS-AIGV-0019`
That authority must operate with an adaptive mandate capable of responding to
technological change, emerging risks, and newly identified failure patterns.

20. `PLOS-AIGV-0020`
Baseline safety, civil-rights, labor, economic, environmental, and societal
protections for high-impact AI must be codified and actively enforced as
minimum standards.

21. `PLOS-AIGV-0021`
AI enforcement must be continuous, adequately resourced, and strong enough to
prevent systemic harm rather than merely reacting after harm becomes entrenched.

## Closing statement

PolicyOS 1.0 proposes:

- one universal kernel
- five targeted overlays
- pillar-level implementation where domain specifics are required
- foundation and strategy prose where values, identity, or project history are the right layer

This is the proposed 1.0 rule structure for further review and canonicalization.
