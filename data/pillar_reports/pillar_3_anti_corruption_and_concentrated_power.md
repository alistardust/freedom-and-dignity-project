## Core Principle
Political power must not be controlled by concentrated wealth, hidden influence, private access networks, or covert manipulation.

## Purpose
Government must answer to the public, not wealthy donors, dark-money networks, monopolistic corporations, foreign influence operations, or shadow lobbying systems.

## 1. Regulate Political Influence Broadly
- Regulate materially political spending broadly, not just direct candidate donations.
- Cover:
  - election spending,
  - large-scale issue advocacy near elections,
  - coordinated persuasion campaigns,
  - hidden influence networks,
  - and other vehicles that function like political spending in practice.

## 2. Campaign Finance Reform
- Ban corporate political donations.
- Eliminate Super PACs.
- Ban or severely restrict dark money.
- Strict donation caps.
- Full transparency in funding.
- Undo or bypass Citizens United-style distortions through constitutional and statutory reform.
- Limit large donations and reduce outsized influence.
- Consider limits on advertisement spending and broadcast/streaming political ad saturation.

## 3. Anti-Coordination Rules
- Expand the definition of coordination to include:
  - shared vendors,
  - shared data,
  - strategic alignment,
  - informal campaign-outside-group collaboration.
- Require demonstrable independence for outside groups claiming independence.
- Close loopholes that allow nominally independent but actually coordinated operations.

## 4. Funding Transparency and Beneficial Ownership
- Require disclosure of ultimate beneficial owners behind political spending.
- Ban shell companies and pass-through entities as concealment tools.
- Require traceability of major funding flows.
- Close anonymous funding channels.
- Use near-real-time disclosure where timing matters most.

## 5. Real-Time Disclosure
- Near-real-time reporting of major political spending.
- Reporting for large advocacy campaigns and influence efforts.
- Priority disclosure windows before elections and major policy decisions.

## 6. Lobbying and Shadow Influence
- Regulate formal lobbying and shadow lobbying.
- Expand disclosure rules to cover:
  - consultants,
  - strategic advisors,
  - think-tank-style paid influence campaigns,
  - lawyers and intermediaries functioning as lobbyists in practice.
- Require disclosure of meetings with public officials.
- Public calendars for senior officials.
- Limit access-based influence systems where money buys private policy access.

## 7. Revolving Door and Self-Dealing
- Ban or sharply limit revolving door between government and lobbying or regulated industries.
- Cooling-off periods for former officials.
- Ban negotiation of private employment while in public office in matters connected to one’s official role.
- Restrict conflicted appointments from industries to regulatory bodies without strong recusal and ethics systems.
- Prevent use of public office to enrich self, family, donors, or business networks.

## 8. Conflict-of-Interest Rules
- Mandatory disclosure of financial interests.
- Divestment or blind trusts where appropriate.
- Real recusal requirements.
- Criminal and financial penalties for violations.
- Public office cannot be used as a profit center.

## 9. Foreign Influence Safeguards
- Ban covert foreign influence operations.
- Ban concealed foreign-linked funding and coordination.
- Beneficial ownership tracing for political spending.
- Criminal penalties for coordination with foreign states or organizations in political manipulation.
- Ban knowing dissemination of false information on behalf of foreign states or organizations.

## 10. Information Integrity, Media, and AI
- Regulate political use of AI and targeted persuasion.
- Require disclosure of paid influence, sponsorship, and financial incentives.
- Require labeling of AI-generated or synthetic political content.
- Ban materially deceptive synthetic political media in election contexts without disclosure.
- Protect against coordinated misinformation campaigns.
- Consider civil remedies, including class-action mechanisms, for campaigns that spread dehumanizing or hateful misinformation about groups of people for the purpose of spreading hatred.
- Protect opinions and sincerely held beliefs while targeting coordinated deception, fraud, and covert influence.
- The bar for regulation should be high and require intentional harmful conduct, especially where foreign influence or deliberate deception is involved.

## 11. Journalism, Media Transparency, and Conflicts of Interest
- Require disclosure of financial incentives, conflicts of interest, and paid opinions in media contexts.
- Strengthen transparency rules for for-profit media where paid influence or conflicts shape public understanding.
- Preserve freedom of the press while increasing anti-corruption transparency.
- Avoid broad “truth-policing” regimes that can be weaponized by future authoritarians.

## 12. Anti-Monopoly and Corporate Power
- Strengthen antitrust enforcement.
- Break up monopolies.
- Prevent excessive consolidation in key industries, supply chains, and essential services.
- Protect competition and small actors.
- Limit the ability of a handful of corporations to dominate daily life, pricing, production quality, access, or distribution.
- Recognize that concentration of private economic power can become a democratic-governance problem.

## 13. Enforcement Architecture
- Independent anti-corruption enforcement body.
- Protected funding.
- Subpoena power.
- Mandatory public reporting.
- Automatic triggers for investigation.
- Criminal penalties and financial penalties.
- Whistleblower protection.
- Enforcement must not deadlock or depend on partisan goodwill.

2. Lobbying and access
3. Revolving door and self-dealing
4. Transparency, media, and AI disclosure
5. Enforcement and foreign influence

## Pressure-Test Lessons That Must Be Incorporated
- If one money channel is capped, influence will reroute elsewhere unless the rules regulate flows and networks, not just channels.
- “Independent” spending is easy to fake through vendors, data, or informal signaling.
- Shell-company and proxy-ownership rules must be closed tightly.
- Registered-lobbyist rules alone are not enough because shadow lobbying can replace them.
- Anti-corruption rules must address “legal but corrupt” systems built around access and agenda control, not only explicit bribery.
- Disclosure that arrives too late does not protect democracy.
- AI and platform-based manipulation are future-proofing necessities, not optional extras.
