## Core Principle
Fundamental rights and civil liberties must be explicit, enforceable, broadly protective, materially meaningful, adaptable to future developments, and difficult for government to erode.

## Purpose
This pillar exists to make rights explicit, modern, durable, and harder to roll back. It must reduce ambiguity where ambiguity enables abuse, clarify intent where bad-faith interpretation is likely, and provide both technical legal protections and plain-language statements of purpose.

## 1. Foundational Rights Framework
- Strengthen and operationalize the Ninth Amendment so it is enforceable rather than symbolic.
- Explicitly recognize that people retain fundamental rights beyond those specifically enumerated.
- Require rights provisions and major rights-related laws to include:
  - technical legal language,
  - plain-language statements of purpose, context, and intent.
- Require interpretation of rights-protecting provisions in light of:
  - text,
  - structure,
  - historical abuse risks,
  - stated purpose and intent,
  - anti-abuse principles,
  - and broader liberty-protective goals.
- Reject interpretive approaches that use vague or outdated language to narrow rights contrary to clearly stated purpose.
- Require new laws affecting fundamental rights to disclose:
  - what rights are implicated,
  - what government interest is being asserted,
  - why the law is narrowly tailored.
- Rights should be interpreted in favor of liberty, dignity, bodily autonomy, privacy, equal citizenship, and protection from arbitrary intrusion.

## 2. Privacy, Data, and Personal Autonomy
- Establish an explicit general right to privacy.
- Protect privacy in:
  - home and personal life,
  - communications,
  - location data,
  - digital activity,
  - medical and mental health records,
  - family and intimate relationships,
  - personal associations.
- Require warrants for government access to personal communications, content, location history, and sensitive personal records.
- Ban warrantless bulk data collection and mass surveillance.
- Ban government purchase of personal data from brokers where the government could not lawfully compel the same data directly.
- Strongly regulate or ban the sale of personally identifying information without affirmative, informed consent.
- Treat personally identifying information as controlled by the individual.
- Require rights to:
  - access,
  - correction,
  - deletion,
  - portability,
  - notice.
- Ban use of personal data for political targeting by government except in narrowly lawful circumstances.
- Protect biometric, facial recognition, gait, voiceprint, and genetic data.
- Limit data brokers and corporate data extraction.
- Discussion also recognized the need to address workplace surveillance and coercive data practices as future additions.

## 3. Bodily Autonomy and Medical Freedom
- Explicitly protect bodily autonomy as a fundamental right.
- Protect:
  - abortion,
  - contraception,
  - miscarriage care,
  - fertility treatment,
  - sterilization by choice,
  - gender-affirming care.
- Ban medically unnecessary, non-consensual surgeries on intersex infants and children except narrowly defined emergency circumstances.
- Ban conversion therapy.
- Protect informed consent as a core medical principle.
- Protect the right to refuse medical treatment, subject to narrow limits where another person’s rights are directly implicated.
- Strictly limit involuntary treatment, forced procedures, or non-consensual confinement, with due process and narrow emergency exceptions.
- Prevent government from imposing personal medical decisions based on political ideology.

## 4. Equality, Anti-Discrimination, and Equal Citizenship
- Explicitly guarantee equal rights regardless of:
  - race,
  - color,
  - ethnicity,
  - national origin,
  - sex,
  - gender,
  - sexual orientation,
  - romantic orientation,
  - disability,
  - religion,
  - age where relevant,
  - other analogous immutable or deeply rooted characteristics.
- Clarify that the list is not exhaustive.
- Prohibit discrimination in:
  - law,
  - enforcement,
  - voting,
  - employment,
  - housing,
  - education,
  - healthcare,
  - public accommodations,
  - family life,
  - access to government services.
- Ban discriminatory enforcement and selective prosecution.
- Ban racial profiling.
- Require equal protection to function as a floor for expansion, not contraction.
- Anti-backsliding protections so established equal-rights protections cannot be casually narrowed.

## 5. Freedom of Conscience, Religion, and Non-Religion
- Explicitly protect both freedom of religion and freedom from religion.
- Prohibit compelled religious observance or participation.
- Prohibit state preference for religion over non-religion, or one religion over another.
- Protect sincerely held beliefs, worship, and peaceful religious expression.
- Clarify that religion is not a valid excuse for:
  - abuse,
  - forced labor,
  - fraud,
  - medical neglect,
  - discrimination in protected contexts,
  - deprivation of civil rights,
  - other rights violations.
- Permit revocation or limitation of tax-exempt status for organizations that:
  - commit abuse,
  - systematically violate rights,
  - operate coercively,
  - hoard wealth unlawfully,
  - commit fraud,
  - exploit members.
- Require transparency and accountability for tax-advantaged religious and ideological institutions that exercise substantial power.
- Make clear distinctions between volunteers and forced labor.
- Remove inappropriate legal shields for abuses committed under religious cover.

## 6. Due Process, State Power, and Protection from Abuse
- Ban indefinite detention.
- Ban foreign detention, offshore incarceration, or imprisonment on foreign soil designed to evade rights.
- Ban mass internment or detention based on:
  - race,
  - ethnicity,
  - religion,
  - nationality,
  - political identity,
  - other group status.
- Ban executive kill lists and disposition-matrix-type systems without full constitutional due process, except under tightly defined war-law circumstances if any such exception survives.
- Strictly constrain watchlists, blacklists, no-fly lists, and predictive-risk systems unless accompanied by notice, due process, appeal rights, and review.
- Require strict due process protections for detention, surveillance, punishment, and deprivations of liberty.
- Constitutional baseline for public records, including a records-preservation duty for presidents and senior officials.
- Protect habeas corpus and judicial review.
- Ban torture, cruel treatment, and state-created legal black holes.

## 7. Social and Economic Rights
### A. Healthcare
- Recognize healthcare as a right.
- Protect access to medically necessary care without discrimination based on politics, ideology, identity, or geography.
- Prevent partisan limitations or political sabotage of medical coverage.
- Universal baseline access nationwide.

### B. Housing
- Recognize housing as a right or, at minimum, protect the right to safe shelter and freedom from arbitrary displacement.
- Protect against exploitative housing practices.
- Protect against discrimination in housing and essential services.

### C. Paid Leave and Family Security
- Guarantee paid medical leave.
- Guarantee paid parental leave.
- Guarantee paid family leave.

### D. Economic Security Guarantees
- Guarantee a minimum economic floor sufficient for:
  - basic survival,
  - dignity,
  - participation in society.
- User view was that a guaranteed basic income is absolutely required.
- Require that the income floor be:
  - regularly adjusted for inflation,
  - tied to cost-of-living realities,
  - protected from arbitrary political erosion.
- Recognize the right to earn a livable wage.
- Require minimum wage to:
  - automatically adjust for inflation,
  - reflect real living costs.
- Require government to maintain systems of:
  - social insurance,
  - disability support,
  - unemployment protection,
  - old-age income support,
  - welfare services.
- Remove arbitrary caps that make payroll-based social insurance less progressive, including the Social Security wage cap or equivalent contribution caps.
- Add social insurance and welfare obligations to constitutional law or create constitutional requirements that government provide them.
- Protect welfare programs from politicization and sabotage.
- Require neutral, rights-protective standards rather than ideological tests.

### E. Labor Rights
- Protect the right to organize.
- Protect collective bargaining and labor protections as an area for further development.

### F. Additional Economic and Social Rights Discussed
- Education.
- Basic subsistence.
- Clean air and water.
- Protection from extreme economic coercion.

## 8. Children’s Rights, Parental Rights, and Family Integrity
- Protect children’s rights to:
  - bodily autonomy appropriate to age and capacity,
  - safety from abuse and neglect,
  - medically appropriate care,
  - education,
  - due process,
  - freedom from coercive institutional harm.
- Protect parental rights for all parents, not only ideologically preferred ones.
- Require balance between parental rights and children’s independent rights and best interests.
- Prohibit the state from privileging one political, religious, or ideological parenting framework over others in ways that suppress lawful pluralism.
- Prevent abuse of “parental rights” as a pretext to deny children safety, education, healthcare, dignity, or equal protection.
- Protect family integrity, including chosen family and diverse family structures where consistent with safety and law.
- Additional rights discussed:
  - right to form relationships,
  - right to chosen family,
  - protection of consensual adult intimate relationships.

## 9. Immigration and Basic Human Rights Protections
- Guarantee core due process and dignity protections to all persons within U.S. jurisdiction, not only citizens.
- Protect against arbitrary detention, abuse, disappearance, family separation without due process, and coercive confinement.
- Require humane treatment, access to counsel or legal process, and judicial review in immigration detention and removal systems.
- Prohibit use of immigration status as a pretext to nullify baseline human rights.
- Ban group-based detention or punishment without individualized due process.
- Distinguish universal human rights from political rights tied specifically to citizenship.

## 10. Environmental Rights
- Recognize a right to clean air, clean water, and a reasonably safe environment.
- Require protection from severe environmental harms that foreseeably threaten health, safety, and long-term habitability.
- Protect communities from discriminatory environmental burdens.
- Require transparency around contamination and public-health risk.
- Treat environmental safety as a rights issue, not just a regulatory preference.

## 11. Emerging Technology, AI, and Automated Power
- Ban government use of AI or automated systems to make unreviewable decisions in:
  - policing,
  - sentencing,
  - benefits,
  - healthcare,
  - education,
  - housing,
  - employment,
  - immigration,
  - credit-like public systems.
- Guarantee the right to:
  - explanation,
  - human review,
  - appeal,
  - correction of automated errors.
- Ban discriminatory algorithmic profiling.
- Restrict high-risk biometric surveillance.
- Ban government circumvention of rights through private technology vendors.
- Limit data brokers and government end-runs around constitutional protections through commercial data purchases.
- Discussion also flagged future work around coercive behavioral manipulation, addictive interface design, and children’s digital protections.

## 12. Firearms, Armed Groups, and Democratic Order
- Rework the Second Amendment or related constitutional text to explicitly affirm government authority to regulate firearms and weaponry.
- Explicitly define “well regulated militia.”
- Clarify that private armies, mercenary groups, and unaccountable armed formations are prohibited.
- Permit strong regulation of:
  - acquisition,
  - carrying,
  - training,
  - storage,
  - militia activity.
- Require:
  - background checks,
  - safety training,
  - de-escalation training,
  - narrowly tailored dangerousness-based mental health evaluations.
- Prohibit blanket firearm exclusion based solely on diagnosis.
- Ban private ownership of weapons of war, including automatic weapons and comparable civilian analogues intended to evade regulation.
- Require militias to:
  - maintain membership records,
  - maintain asset records,
  - disclose chain of command,
  - carry insurance,
  - submit to regular audits,
  - follow approved training and safety standards.
- Permit federal and state regulation and oversight of militias.
- Allow and encourage lawful service roles such as disaster relief and search and rescue under clear accountability systems.

## 13. Enforcement, Interpretation, and Remedies
- Rights in this pillar apply nationwide as a federal floor.
- States and localities may expand protections but may not reduce them below that floor.
- Any law burdening a fundamental right must satisfy the highest level of constitutional review.
- Government must prove:
  - compelling interest,
  - necessity,
  - narrow tailoring,
  - least restrictive means.
- Rights-protective provisions must be interpreted using:
  - text,
  - structure,
  - stated purpose,
  - context,
  - anti-abuse intent.
- Plain-language statements of intent are treated as authoritative interpretive guidance.
- Congress must have explicit power to enforce these guarantees through legislation.
- Courts must provide meaningful remedies.
- Private entities exercising:
  - public,
  - quasi-public,
  - monopolistic,
  - coercive power
  in defined contexts may be subject to tailored rights constraints.
- Ban retaliatory state action against individuals for exercising protected rights.

## 14. Rights-Conflict Resolution
- When rights conflict:
  - maximize liberty and dignity,
  - minimize coercion and harm,
  - prioritize protection against irreversible harm,
  - apply heightened scrutiny where structural power imbalances exist.
- This is intended to resolve recurring tensions such as:
  - religious liberty vs anti-discrimination,
  - parental rights vs children’s rights,
  - privacy vs public safety,
  - speech vs coordinated deception,
  - gun rights vs community safety.

## 15. Remedies
- Injunctive relief.
- Damages.
- Expedited judicial review.
- Federal cause of action.
- Attorney’s fees.
- Rights without remedies are considered inadequate.

## Additional Gaps Flagged for Later Inclusion
- Freedom of association and intimate association explicitly stated.
- Right to marry and form a family without arbitrary interference.
- Expungement, sealing, and reintegration rights after punishment.
- Rights protecting disabled people from institutionalization abuse.
- Elder rights and protection from coercive guardianship.
- Workplace surveillance protections.
- Coercive behavioral manipulation and addictive interface design.

## Pressure-Test Lessons That Must Be Incorporated
- Avoid making the unenumerated-rights framework so vague that it becomes shapeless.
- Distinguish strongly between protecting belief and allowing harmful conduct.
- Ensure bodily autonomy language cannot be casually weaponized against all public-health rules without a strict harm framework.
- Positive rights like healthcare and housing require implementation obligations, not just declarations.
- Federal floor plus state expansion is the correct anti-patchwork model.
- Corporate power and outsourced public functions must not become an end run around constitutional rights.
