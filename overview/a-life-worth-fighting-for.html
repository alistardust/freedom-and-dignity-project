Part I

Remember When
Things Felt Real?

Do you remember food that tasted like something?
A house that felt solid under your feet?
A job your father had for thirty years — with a pension?
Music you still know every word to, twenty years later?

There was a time when the ordinary things of life had weight. When you bought a washing machine, you expected it to last until your kids were grown. A couch was something you'd still have in a decade. A town had its own hardware store, its own diner, its own face — a place with a name that meant something to the people who lived there.

You didn't have to be rich to live like that. It was just... how life worked. A reasonable expectation. The baseline.

Food
"My grandmother's tomatoes tasted like summer. I can't find that anymore."
Work
"My dad worked one job for 32 years. They knew his name. He retired with a pension."
Home
"That house is still standing. They built it in 1952. My apartment was built in 2019 and the walls shake."
Community
"We had a Main Street. Three hardware stores, a movie theater, two diners. All gone now."
Things
"My mother's KitchenAid mixer is 40 years old. Mine lasted three."
Culture
"Albums used to be events. You'd listen to the whole thing. You'd remember it."

That's not nostalgia talking. Those things were real. And they didn't disappear on their own.

Part II

Something Went Wrong.
You Can Feel It.

Everything costs more.
Everything feels worse.
Everything feels thinner somehow. And you keep working harder — and keep falling behind.

Food doesn't taste the same. Music feels manufactured — written by committee, optimized for a thirty-second clip, gone before you can remember it. We are more connected than at any point in human history, and somehow more isolated, more alone.

Your house feels flimsier than your grandparents' house. Your furniture won't survive a second move. Your appliances break on schedule. Your clothes fall apart after a season. Your phone is obsolete in two years — on purpose. The things that were supposed to last, don't. And you're paying more for all of it.

There is a constant low-level exhaustion that most people just accept as normal now. You're always one bad month away from something. Job security feels like a myth. Retirement feels unreachable. Homeownership feels like a punchline. And through all of it, you're being told: this is just how it is now. This is just the market. There's nothing to be done.

This is not how it's supposed to feel.

This is not your fault. This is not random. And it is not inevitable.

Something fundamental has changed in how this economy works — and it changed on purpose, by design, in the interest of people who were not thinking about you.

Part III

What Actually
Happened

The degradation of ordinary American life is not an accident of nature or an inevitable consequence of progress. It is the predictable result of specific choices, made by specific people, over several decades. Here is what they chose.

  • 1
    Financialization — Everything Optimized for Profit, Nothing for People
    When Wall Street owns everything, everything gets optimized for quarterly returns. Not for quality. Not for durability. Not for the communities that depend on it. Manufacturers stopped competing on how long things last and started competing on how cheap they could make them. The difference went to shareholders.
  • 2
    Planned Obsolescence — Things Designed to Break
    Your appliances, electronics, furniture — they are increasingly designed with a lifespan in mind. Not a long one. Right-to-repair laws were gutted by industry lobbying. Warranties narrowed. Replacement parts made unavailable. The things you buy are engineered to become landfill so you'll buy them again.
  • 3
    Agricultural Consolidation — Four Companies, Your Food Supply
    Four corporations control most of what reaches your table. They optimized for yield, shelf life, and appearance — not flavor, not nutrition, not the farmers who grow it. The variety is gone. The local economy that supported it is gone. What's left is uniform, industrial, and less nutritious than what your grandparents ate.
  • 4
    Wage Theft — The Gains of Fifty Years Went Upward
    Productivity — the total output of American workers — grew roughly 60% since 1979. Typical worker pay grew about 18%. The rest went to shareholders and executives. This was not a market outcome. It was a policy outcome: weakened unions, suppressed minimum wage, legal structures that favor capital over labor.
  • 5
    The Ownership Economy Was Replaced by the Rental Economy
    Subscriptions replaced ownership. Platforms replaced property. Leases replaced mortgages. The economy was deliberately redesigned — through housing policy, through intellectual property law, through the structure of the tech industry — so that fewer people own things outright, and the people who own everything collect rent forever. Some people in power call this progress. They call it "you'll own nothing and be happy." This project calls it theft.
  • 6
    Political Capture — The System Works for Its Donors
    The Supreme Court ruled that unlimited corporate money in elections is free speech. Members of Congress trade stocks in industries they regulate. Former regulators become industry lobbyists. A class of people commits crimes every day and faces no consequences. The people who created this system funded the campaigns of the people who were supposed to stop it.
  • 7
    The Attention Economy — Algorithmic Culture
    Music, art, news, and connection were routed through platforms that optimize for engagement — which turns out to mean anxiety, outrage, and compulsive checking. The artists make less. The platforms take more. The content gets cheaper and more inflammatory. The loneliness epidemic is not coincidental; it is the byproduct of an industry that profits from it.

Part IV

This Is a Rights Project.
It Has a History.

The First Bill of Rights told government what it could not do to you. Essential. Still contested. Still, in many places, unfinished.

In 1944, with the Depression still in living memory and a world war still raging, Franklin Roosevelt stood before Congress and said something that changed the frame:

Franklin D. Roosevelt — January 11, 1944
"True individual freedom cannot exist without economic security and independence. Necessitous men are not free men."

He proposed eight new rights. Congress never passed them. He died sixteen months later. They remain unfinished.

  • The right to a useful and remunerative job
  • The right to earn enough for adequate food, clothing, and recreation
  • The right of every farmer to raise and sell products at a decent living
  • Freedom from unfair competition and monopoly
  • The right to a decent home
  • The right to adequate medical care
  • The right to protection from economic fear — old age, sickness, unemployment
  • The right to a good education

Eighty years later. Unfinished. This project finishes them — and goes further.

"You can't be free if you're sick, homeless, or in debt."
American Renewal Project — in the tradition of Roosevelt, 1944

This project is the Third Bill of Rights. Completing what FDR started, and extending it into a century he could not have imagined: mass digital surveillance, algorithmic control, climate collapse, and private power concentrated at a scale that makes the monopolies of his era look modest.

Freedom has two dimensions. The first is freedom from — from coercion, from arbitrary power. We defend those completely. The second is freedom to — the actual, practical capacity to make real choices about your own life. That freedom depends on conditions. You cannot exercise your rights if you are spending every hour of your life just surviving.

Part V

Imagine What
Better Looks Like

None of what follows is utopian.
Most of it is simply what other wealthy countries already have.
All of it was once within reach — and can be again.
Healthcare
Imagine going to the doctor without first checking your bank account.
No negotiation. No coverage denial. No bankruptcy because someone in your family got sick. Healthcare as a right — the way FDR intended in 1944.
Work & Wages
Imagine a job that pays enough to live on — without a second job, without cutting corners, without choosing between groceries and rent.
Wages indexed to productivity and inflation. Enforced. The work your labor creates pays you what it's worth.
Things That Last
Imagine buying a couch you'll still have in twenty years. A washer that outlasts your mortgage. A phone you can actually fix.
Right-to-repair laws. Durability standards. The stuff you own is built to last — because the incentive to make disposable junk has been removed.
Food
Imagine food that tastes the way your grandmother's did — grown by farmers who can actually make a living growing it.
Real antitrust enforcement in agriculture. Support for independent farmers. A food system optimized for people, not for quarterly returns.
A Home
Imagine homeownership as an achievable goal — not a punchline. A place that's yours, that can't be taken away on a landlord's whim.
Housing policy that builds supply, breaks speculation, and treats homes as places to live rather than financial instruments to extract value from.
Your Community
Imagine a town with a Main Street. A local hardware store. A diner that's been there for forty years. A sense that this place is yours.
Antitrust enforcement that prevents chain consolidation from obliterating local economies. Investment in the infrastructure of community life.
Culture
Imagine music made by artists who could afford to take two years to get it right. Art that isn't focus-grouped. News that isn't optimized for outrage.
Break the platform monopolies. Fund public media. Protect artists' rights. Return culture to the people who make it.
Privacy & Dignity
Imagine your data belonging to you. Your phone not tracking you. Your medical decisions being between you and your doctor — not your insurer.
Surveillance capitalism ended. Bodily autonomy protected. Your digital life subject to the same rights as your physical one.
The Planet
Imagine your kids inheriting a livable world. Clean water from the tap. Air that doesn't make them sick. A summer that isn't a disaster.
Climate rights as genuine rights. Environmental protection treated as non-negotiable. The right to a livable planet as the floor of every other freedom.

This is not the future. This is what other countries have already built. This is what we had — in pieces — and can have again. And more.

Part VI

What We're
Building

Five foundations. Not five promises. Five rights — the categories of a society that is actually free. Organized from values to enforceable rules, from principle to policy, from why to how.

Foundation I
Accountable Power

No president, judge, corporation, or billionaire is above the law. Power must be constrained, auditable, and answerable. This is where it starts. When it breaks here, everything else breaks with it.

Foundation II
Clean Democracy

Government must answer to people, not money. Fair elections. Dark money ended. Corporate concentration broken before it can capture the institutions meant to regulate it.

Foundation III
Equal Justice

The same law applied the same way to everyone. Regardless of wealth, race, immigration status, or connections. No class of people for whom consequences don't apply.

Foundation IV
Real Freedom

Rights must be explicit, enforceable, and protected. Privacy — including your digital life. Bodily autonomy. Freedom from weapons designed for mass casualties. Rights that exist in practice, not just on paper.

Foundation V
Freedom to Thrive

Healthcare, a living wage, clean air and water, a livable climate — these are not luxuries. They are the material conditions without which every other freedom becomes theoretical. They are the floor.

Part VII

What It Takes

These are not aspirations. They are the minimum structural changes required to make the system work for people rather than against them. They follow directly from the rights above.

  • 01Break up concentrated corporate power — in agriculture, media, pharmaceuticals, technology, housing, and finance
  • 02End the revolving door: no lobbyists into regulation, no regulators into lobbying
  • 03Publicly fund elections — make dark money structurally impossible, not just theoretically prohibited
  • 04Guarantee healthcare access — no person should be bankrupted for getting sick
  • 05Index wages to productivity and inflation and enforce them — the gains of work belong to the workers who created them
  • 06Enact right-to-repair — if you own something, you have the right to fix it
  • 07Set durability and repairability standards — build things to last
  • 08Restore real antitrust enforcement — four companies cannot control the food supply
  • 09Tax wealth fairly — prevent compounding fortunes from becoming a permanent aristocracy
  • 10Protect the digital commons — your data is yours; surveillance capitalism must end
  • 11Enforce accountability at every level — the law applies to everyone or it applies to no one
  • 12Protect a free press and an informed public — democracy requires that people can know what is true
